I run a public, dual-audience, catalyst-neutral credit-markets desk. Its analyst spine is ~75% complete; three sections need a sourced empirical backbone to match the rest of the desk's density. My standard is strict: **every figure must be sourced or clearly labeled qualitative — never invented; methodology and as-of dates always stated; an honest "no public figure exists" is more useful than a fabricated one.** For each of the three gaps below, give me sourced, citable content I can turn into analyst tables, decoder cards, and a scenario matrix, plus the precise definitions and the honest caveats.

Frame everything for **US credit** (IG + HY corporate, leveraged loans, EM sovereign where noted), and anchor the empirical claims to these stress episodes where relevant: **2008 GFC, 2011 euro-sovereign, 2015–16 energy/commodity distress, 2020 COVID dash-for-cash, 2022 rate-shock/UK-LDI.**

**GAP 1 — Credit factors (carry, quality, spread-duration/DTS, momentum/crowding).**
- Define each credit factor precisely as practitioners use it: **carry** (OAS minus expected loss; or breakeven spread), **quality** (up-in-quality: IG vs HY, BB vs CCC), **spread duration / DTS** (duration-times-spread as the risk unit), **momentum/value** in credit, and any **crowding** gauge.
- For each episode above: which factor led, which protected capital, which broke — with **sourced magnitudes** where they exist (e.g., excess returns or drawdowns of an up-in-quality vs carry posture; DTS behavior). Cite the studies (academic or sell-side/asset-manager research that's publicly readable).
- Is there a canonical "credit factor rotation by regime" framing (who wins in risk-on vs risk-off vs rate-shock)? Provide it as a table if so.
- Current readings only if a free public source carries them; otherwise say so.

**GAP 2 — Positioning & technicals (the supply/demand and forced-selling read).**
- **Dealer inventory:** the NY Fed primary-dealer net positions in corporate bonds (series, URL, cadence) and how to read inventory build/drawdown as a technical. Post-2008 balance-sheet constraint (SLR) context.
- **TRACE** volume/breadth: what FINRA TRACE publicly discloses (and doesn't) for reading market depth and stress.
- **Fund flows:** ICI / EPFR / Lipper weekly IG & HY fund flows — what's free, cadence, how to read flow-driven spread moves.
- **Credit-index options / CDX swaptions:** how index-options positioning and skew signal hedging pressure; any public source.
- **Primary issuance** as a supply technical (we already source SIFMA): how heavy/light supply moves secondary spreads.
- The key interpretive question: **how do you distinguish spread-widening driven by dealer/forced-selling (technical) vs fundamental repricing?** Give the tells, with episode examples.

**GAP 3 — Cross-asset & regime correlations (when relationships hold and when they break).**
- The key credit cross-asset relationships and their **regime-dependence**: HY OAS vs equity volatility/drawdown; OAS vs Treasury level and curve shape; OAS vs the broad dollar; stock–bond correlation regimes (and the post-2022 sign flip). For each: typical correlation, what regime inverts it, **sourced examples**.
- A **scenario × credit-cohort matrix**: rows = {risk-off/flight-to-quality, rate shock, growth re-acceleration, commodity/energy shock, dollar-funding squeeze}; columns = {IG, HY, leveraged loans, EM sovereign}; cells = directional outcome + the mechanism + a historical precedent. Sourced where possible.
- Explicit guidance on **when each correlation should NOT be trusted** (the regime breaks), since the desk's brand is honest about model fragility.

For every claim: state the **source with a working link**, the **as-of date**, and whether it's a hard figure or a qualitative/directional read. Where no free public figure exists, say "no public figure — qualitative only" rather than estimating. Prefer primary sources (Fed/FRED, FINRA, ICI, NY Fed, rating agencies, academic papers) and publicly-readable sell-side/asset-manager research. A confirmed "this isn't publicly available" is a valuable answer.
