## TASK 1 — TREASURY BUYBACKS (PRIMARY FOCUS)

### 1.0 Why this tile matters and what it is *not*

The buyback program is the piece of post-2020 Treasury plumbing that R-09 left implicit. It is the structural answer to the "dash-for-cash" episode of March 2020, when the off-the-run Treasury market seized and dealers could not warehouse the paper investors wanted to sell. Treasury's response was a **regular, pre-announced reverse-auction facility** to repurchase older, less-liquid securities — re-launched in May 2024 after a two-decade hiatus (the prior program ran 2000–2002). The single most important framing for the verdict board: **this is debt management, not monetary policy.** It is deficit-neutral and debt-stock-neutral; every dollar spent buying back a bond must be re-borrowed elsewhere on the curve. It is emphatically *not* QE — Treasury creates no reserves and prints no money — and Treasury has stated plainly it does **not currently intend to use buybacks to mitigate acute market stress** ([TreasuryDirect Buyback FAQs](https://www.treasurydirect.gov/help-center/faqs/buyback-faqs/)). **[DIR]**

### 1.1 (a) The two buckets — sizes, cadence, caps, maturity sectors

Treasury runs **two distinct buyback programs** with different objectives, cadences, and maturity targets ([TreasuryDirect Buyback FAQs](https://www.treasurydirect.gov/help-center/faqs/buyback-faqs/); [Office of Debt Management Q2 2024 Supplement](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TreasurySupplementalQ22024.pdf)):

| Feature | **Liquidity Support** | **Cash Management** |
|---|---|---|
| Objective | Establish a regular, predictable outlet for selling **off-the-run** securities; bolster secondary-market liquidity | Smooth Treasury's cash balance & bill issuance around tax dates; reduce bill-supply disruptions; potentially lower borrowing cost over time |
| Maturity coverage | Across the **nominal and TIPS curves** (1Mo–2Y, 2–3Y, 3–5Y, 5–7Y, 7–10Y, 10–20Y, 20–30Y nominal; short & long TIPS) | **1-month to 2-year only** |
| Cadence | **Once or twice per week** (typically afternoons); each bucket covered ≥1×/quarter | **Seasonal** — clustered around major tax dates (mid-Apr, mid-Jun, mid-Sep, mid-Dec) |
| Per-operation cap (2026) | **$4bn** for core nominal buckets (1Mo–2Y, 2–3Y, 3–5Y, 5–7Y, 7–10Y); **$2bn** for 10–20Y and 20–30Y; **$750mn** short TIPS; **$500mn** long TIPS | **$12.5bn–$15bn** per operation (varies by quarter with seasonal flows) |
| Carry-forward | **No** — unused capacity is *not* carried to the next operation | **Yes** — capacity may be carried forward across operations |

**[HARD]** Per-operation caps for the May 2026 refunding quarter are taken directly from the [Tentative Schedule of Treasury Buyback Operations, May 2026 QRA](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/Tentative-Buyback-Schedule.pdf): e.g., 5/19/26 nominal 3–5Y at $0–$4bn; 6/3/26 nominal 20–30Y at $0–$2bn; 5/13/26 TIPS 1Y–10Y at $0–$750mn; and cash-management nominal 1Mo–2Y operations on 6/5/26 and 6/12/26 each at $0–$12.5bn.

**Quarterly caps (Feb–Apr 2026 refunding quarter)** — the most recent fully-stated headline figures: **up to $38bn** across buckets for liquidity support and **up to $75bn** in the 1Mo–2Y bucket for cash management ([Feb 2026 Quarterly Refunding Statement, sb0384](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0384)). **[HARD]** The $75bn cash-management cap was **the largest par amount in a single refunding quarter since the program's inception** ([Treasury Presentation to TBAC, Q2 2026](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TreasuryPresentationToTBACQ22026.pdf)). The task brief's "~$30bn/quarter, ~$4bn/operation" reflects the program's *original* May 2024 calibration; the **current liquidity-support quarterly run-rate has scaled to ~$38bn** and cash-management has grown materially — a worth-flagging update to the desk's prior assumption. **[HARD]**

**Published schedule (free):** the tentative buyback schedule is released at each Quarterly Refunding (first Wednesday of Feb/May/Aug/Nov) in both **PDF and XML** at the [Most Recent Quarterly Refunding Documents page](https://home.treasury.gov/policy-issues/financing-the-government/quarterly-refunding/most-recent-quarterly-refunding-documents) and mirrored at the [TreasuryDirect Buyback Announcements & Results page](https://www.treasurydirect.gov/auctions/announcements-data-results/buy-backs/). **[HARD]** The next QRA after the May 6, 2026 release is **August 5, 2026** (which will publish the Aug–Oct / Q3-2026-refunding schedule). **[HARD]**

**Three-stage announcement cadence** ([TreasuryDirect Buyback FAQs](https://www.treasurydirect.gov/help-center/faqs/buyback-faqs/)): (1) **Quarterly Tentative** at the QRA; (2) **Operational Preliminary** ≥1 business day prior (preliminary CUSIP list + max size, 11:00am ET); (3) **Operational Final** at 11:00am ET on operation day (final CUSIP list, supersedes preliminary). Operations run ~1:40–2:00pm ET. Results — total par offered, total par accepted, par accepted and weighted-average accepted price per CUSIP — are posted promptly after close. **[HARD]**

**Eligibility / participation guardrails** ([TreasuryDirect Buyback FAQs](https://www.treasurydirect.gov/help-center/faqs/buyback-faqs/)): conducted with **primary dealers** plus a limited set of Treasury-approved direct counterparties; eligibility for direct access requires **>$35bn in 10-year-equivalent auction awards over a six-month observation window**, reviewed semiannually. Hard purchase limits: **free float must stay above $10bn par for nominal coupons and $5bn par for TIPS**; **SOMA ownership of any security may not exceed 70%** post-settlement; **minimum $10mn par** per single security. **[HARD]** These guardrails are the structural reason buybacks cannot become a back-door price-support facility — they cap how much of any line Treasury can pull.

### 1.2 (b) What buybacks DO and DON'T do

**They DO:**
- **Drain off-the-run inventory** — retire "dusty" older securities and let Treasury re-issue liquid on-the-run benchmarks, improving secondary-market depth (the structural lesson of March 2020). **[DIR]**
- **Smooth the cash balance / TGA** — cash-management buybacks pull forward redemptions in high-receipt windows, tempering the bill-auction-size cuts that would otherwise be needed around tax dates ([Apr 2025 QRA statement sb0120](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0120)). **[HARD]**
- **Modestly manage the maturity profile** — by repurchasing across the curve and re-issuing where it chooses, Treasury exerts marginal control over WAM, though it explicitly does **not** attempt to align new issuance with the specific tenor bought back ([ODM Q2 2024 Supplement](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TreasurySupplementalQ22024.pdf)). **[HARD]**

**They DON'T:**
- **Add net liquidity / reserves** — unlike QE, no money is created; the purchases are financed by issuing other debt. **Deficit-neutral and debt-stock-neutral.** ([BloFin analysis of the June 2025 $10bn operation](https://blofin.com/academy/flow-insights/a-market-intervention-decoding-the-treasury-s-10b-buyback)). **[DIR]**
- **Constitute a market rescue** — the widely-cited June 3, 2025 "$10bn buyback" was a *pre-announced, routine cash-management operation* in sub-2Y notes, not a discretionary Bessent intervention; the maximum had been set in the May QRA weeks earlier. The "market intervention" reading was incorrect. ([BloFin](https://blofin.com/academy/flow-insights/a-market-intervention-decoding-the-treasury-s-10b-buyback)). **[DIR]** This is the canonical mis-read to inoculate the audience against.
- **Replace QE or offset QT** — note the "fiscal dominance" critique (e.g., [PlanQuant](https://planquant.com/treasury-buyback-fiscal-dominance/)) argues buybacks plus bill-skewed issuance *re-engineer* liquidity even as the Fed runs QT; present this as a **minority interpretive view**, not consensus, and weigh it against Treasury's stated deficit-neutral mechanics. **[DIR]**

### 1.3 (c) How to read buybacks as a signal — stress vs. routine

This is the analytically valuable part, and the operation-level data since May 2024 gives a clean baseline. Two ratios matter, both published by Treasury/ODM:

- **Offer-to-Max** = total par *offered* by dealers ÷ Treasury's max purchase size. High = dealers eager to sell that sector.
- **Buyback Ratio (≈ fill ratio)** = par *accepted* ÷ max. =1.0 means Treasury filled the whole bucket; <1.0 means demand to sell was weak *or* prices offered were unattractive.

**The single most important interpretive takeaway:** the **structural demand pattern is U-shaped, not flat.** Dealers are perennially eager to offload **the very front end (1Mo–2Y) and the long end (10–20Y, 20–30Y)** — those buckets are *chronically oversubscribed* (offer-to-max 8–18×) and *always fill to 100%*. The **belly, especially 7–10Y, is structurally undersold** — only $3.2bn purchased against a $30bn cumulative max, fill ratio averaging ~0.1. ([Treasury Presentation to TBAC, Q2 2026](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TreasuryPresentationToTBACQ22026.pdf)). **[HARD]** This is *routine and persistent*, driven by the fact that off-the-run long bonds and old front-end notes are the "dustiest" paper dealers most want off balance sheet, while 7–10Y off-the-runs remain relatively liquid and valued. **A low fill in 7–10Y is therefore NOT a stress signal — it is the program's normal resting state.**

**So how would stress actually show up?** Read the *signal against each sector's own baseline*, not against a uniform threshold:
- **Routine:** front-end and long-end fill at 100% with offer-to-max in the high single digits to ~18× (current 20–30Y offer-to-max has been **~18× since March 2026** — robust, not stressed) ([Treasury Presentation to TBAC, Q2 2026](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TreasuryPresentationToTBACQ22026.pdf)). **[HARD]**
- **Potential stress flag #1 — surging offer volume + price concession:** a *sudden jump* in offer-to-max in a normally-quiet sector (e.g., 5–7Y or 7–10Y spiking well above its 1–2× norm) paired with dealers offering at *cheap* weighted-average accepted prices would suggest dealers are dumping inventory they cannot finance — the off-the-run-illiquidity tell. Watch the per-CUSIP weighted-average accepted price vs. fair value (the "concession").
- **Potential stress flag #2 — Treasury filling to max in a sector it usually under-fills:** if 7–10Y suddenly cleared at 1.0 fill, that is a regime change worth investigating.
- **Important caveat:** Treasury has *explicitly disclaimed* using buybacks for acute stress, so even a stress-flagged operation is a *liquidity thermometer*, not a backstop. For genuine acute stress the relevant facility is the Fed's SRF (see Open Floor §4). **[DIR]**

**Observed near-misses / soft prints (current quarter, 2/19/26–4/28/26):** 3–5Y on 3/4/26 filled below the $4bn max; 5–7Y filled **less than half** the $4bn max; 7–10Y "significantly less than max"; one cash-management operation on 3/10/26 filled $14.7bn vs. $15bn max. None of these are stress — all sit inside each sector's historical band; the TBAC deck characterizes offer volume as "robust" throughout. ([Treasury Presentation to TBAC, Q2 2026](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TreasuryPresentationToTBACQ22026.pdf)). **[HARD]** No operation since inception has printed an unambiguous stress signature in the public data reviewed.

### 1.4 (d) Interaction with bill issuance and the QRA

Buybacks sit *inside* the quarterly refunding machinery and are financed like any other borrowing need ([ODM Q2 2024 Supplement](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TreasurySupplementalQ22024.pdf)). The mechanical linkages:
- **Cash-management buybacks are a bill-supply shock absorber.** Around tax dates, receipts surge and Treasury would otherwise have to slash bill auction sizes abruptly; instead it deploys cash to buy back sub-2Y notes, "tempering reductions to bill auction sizes that would otherwise occur" ([Apr 2025 QRA statement sb0120](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0120)). **[HARD]** This keeps the bill market's plumbing smooth for money funds.
- **In the QRA financing arithmetic, buybacks are an explicit line.** The Q2 2026 TBAC framing shows, for the Apr–Jun 2026 (FY26 Q3) quarter: net marketable borrowing **$671bn**, net coupon issuance **$367bn**, assumed buybacks **$44bn**, implied change in bills **$348bn** ([Treasury Presentation to TBAC, Q2 2026](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TreasuryPresentationToTBACQ22026.pdf)). **[HARD]** Buybacks are funded *predominantly by issuing more bills* in practice (since liquidity-support buys retire coupons and the offset is bill-skewed), which is why the "activist Treasury / bill-skew" critique in R-09 connects directly here: buybacks of long paper financed by bills shorten effective WAM at the margin and raise rollover risk — the [PlanQuant](https://planquant.com/treasury-buyback-fiscal-dominance/) thesis, presented as a contested view. **[DIR]**
- **TBAC sizing convention:** for projection purposes, liquidity-support buybacks are assumed equal to the prior quarter's actuals; cash-management buybacks equal the most recent *comparable* (seasonal) quarter ([Treasury Presentation to TBAC, Q1 2026](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/CombinedChargesforArchivesQ12026.pdf)). **[HARD]**

**Program evolution worth tracking (forward signals):** (i) Treasury issued a **Notice of Proposed Rulemaking on Jan 14, 2026** to widen *direct* buyback access to additional auction-active counterparties beyond primary dealers; comment period closed Feb 13, 2026; **final rule expected H1 2026**. (ii) Treasury plans to **migrate operations to the NY Fed's new FedTrade Plus platform**, preceded by a **small-value test buyback** (explicitly flagged as *not* a policy signal). ([Feb 2026 QRA statement sb0384](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0384)). **[HARD]** (iii) At the **Aug 2025 refunding**, Treasury **doubled the frequency** of 10–20Y and 20–30Y operations, re-bucketed TIPS to 1Y–10Y / 10Y–30Y, and raised short-TIPS per-op max from $500mn to $750mn — evidence the program is being actively scaled toward the long end where demand is strongest. ([Treasury Presentation to TBAC, Q2 2026](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TreasuryPresentationToTBACQ22026.pdf)). **[HARD]**

## TASK 2 — US FISCAL CALENDAR: DEBT CEILING / X-DATE / SHUTDOWNS / TGA

### 2.1 (a) Debt-ceiling mechanics + 2025 episode + VERIFIED 2026 status

**Mechanics.** When the statutory limit binds, Treasury deploys **extraordinary measures** — accounting maneuvers (e.g., suspending reinvestment of the G-Fund and CSRDF federal-retiree funds) that *suppress intragovernmental debt to create headroom under the public-debt ceiling*. These buy weeks-to-months. The **X-date** is when even extraordinary measures plus cash run out. ([Brookings — The Debt Limit explained](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/the-hutchins-center-explains-the-debt-limit/)). **[HARD]**

**Market footprint.** As the X-date approaches, a **"kink" appears in the bill curve** — bills maturing *just after* the suspected X-date trade cheap (higher yield) on default-timing risk, while bills maturing before trade rich. Treasury is forced to **shrink bill supply** (it can't add debt), draining the safe-asset supply MMFs crave and pushing cash into the RRP. Post-resolution, Treasury **floods the bill market to rebuild the TGA**, reversing the flow. **[DIR]**

**2025 episode (verified).** The debt limit was **reached at $36.1tn on January 1, 2025** (post the prior suspension lapse); Treasury began extraordinary measures in **January 2025** (Yellen flagged Jan 21 start; under Bessent the measures were managed aggressively). Secretary Bessent told Congress on **May 9, 2025** that bills couldn't be paid in full beyond **August** absent action; CBO had pushed the X-date to **mid-August–end-September 2025**. ([Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/the-hutchins-center-explains-the-debt-limit/); [Washington Examiner, Jun 13 2025](https://www.washingtonexaminer.com/news/business/3438127/scott-bessent-debt-ceiling-race-treasury-department-congress-one-big-beautiful-bill-trump/)). **[HARD]**

**CURRENT 2026 STATUS — VERIFIED, AND IT MATTERS:** The debt-ceiling threat is **NOT live in 2026.** Congress, in the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act signed July 2025, raised the debt ceiling by $5 trillion — to $41.1 trillion** — which "probably will delay another debt-ceiling showdown for a year or two." ([Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/the-hutchins-center-explains-the-debt-limit/)). **[HARD]** **The desk should explicitly note: as of mid-June 2026 there is no active extraordinary-measures clock, no live X-date, and no bill-curve X-date kink.** The next flashpoint is plausibly **2027.** This is the single most important verification in Task 2 — any stale "X-date approaching" framing inherited from 2025 must be corrected.

### 2.2 (b) The 2026 shutdowns — what they do and don't do to Treasuries

Two distinct 2026 shutdowns occurred ([Wikipedia — 2026 US federal government shutdowns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_United_States_federal_government_shutdowns); [Guardian, Jan 31 2026](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jan/31/us-government-shutdown-dhs-funding)): **[HARD]**
- **First:** **January 31 – February 3, 2026 (4 days)** — short, broad lapse.
- **Second:** **February 14 – April 30, 2026 (76 days)** — long, **DHS-focused**, over restrictions on federal immigration agents. ICE/CBP were largely insulated by ~$140bn of OBBBA reconciliation funding; back pay for the Feb 14–Apr period landed mid-April ([FedTools shutdown guide](https://www.fedtools.com/blog/government-shutdown-guide-2026)). **[HARD]**

**What a shutdown does NOT do to Treasuries:** **debt service is exempt — coupons and principal keep paying.** A shutdown is an *appropriations* lapse, legally and operationally distinct from a *debt-ceiling* breach; there is no missed-payment / technical-default risk to USTs from a shutdown. **[DIR]**

**What it DOES do:** (i) **Economic-data blackout** — BLS/BEA/Census releases (payrolls, CPI, GDP) are suspended, leaving the Fed and the market flying partially blind on data-dependent decisions — acutely awkward against a hawkish Fed and an oil-shock inflation impulse; (ii) **modest growth drag** (furloughs, deferred spending), generally recovered post-reopening; (iii) **second-order rates effect** — data gaps raise uncertainty and can compress or distort the front end via shifting Fed-path expectations rather than via any direct Treasury-payment risk. Historically the rates-market reaction to shutdowns has been muted-to-mildly-risk-off, dominated by the data vacuum rather than credit concern. **[DIR]**

### 2.3 (c) TGA mechanics + link to SOFR/RRP/QT + free series

The TGA is Treasury's checking account at the Fed and a **first-order driver of bank reserves**:
- **TGA drawdown → reserves rise** (Treasury spends, money moves from its Fed account into the banking system) → eases funding pressure, supportive for SOFR.
- **TGA rebuild → reserves fall** (Treasury issues debt / collects taxes, draining the system) → tightens funding, pressures SOFR higher and can pull cash out of RRP. **[DIR]**

This is the channel that links fiscal flows to R-09's SOFR/QT/RRP tile: with QT having shrunk the reserve buffer, a large TGA rebuild is now a more potent reserve drain than it was in the QE-flush era. **[DIR]**

**Verified current levels (mid-June 2026):** TGA stood at roughly **$801bn (Jun 10), $846bn (Jun 3), $843bn (May 27)** per the Fed H.4.1 weekly series ([FRED WTREGEN](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WTREGEN); the Wednesday-level twin [D2WLTGAL](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/D2WLTGAL) shows $801bn Jun 10). **[HARD]** That is up sharply from **~$379bn a year earlier (June 2025)** — i.e., the post-OBBBA TGA rebuild has been executed and the account is now near its target zone. Treasury's own assumptions: **~$850bn end-March, a possible April peak ~$1,025bn (±$50bn)**, and **$900bn assumed for end-June 2026** ([Feb 2026 QRA sb0384](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0384); [Treasury Presentation to TBAC, Q2 2026](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TreasuryPresentationToTBACQ22026.pdf)). **[HARD]** SOFR printed **3.60% on June 11, 2026** ([FRED SOFR](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/SOFR)). **[HARD]**

**Free series for the verdict board:** Fiscal Data **Daily Treasury Statement (DTS)** for the daily operating cash balance; **FRED WTREGEN** (weekly, H.4.1) and **D2WLTGAL** (Wednesday level) for TGA; **FRED SOFR / SOFRINDEX** (daily, updated ~7am CT next day) for the funding rate; NY Fed reference-rates page for repo/RRP. All free. **[HARD]**

### 2.4 (d) Lead indicators + upcoming fiscal flashpoints

- **Lead indicators to watch:** daily TGA path vs. Treasury's quarter-end assumption (DTS); bill-supply trajectory in the QRA (the Feb 2026 statement guided to a **cumulative $250–300bn net decline in bill supply by early May** around the April tax date — a reserve-add tailwind) ([Feb 2026 QRA sb0384](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0384)) **[HARD]**; SOFR–IORB and SOFR–RRP spreads as the funding thermometer; RRP balance as the marginal liquidity buffer.

## TASK 4 — OPEN FLOOR (my agenda: three highest-value follow-ups)

I'd prioritize these three, ranked by marginal value to a risk-free-curve desk given what R-09 + this supplement already cover:

**1 · SRF / Standing Repo Facility — the true acute-stress backstop (HIGHEST PRIORITY).** This is the natural complement to the buyback tile and closes a conceptual gap: buybacks explicitly are *not* a stress tool, so the desk needs to document *what is*. The Fed's domestic **SRF** (made permanent July 2021) and the **FIMA repo facility** (foreign official accounts) are the operational ceilings on repo rates — counterparties can borrow against Treasuries/agencies at a set rate, capping funding spikes like the Sept 2019 and March 2020 episodes. With QT having drained reserves toward the "ample" boundary, **SRF take-up is now the single best real-time gauge of genuine funding stress** — far more diagnostic than buyback fill ratios. Build-ready: NY Fed publishes SRF operation results free; pair SRF usage with SOFR–IORB spread on the verdict board. **[DIR]** This directly extends the SOFR/QT/RRP tile and gives the stress narrative an honest backstop.

**2 · WAM management & the bill-skew / "activist Treasury" debate, quantified via buyback-issuance interaction.** R-09 flagged the activist-Treasury debate; this supplement adds the missing mechanical link: buybacks of long paper financed by bills are a *de facto* WAM-shortening lever even when headline coupon sizes are held flat (Treasury has guided to **maintaining nominal coupon/FRN sizes for several quarters** while flexing bills — [Feb 2026 QRA sb0384](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0384)). **[HARD]** A worthwhile follow-up: track **weighted-average maturity of marketable debt** (free, Fiscal Data / Monthly Statement of the Public Debt) against bill-share-of-outstanding, and frame the rollover-risk vs. cost-saving trade-off as a contested thesis ([PlanQuant fiscal-dominance view](https://planquant.com/treasury-buyback-fiscal-dominance/)). This is where buybacks, QRA, and term premium all intersect.

**3 · MMF reform, RRP drainage, and the bill-demand structural shift.** The RRP facility has drained dramatically as bill supply and money-fund AUM grew; Treasury itself is "monitoring SOMA purchases of Treasury bills and growing demand for Treasury bills from the private sector" ([Feb 2026 QRA sb0384](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0384)). **[HARD]** The follow-up: document how SEC 2023 MMF reforms (swing pricing, liquidity fees) reshaped where cash sits, how that interacts with cash-management buybacks (which deliberately smooth bill supply for these same funds), and whether a near-empty RRP removes the shock-absorber that previously cushioned TGA rebuilds — a direct reserve-scarcity risk worth a dedicated tile. **[DIR]**

*Runners-up not developed here but worth a line each:* **STRIPS/zeros** (the stripped-amount enters the buyback free-float calc, so it's already adjacent); **FRNs** (the 2Y FRN is the only floating marketable UST and a clean front-end-demand read); **SOMA reinvestment mechanics** (the 70% SOMA cap directly constrains buyback eligibility, so SOMA's per-CUSIP holdings feed the buyback tile); **primary-dealer balance-sheet limits / SLR** (the deep reason off-the-run liquidity decays and buybacks exist at all).
