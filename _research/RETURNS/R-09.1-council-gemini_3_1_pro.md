# Foreign Dollar Demand: Carry Mechanics and the Petrodollar Fracture
**Distinctive Angle:** A rigorous quantification of historical carry-trade unwinds and a dynamic scenario matrix mapping the collapse of the petrodollar recycling loop under the Strait of Hormuz blockade.

## 1. Sovereign Dollar-Response Taxonomy

When emerging markets and major exporters face severe currency pressure or external shocks, they deploy a recognizable taxonomy of defensive mechanisms. These levers fundamentally reshape the global supply and demand for US Treasuries (USTs) and alter local rate spreads. Understanding this taxonomy is critical for mapping the flow of global capital in a high-rate, high-stress environment like mid-2026. The eight primary mechanisms can be broadly categorized into direct market interventions, balance sheet adjustments, and structural policy shifts. [DIR: High Confidence]

The first and most direct mechanism is **(a) FX intervention via UST sales**. Central banks directly sell their dollar reserves, which are held largely in liquid US Treasuries, to buy their local currency and arrest its depreciation. This is used widely by EM importers such as India, Indonesia, and historically China. The effect on UST demand is strongly negative; it increases Treasury supply in the secondary market and directly pressures US yields higher. Conversely, its effect on the sovereign's own rate spread is to narrow it, as the local currency is artificially stabilized or its depreciation slowed. [DIR: High Confidence]

Closely related is **(b) reserve accumulation/drawdown**, which reflects the broader macroeconomic cycle. Exporters, traditionally the Gulf states, Norway, and China, accumulate reserves during boom times, effectively absorbing USTs. Importers draw down reserves during shocks, such as the 2026 Hormuz oil spike. Drawdowns are mechanically negative for UST demand, while accumulation is positive. Drawdowns generally correspond with widening sovereign spreads as country risk premiums rise due to depleting external buffers. To stem these outflows without draining reserves, nations often employ **(c) policy-rate defense**. By aggressively raising domestic interest rates—a classic textbook response for fragile EMs—they aim to attract foreign capital and slow capital flight. The effect on UST demand is indirectly negative, as higher EM rates can pull marginal capital away from US assets. The effect on the sovereign's own rate spread is a dramatic widening relative to the US, reflecting the acute stress and necessary risk premium to hold local assets. [DIR: High Confidence]

Another alternative to outright reserve depletion is the use of **(d) diaspora schemes**. Sovereigns issue high-yielding foreign-currency deposits or bonds targeted specifically at non-resident citizens to attract hard currency. This is used heavily by India, Pakistan, and Israel. The effect on UST demand is neutral to mildly positive; it acts as a direct substitute for FX intervention, preventing the need to sell USTs. The effect on the sovereign's rate spread is complex; it raises specific sovereign funding costs but stabilizes the broader currency market. When liquidity dries up completely, developed and allied central banks turn to **(e) FIMA/swap lines**. They access dollar liquidity directly from the Federal Reserve by pledging Treasuries as collateral (FIMA repo) or through bilateral swap lines. The effect on UST demand is highly protective; it allows central banks to access dollars without selling their Treasuries into the open market, preventing a fire sale. The effect on their own rate spread is immediate compression by alleviating acute dollar funding shortages. [DIR: High Confidence]

When market-based defenses fail, sovereigns resort to structural restrictions. **(f) Capital controls** restrict the outflow of capital to preserve domestic liquidity and currency value, a drastic measure used in severe crises. The short-term effect on UST demand is neutral, but structurally negative long-term as it damages financial integration. It artificially compresses domestic spreads by trapping capital, though offshore spreads typically blow out. Meanwhile, long-term strategic shifts include **(g) gold accumulation**, where central banks diversify reserves away from fiat currencies into physical gold, aggressively pursued by China and Russia. This is structurally negative for UST demand and has marginal direct impact on spreads, though it may lower long-term sovereign risk perception. Finally, **(h) local-currency bond development** involves deepening domestic capital markets to borrow in local currency, reducing reliance on dollar-denominated debt. This is structurally negative for UST demand over decades, as it reduces the need to hoard dollar reserves, but it vastly reduces vulnerability to US rate shocks, structurally stabilizing local spreads over time. [DIR: High Confidence]

## 2. CARRY COMPLEX — DEEPEST SECTION 1

The carry trade—borrowing in low-yielding currencies to invest in high-yielding assets—is a massive, largely opaque force in global capital flows. The mid-2026 environment, characterized by a hawkish Fed, persistently high US real rates, and squeezed EM currencies, creates an extraordinarily precarious setup for the unwinding of these trades. The core funding currencies currently exhibit historically low policy rates: JPY (0.75%), CHF (0.0%), CNH (3.0%), and EUR (2.4%) [HARD: Central Bank Data, June 2026]. These jurisdictions provide cheap liabilities. The capital flows toward high-yield recipients such as TRY (37.0%), BRL (14.5%), MXN (6.5%), ZAR (7.0%), and INR (5.25%) [HARD: Central Bank Data, June 2026]. Crucially, the US Dollar itself, with the Fed Funds rate anchored at roughly 5.5% and the 10y at 4.49% (real 10y +2.16%) [HARD: US Treasury, June 2026], acts as the ultimate high-yield, low-risk vacuum, actively reshaping the carry landscape by stripping capital away from traditional EM targets. [DIR: High Confidence]

Mechanically, the carry trade relies on the assumption of low FX volatility and stable interest rate differentials. Investors borrow in Yen, convert to Dollars or Pesos, and buy local sovereign debt. The profit is the rate spread minus hedging costs (if hedged) or FX depreciation (if unhedged). In mid-2026, the high US real rate of +2.16% effectively nullifies the risk-adjusted appeal of many EM destinations. Why take on Brazilian or Turkish political risk when US Treasuries offer substantial real yields? This structural gravitational pull is actively starving EMs of carry capital. Unwind triggers typically include a hawkish pivot by the funding central bank (e.g., the BOJ), a sudden spike in global volatility (VIX), or a rapid depreciation of the recipient currency that wipes out years of yield advantage in a matter of days. When these triggers hit, the leverage inherent in carry trades forces rapid, indiscriminate liquidation of the high-yield assets and frantic repurchasing of the funding currency, leading to violent market dislocations. [DIR: High Confidence]

The historical lineage of carry unwinds is marked by spectacular, rapid-fire crises. The **1994 bond massacre** serves as the original template for Fed-driven capitulation. In February 1994, the Fed hiked the federal funds rate from 3.0% to 3.25%, the first of six hikes that took the rate to 5.5% [HARD: Federal Reserve, 1994]. This unexpected tightening collapsed global bond prices and sucked capital out of emerging markets. The delayed but devastating consequence was the Mexican peso crisis in December 1994, where the peso was devalued from 3.5 to 5.75 per dollar within days, culminating in a nearly $50 billion international bailout package [HARD: Brookings Institution/Richmond Fed, 1995]. The speed of the initial bond shock was rapid (weeks), but the EM currency collapse took 10 months to gestate before breaking violently. Four years later, the **1998 LTCM and Russian ruble default** triggered a legendary Yen carry unwind. In October 1998, as hedge funds violently deleveraged following the August Russian default, the Yen appreciated by nearly 15% against the dollar in a matter of days, one of the most dramatic FX moves in modern history [HARD: Federal Reserve IFDP 899, October 1998]. The magnitude of the equity and bond selloff was systemic, requiring Fed intervention, and the speed was blisteringly fast, unfolding over less than two weeks. [DIR: High Confidence]

The modern era of zero-interest-rate policy birthed the massive **2003-2007 Yen carry buildup**. Years of suppressed Japanese rates funded global risk-taking. The initial crack appeared on August 16, 2007, when the JPY price of the AUD collapsed by -7.7% in a single day, compared to a daily average change of just 0.7% prior to the event [HARD: CESifo WP 2707, August 2007]. This unwind saw severe volatility spikes and equity drawdowns. Further violent unwinds occurred in January 2008 and October 2008 as the global financial crisis accelerated, with the Yen appreciating massively as global leverage was extinguished. The speed was characterized by acute, multi-day shocks interspersed with false recoveries. The **2013 taper tantrum** demonstrated the vulnerability of the "Fragile Five" (BRL, ZAR, IDR, INR, TRY). Ben Bernanke's mere suggestion of tapering quantitative easing in May 2013 sent the US 10-year yield soaring by over 100 basis points by September. Capital violently fled these EMs; the Indian Rupee depreciated by roughly 20% between May and August [HARD: RBI data, 2013]. The magnitude was a severe EM currency crisis, and the speed was a sustained bleed over four months. [DIR: High Confidence]

More recent shocks highlight the fragility of the complex. The **August 11, 2015 CNH devaluation**, where the PBOC adjusted the yuan fix lower by nearly 2% [HARD: PBOC, August 2015], sent shockwaves through EM carry trades tied to Chinese growth, sparking a rapid, multi-week global equity selloff and EM currency depreciation. The **2018 EM crisis** saw severe localized unwinds in Turkey and Argentina as US rates rose. Most recently, the **August 2024 yen-carry unwind** provided a stark preview of current risks. The BOJ hiked rates on July 31, 2024, signaling an end to free money. By August 5, the VIX spiked above 60 intraday as hedge funds aggressively liquidated US tech and momentum stocks to cover Yen shorts [HARD: Wellington Management/Market Data, August 2024]. The Yen appreciated sharply, and global equities suffered a violent, albeit brief, multi-trillion dollar drawdown. Quantifying the typical major carry unwind: it involves a 5% to 12% appreciation of the funding currency against high-beta recipients within a compressed 3-to-10 day window, accompanied by a VIX spike exceeding 35, and forced liquidations that can erase 10% or more of global equity market capitalization before central bank liquidity stabilizes the system. [DIR: High Confidence]

## 3. Diaspora-deposit mechanism

When traditional market access closes and FX reserves are bleeding rapidly, emerging markets with large expatriate populations deploy a specific, highly effective defensive tool: the diaspora deposit mechanism. This mechanism allows a sovereign to attract hard currency directly from its citizens abroad, bypassing volatile institutional capital markets and mitigating the need to sell US Treasuries to defend the local currency. In the mid-2026 environment, with the Hormuz shock devastating the current accounts of oil importers, this tool has transitioned from a niche policy option to a primary line of defense. [DIR: High Confidence]

India stands as the paramount architect of this strategy. On June 8, 2026, facing acute pressure, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) announced a new Foreign Currency Non-Resident (Bank) or FCNR(B) swap window [HARD: RBI Announcement, June 2026]. The target size is an ambitious $50-$70 billion. To attract this massive sum, banks are offering headline dollar rates (e.g., LIBOR/SOFR plus a spread), but the true allure is the synthetic local currency return. Reports indicate synthetic Rupee returns approaching 17% annualized over a 3-year lock-in. This exorbitant rate is made possible not by bank generosity, but by an RBI subsidy. The central bank provides a heavily discounted forward hedge to the participating banks, absorbing the currency depreciation risk onto its own balance sheet. This allows banks to offer risk-free, double-digit returns to non-resident Indians (NRIs), ensuring massive uptake. [DIR: High Confidence]

This 2026 mobilization is not unprecedented; it is the culmination of a long lineage of financial statecraft. India has successfully deployed this playbook during every major external crisis of the past three decades. The lineage begins with the India Development Bonds (IDB) in 1991, which raised $1.6 billion to stave off a balance of payments crisis. This was followed by the Resurgent India Bonds (RIB) in 1998 ($4.2 billion) following nuclear test sanctions, and the India Millennium Deposits (IMD) in 2000 ($5.5 billion) [HARD: RBI Historical Data, 1991-2000]. The most direct precedent for the 2026 action is the 2013 FCNR(B) swap window deployed by then-Governor Raghuram Rajan during the Taper Tantrum. That scheme, utilizing the exact same subsidized hedging mechanics, successfully raised $34 billion, instantly crushing Rupee volatility and restoring FX reserves without selling a single US Treasury [HARD: RBI Data, 2013]. [DIR: High Confidence]

The efficacy of the diaspora model has led to its adoption across the emerging world, though rarely with India's scale. Israel is the pioneer of the continuous diaspora bond, utilizing the Development Corporation for Israel (DCI) to issue bonds steadily since 1951, providing a reliable, non-market source of hard currency regardless of geopolitical stress. Pakistan has increasingly relied on its Roshan Digital Account, a comprehensive banking framework for overseas Pakistanis designed to funnel remittance capital into government certificates and local deposits to support the perpetually strained Rupee. [DIR: Medium Confidence]

Other nations attempt similar feats under duress. Nigeria frequently offers premium rates to attract diaspora dollars to alleviate chronic FX shortages. Egypt has issued specific dollar-denominated certificates of deposit with high yields exclusively for Egyptians abroad to counteract capital flight. Bangladesh and Sri Lanka, both having suffered severe recent economic crises, have also engineered specialized premium accounts and bonds targeting expatriate wealth. The critical substitution mechanism across all these examples is identical: every dollar raised from a dentist in New Jersey or an engineer in Dubai is a dollar the central bank does not have to raise by liquidating its holdings of US Treasuries. In a macro environment where foreign central bank selling is a major risk to US yields, these diaspora schemes act as vital shock absorbers for the global financial system. [DIR: High Confidence]

## 4. Loop back to US rates

The mid-2026 interest rate environment is fundamentally hostile to emerging markets, and the sovereign responses outlined above directly dictate the trajectory of US Treasuries. Across the globe, the dominant response mechanisms are bifurcating based on balance sheet strength and energy dependence. Japan and China, possessing massive FX reserves but facing structural currency weakness against a 4.49% US 10-year yield, have primarily relied on FX intervention via UST sales. However, to mask outright dumping and avoid crashing the market they hold, they heavily utilize FIMA repo, pledging Treasuries for cash rather than selling them outright. Conversely, India, Brazil, and Turkey are forced into aggressive policy-rate defense and alternative funding like diaspora deposits, attempting to preserve what remains of their dollar liquidity. [DIR: High Confidence]

The most profound and disruptive structural shift in 2026, however, is the complete fracture of the petrodollar recycling channel. For half a century, the global financial architecture relied on a simple, elegant loop: oil importers paid dollars for energy, those dollars flowed to Gulf exporters, and the Gulf states subsequently reinvested those massive surpluses directly into US Treasuries. This persistent, price-insensitive bid from the Middle East quietly subsidized American borrowing costs, helped fund US deficits, and cemented the dollar's status as the supreme reserve currency [HARD: Council on Foreign Relations, 2026 analyses]. [DIR: High Confidence]

The March 2026 closure of the Strait of Hormuz has violently severed both halves of this equation. On the exporter side, the revenue has simply stopped. Gulf producers physically cannot route their barrels to the open market. Instead of accumulating vast surpluses—Saudi Arabia, for example, held $149.5 billion in US Treasury securities as recently as December 2025 [HARD: US Treasury TIC Data, Dec 2025]—these nations are now running sudden, acute fiscal deficits. They have transitioned from being the marginal buyer of US debt to potential net sellers, as they draw down existing reserves to fund domestic budgets and massive, unexpected military expenditures. [DIR: High Confidence]

On the importer side, the dynamic is equally destructive for US rates. Energy-dependent nations are forced to pay exorbitant premiums for alternative, non-Hormuz oil supplies. To fund these purchases and defend their collapsing currencies against the resulting inflationary shock, EM central banks are actively selling their existing US Treasury holdings. Thus, the petrodollar loop has inverted: exporters have stopped buying, and importers have started selling. This dual shock is clearly visible in the US Treasury International Capital (TIC) data, which shows persistent net foreign selling by official institutions throughout the spring of 2026 [HARD: Treasury TIC Data, April 2026]. [DIR: High Confidence]

This fracture also severely impacts the "Caribbean" TIC line. Historically, this line serves as a proxy for offshore hedge fund activity and basis trades, often funded indirectly by recycled global liquidity, including Gulf capital managed out of London or New York. As the primary liquidity source dries up, the leverage capacity of these offshore vehicles contracts, leading to an unwinding of Treasury basis trades and further exacerbating liquidity issues in the Treasury market. The evaporation of petrodollar recycling is not a marginal event; it is a primary structural driver sustaining the US 10-year yield at 4.49% despite growing domestic economic friction. Without the Gulf bid, the US Treasury must clear record issuance entirely through price-sensitive domestic buyers and allied central banks. [DIR: High Confidence]

## 5. SCENARIO MATRIX — DEEPEST SECTION 2

The collision of the Hormuz oil shock, high US real rates, and fragile carry structures creates a highly volatile forward path. This scenario matrix maps the four most probable trajectories for foreign demand shifts and their direct transmission to the US yield curve over the next 12 to 18 months. These scenarios are non-linear and path-dependent, reflecting the extreme fragility of the mid-2026 global financial architecture. [DIR: High Confidence]

**ESCALATION (Hormuz extended, oil spike persists)**
In this scenario, the conflict with Iran deepens, and the Strait of Hormuz remains impassable well into 2027. Oil prices remain structurally elevated above $120/bbl, creating a persistent stagflationary shock globally. The dominant sovereign response is defensive desperation. EM importers, hemorrhaging capital to pay for energy, engage in heavy, sustained FX intervention, liquidating their US Treasury holdings at a rapid clip. Simultaneously, Gulf exporters, physically unable to sell their primary asset, run deepening fiscal deficits and entirely cease their petrodollar recycling into USTs. [DIR: High Confidence]
*   **Net UST-Demand Effect:** Strongly negative. The market faces coordinated structural selling pressure from EMs and a complete absence of the traditional Gulf bid. The quantifiable impact is an estimated +30 to +50 basis point increase on the US 10-year yield, pushing it toward 5.0%, driven entirely by a foreign buyers' strike and supply indigestion.
*   **Lead Indicators:** Persistent net foreign selling in monthly TIC data; sustained crude prices above $120/bbl; rapidly depleting EM FX reserves (especially in India, Turkey, and Southeast Asia).
*   **Falsifiers:** A sudden, massive surge in domestic US bank buying that seamlessly absorbs the foreign supply; or Gulf exporters rapidly establishing massive, secure pipeline alternatives that bypass Hormuz and instantly restore their revenue and the petrodollar loop.

**DE-ESCALATION (Iran ceasefire, oil retreats)**
This scenario envisions a diplomatic breakthrough leading to a ceasefire with Iran and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz. Oil prices retreat sharply to the $70-$80/bbl range, instantly relieving the acute stagflationary pressure on the global economy. The dominant sovereign response pivots from defense to recovery. EM importers cease their FX intervention and begin the long process of rebuilding their depleted FX reserves, which necessitates purchasing US Treasuries. Concurrently, Gulf exporters resume full production, their revenues surge, and the petrodollar recycling loop into US Treasuries is powerfully re-established. [DIR: High Confidence]
*   **Net UST-Demand Effect:** Highly positive. A strong, coordinated return of foreign official buyers to the Treasury market occurs. The quantifiable impact is an estimated -25 to -40 basis point compression on the US 10-year yield, pulling it back toward 4.0% as the supply-demand imbalance resolves.
*   **Lead Indicators:** A sharp, sustained decline in global oil benchmarks; subsequent TIC data showing a strong resumption of net foreign official purchases; a rapid easing of EM currency depreciation metrics.
*   **Falsifiers:** US domestic inflation remaining stubbornly high despite the energy retreat, preventing the Fed from signaling rate cuts and keeping yields elevated regardless of the resumed foreign bid; or EMs choosing to prioritize domestic fiscal stimulus over rebuilding their US dollar reserve buffers.

**CARRY UNWIND (BOJ hawkish or JPY squeeze)**
This scenario is triggered not by geopolitics, but by monetary policy mechanics. The Bank of Japan, facing imported inflation and an unacceptably weak Yen, executes a hawkish pivot, raising policy rates above 1.0% and forcefully signaling quantitative tightening. The dominant sovereign response from Japan is a massive repatriation of capital. Japanese institutions, life insurers, and retail investors violently sell their foreign assets—including massive blocks of US Treasuries and US equities—to bring capital back to higher-yielding domestic markets. In the EM sphere, the rapid appreciation of the Yen triggers a catastrophic collapse in high-yield currencies (BRL, MXN, ZAR) as leveraged carry trades are forced to liquidate. [DIR: High Confidence]
*   **Net UST-Demand Effect:** Severely negative in the short term. The market must absorb acute, concentrated selling pressure from Japanese investors, who are collectively the largest single foreign holder of US debt (approx. $1.2 trillion) [HARD: US Treasury TIC Data, April 2026]. The quantifiable impact is a sharp, violent +20 to +40 basis point spike in US yields within a matter of days or weeks, accompanied by severe illiquidity.
*   **Lead Indicators:** BOJ policy rate hikes above 1.0%; a rapid appreciation of the JPY (e.g., breaking below 140 USD/JPY); a severe spike in the VIX (>35) alongside forced equity liquidations.
*   **Falsifiers:** Japanese institutions opting to heavily hedge their FX risk rather than outright selling their underlying US assets; or the unwind occurring in a slow, orderly fashion orchestrated via back-channel central bank coordination rather than a violent market shock.

**US LONG-END / TERM-PREMIUM SPIKE (10y → 5%+, foreign demand strike)**
In this darkest scenario, foreign investors lose faith in the fiscal trajectory of the United States. Driven by the realization that US deficits are structurally uncontainable and inflation may be permanently unanchored, a broad-based "buyers' strike" materializes. The dominant sovereign response is a collective refusal by both DM and EM central banks to accumulate further dollar reserves. Instead, they pivot aggressively toward gold, commodities, and bilateral currency arrangements. [DIR: High Confidence]
*   **Net UST-Demand Effect:** Severely, structurally negative. The market enters a self-reinforcing doom loop: lack of foreign demand drives yields higher, which increases US debt servicing costs, which further widens the deficit, which deters further investment. The quantifiable impact is the 10-year yield breaching 5.0% and establishing a new, higher structural floor, driven primarily by an exploding term premium rather than Fed policy expectations.
*   **Lead Indicators:** Repeated weak bid-to-cover ratios and long tails at US 10-year and 30-year Treasury auctions; a violent steepening of the US yield curve as term premiums expand; gold prices rising simultaneously with rising real yields (indicating a fundamental loss of confidence in the fiat anchor).
*   **Falsifiers:** A severe, deflationary US recession that forces massive Fed rate cuts, triggering a domestic safe-haven rally that overwhelms fiscal fears; or the US government implementing severe financial repression (e.g., forcing domestic banks and pension funds to hold mandated quotas of Treasuries) to artificially cap yields.

## 6. Layman Anchor Bank + Kit

To communicate these complex dynamics to non-specialist policymakers or board members, the following analogies and explanations are designed to reduce cognitive load while preserving analytical accuracy. These anchors serve as vital communication tools during crisis conditions. [DIR: High Confidence]

**Everyday-Money Analogy: The Carry Trade Casino**
Imagine the global economy is a giant casino. The **Carry Trade** is like borrowing chips from the cheapest, most boring table in the casino (Japan), where they charge almost zero interest for the loan. You take those borrowed chips and walk over to the high-stakes, high-reward VIP room (Emerging Markets or US Tech stocks). As long as the VIP room pays out more in winnings than the boring table charges in interest, you make free, effortless money. However, if the VIP room suddenly crashes, or the manager of the boring table suddenly demands higher interest, you have to frantically sell everything you own to pay back your original loan. That chaotic, desperate selling across the entire casino is a "carry unwind."

**Worked Numeric: How Country Selling USTs Raises US Mortgage Rates**
It can be unintuitive why a crisis in Asia affects a homeowner in Ohio. The math works through supply and demand. If the Bank of Japan needs dollars to defend the Yen, they might sell $50 billion of US Treasuries on the open market. To find buyers for that massive, sudden extra supply of bonds, the price of the bonds must drop to entice buyers. It is a fundamental law of finance that when bond prices drop, their yield (the interest rate they pay) goes up. Because US 30-year fixed mortgage rates are strictly priced off the 10-year Treasury yield, that Japanese selling directly and immediately pushes up the 10-year yield, which directly increases the monthly mortgage payment for a homebuyer in Ohio.

**Top Misconception 1: "Foreigners hold all US debt."**
This is fundamentally false. Foreign investors hold roughly $9.5 trillion of US debt, which represents only about 25% of the total outstanding [HARD: US Treasury Data, 2026]. The vast majority of US debt is held by domestic entities: the Federal Reserve, US mutual funds, commercial banks, and American citizens. While foreign flows dictate the margin and can spike rates in the short term, the core of US funding is domestic.

**Top Misconception 2: "If China sells its Treasuries, the US will go bankrupt."**
False. A sudden Chinese liquidation (they hold roughly $0.7 trillion) would certainly spike interest rates and cause temporary market chaos. However, the US borrows in its own sovereign currency. In an extreme crisis, the Federal Reserve can simply act as the buyer of last resort (via Quantitative Easing) to absorb the supply, preventing bankruptcy, though risking inflation. The threat is to market stability, not sovereign solvency.

**Top Misconception 3: "High oil prices are always terrible for US financial markets."**
Historically, this is false. Under the "petrodollar" system, when oil prices spiked, Gulf nations took their massive windfall profits and reinvested them directly into US Treasuries. This massive influx of capital actually helped keep US interest rates low, cushioning the economic blow. The 2026 Hormuz crisis is uniquely dangerous because it chokes off the physical volume of oil, destroying the revenue and breaking this protective recycling loop entirely.
