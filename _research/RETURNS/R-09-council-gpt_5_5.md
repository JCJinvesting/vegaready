## 1 · SCOPE & STATE

This dossier is for the US Rates desk: nominal UST bills/notes/bonds, TIPS/real rates, breakevens and inflation forwards, curve slope, term premium, Treasury auction demand, repo/SOFR funding, swap-spread plumbing, QT/RRP/reserve accounting, and duration/convexity risk in government-rate instruments. [DIR; confidence: high; scope definition]
It is not a corporate-credit dossier: default risk, corporate spread beta, issuer fundamentals, and capital-structure relative value belong to Credit, while FX carry and rate-differential trades belong to FX even when UST yields are a driver. [DIR; confidence: high; desk-boundary convention]
The regime call is: **an upward-sloping, dis-inverted curve is being pulled by two opposing engines—oil-driven CPI/Fed repricing lifts the front end and term premium, while geopolitical safe-haven/growth fear can cap the long end—so the base case is a realized inflation shock with bear-flattening pressure at first and bear-steepening risk if term premium de-anchors.** [DIR; confidence: medium-high; synthesis]

## 2 · THE ANALYST SPINE (§1–§10)

**§1 Regime — DGS10 and Fed path.** The read is “restrictive policy plus oil-inflation shock risk,” not a clean recessionary bond-bull setup, because DGS10 is **4.45% on 2026-06-11** and the March FOMC still had a **3.50–3.75%** target range with inflation “somewhat elevated” and Middle East implications “uncertain” ([FRED DGS10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS10), [March 2026 FOMC statement](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20260318a.htm)). [HARD; confidence: high]
Best indicators are **DGS10** for the long nominal risk-free rate and **DFF/SEP median fed funds** for the policy-rate anchor; exact FRED IDs are DGS10 and DFF, while SEP is a Federal Reserve table rather than a FRED series ([FRED DGS10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS10), [FRED DFF](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFF), [March 2026 SEP](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomcprojtabl20260318.htm)). [HARD; confidence: high]
Current values are DGS10 **4.45% on 2026-06-11**, DFF **3.62% on 2026-06-11**, and SEP 2026 fed-funds median **3.4% on 2026-03-18** ([FRED DGS10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS10), [FRED DFF](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFF), [March 2026 SEP](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomcprojtabl20260318.htm)). [HARD; confidence: high]
Mechanically, an oil shock raises the expected near-term policy-rate path if the Fed treats energy-driven headline inflation as persistence risk rather than a transitory relative-price shock, and that impulse initially lives most strongly in bills, 2s, and SOFR futures rather than 30s. [DIR; confidence: medium-high]
A clean rates-desk trigger for changing regime would be the combination of falling oil, narrowing breakevens, and a front-end rally that pulls DFF-forward proxies back toward the March SEP path. [DIR; confidence: medium]

**§2 Structure — T10Y2Y, T10Y3M, and bull/bear quadrants.** The curve is officially positive: **T10Y2Y +0.39% on 2026-06-12** and **T10Y3M +0.70% on 2026-06-12**, while the task's desk-live slope is +40 bp for 10s2s and +77 bp for 10y–3m ([FRED T10Y2Y](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/T10Y2Y), [FRED T10Y3M](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/T10Y3M)). [HARD; confidence: high]
The quadrant read is currently **bear-steepened versus the deeply inverted 2022–24 world**, but the next impulse is two-sided: oil/Fed shock = bear flattener if 2s lead higher, while supply/term-premium shock = bear steepener if 10s/30s cheapen more than front-end rates. [DIR; confidence: medium-high]
Best indicators are exact FRED IDs **T10Y2Y** and **T10Y3M**, plus the level panel DGS2/DGS10/DGS3MO to separate spread moves from parallel moves ([FRED T10Y2Y](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/T10Y2Y), [FRED T10Y3M](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/T10Y3M), [FRED DGS2](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS2)). [HARD; confidence: high]
Dis-inversion is not automatically “all clear,” because the yield-curve recession debate treats re-steepening after inversion as either a normalization signal or, when led by front-end collapse, a late-cycle warning; the current episode is not a classic bull steepener because the front-end has not collapsed ([Federal Reserve yield-curve note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/predicting-recession-probabilities-using-the-slope-of-the-yield-curve-20180301.html), [FRED T10Y3M](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/T10Y3M)). [DIR; confidence: medium]
The desk should tag each daily curve move by level and slope: **bull steepener** = yields down, short-end down more; **bull flattener** = yields down, long-end down more; **bear steepener** = yields up, long-end up more; **bear flattener** = yields up, front-end up more. [DIR; confidence: high]

**§3 Valuation — DFII10 and term premium.** Real-rate valuation is demanding because DFII10 is **2.16% on 2026-06-11**, and a positive real yield above 2% means nominal buyers are being paid a sizable real return before inflation compensation ([FRED DFII10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFII10)). [HARD; confidence: high]
Term-premium valuation is less certain because ACM and Kim-Wright differ by construction: NY Fed ACM puts the 10y premium at **0.6697% on 2026-05-29**, while Kim-Wright/THREEFYTP10 is **0.8019% on 2026-06-05** ([NY Fed ACM CSV](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/data_indicators/acmPlot_data.csv), [FRED THREEFYTP10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/THREEFYTP10)). [HARD; confidence: high]
Best free indicators are **DFII10**, **THREEFYTP10**, and the NY Fed ACM CSV field **TERMYld**; the requested **ACMTP10** exact FRED ID was not available through the FRED page fetch, so the official NY Fed CSV is the free source to use for ACM. [HARD; confidence: high]
ACM is useful because it decomposes yields into expected short-rate path plus residual term premium, while Kim-Wright is useful as an independent model check; neither is a traded price, so the valuation signal should be treated as model-estimated compensation for duration risk rather than a no-arbitrage executable spread. [DIR; confidence: high]
If oil keeps realized inflation high while fiscal supply and QT continue, the correct valuation question is not “is 4.5% high,” but “is 0.7–0.8% enough term premium for holding 8–16 duration in a world with uncertain inflation and reduced central-bank absorption.” [DIR; confidence: medium-high]

**§4 Fundamentals/cycle — T10YIE, T5YIFR, Fed reaction function.** Inflation compensation is elevated but not unanchored: T10YIE is **2.31% on 2026-06-12** and T5YIFR is **2.23% on 2026-06-12** ([FRED T10YIE](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/T10YIE), [FRED T5YIFR](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/T5YIFR)). [HARD; confidence: high]
The fundamental shock is headline-first because BLS reported May 2026 CPI-U **+0.5% m/m and +4.2% y/y**, energy **+3.9% m/m and +23.5% y/y**, and gasoline **+7.0% m/m and +40.5% y/y** ([BLS CPI Summary](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.nr0.htm)). [HARD; confidence: high]
The Fed reaction function is asymmetric here: if oil only lifts headline inflation briefly, the Fed may look through it; if it lifts inflation expectations, wage/price behavior, or core services persistence, the Fed has to defend credibility, especially after stating inflation remains somewhat elevated ([March 2026 FOMC statement](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20260318a.htm)). [DIR; confidence: medium-high]
A Fed staff note estimates that a permanent **10% oil-price increase** raises energy CPI by about **1.5% on impact and 2.3% after two quarters**, food CPI by about **0.3% after eight quarters**, and core CPI by about **0.1% after eight quarters**, giving a practical CPI impulse range for the desk's oil scenarios ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/second-round-effects-of-oil-prices-on-inflation-in-the-advanced-foreign-economies-accessible-20231215.htm)). [HARD; confidence: medium-high]
The best indicators are **T10YIE**, **T5YIFR**, **CPI energy/gasoline**, and **FOMC language**, because the market question is whether the shock stays in near-term breakevens or leaks into five-year/five-year inflation expectations. [DIR; confidence: medium-high]

**§5 Cohorts/segments — front-end, belly, long bond, nominals/TIPS, bills/coupons.** The front end is the Fed path: DGS3MO is **3.78%**, DGS2 is **4.05%**, and DFF is **3.62%**, so the 2y already embeds policy risk above realized overnight cash ([FRED DGS3MO](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS3MO), [FRED DGS2](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS2), [FRED DFF](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFF)). [HARD; confidence: high]
The belly is the collision zone between Fed path and term premium: DGS5 is **4.18% on 2026-06-11**, and a 5y par-bond modified-duration example at that yield has duration about **4.47** and loses about **1.11%** for a +25 bp parallel shock before carry ([FRED DGS5](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS5); calculation saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_math_examples.json`). [HARD; confidence: high for market value, medium for model example]
The long bond is term-premium and convexity: DGS30 is **4.95% on 2026-06-11**, and a 30y par-bond example at 4.95% has modified duration about **15.54**, convexity about **355**, and loses about **3.77%** on a +25 bp shock before carry ([FRED DGS30](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS30); calculation saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_math_examples.json`). [HARD; confidence: high for market value, medium for model example]
Nominal-versus-TIPS allocation depends on whether oil inflation is compensated: with DFII10 **2.16%** and T10YIE **2.31%**, the 10y nominal yield is roughly real yield plus breakeven, so TIPS outperform nominals if realized CPI plus inflation-risk repricing beats the breakeven path ([FRED DFII10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFII10), [FRED T10YIE](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/T10YIE)). [HARD/DIR; confidence: high]
Bills versus coupons is a liquidity and convexity choice: bills avoid duration drawdown but reinvest at whatever the Fed does next, while coupons lock yields and add roll-down/convexity exposure that helps only if yields stabilize or fall. [DIR; confidence: high]

**§6 Factors — duration, carry/roll-down, curve, convexity.** Duration is first-order: in the worked par-bond examples, a +100 bp shock produces approximate price returns of **−1.88% for 2y**, **−4.35% for 5y**, **−7.62% for 10y**, and **−13.77% for 30y**, so the same rate view has very different P&L by tenor (calculation saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_math_examples.json`). [HARD; confidence: medium-high]
Convexity is second-order but important in tails: the same examples gain about **+8.38%** for a −100 bp move in 10s but lose **−7.62%** for a +100 bp move, and gain about **+17.32%** for a −100 bp move in 30s but lose **−13.77%** for a +100 bp move (calculation saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_math_examples.json`). [HARD; confidence: medium-high]
Carry/roll is currently positive under an unchanged curve: the illustrative one-year carry-plus-roll estimates are **4.20% for 2y**, **4.34% for 5y**, **4.85% for 10y**, and **5.33% for 30y**, but those returns can be erased by +50–75 bp long-end shocks in 10s/30s (calculation saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_math_examples.json`). [HARD; confidence: medium]
Curve factor risk matters because a 2s10s steepener can make money while an outright long-duration position loses money if both rates rise and 10s rise more than 2s. [DIR; confidence: high]
The desk should size shock P&L in **DV01 × bp move + convexity adjustment**, not in yield language alone, because a 25 bp move in 30s has roughly eight times the price effect of a 25 bp move in 2s in the worked examples. [DIR/HARD; confidence: medium-high]

**§7 Positioning/plumbing — auctions, swap spreads, SOFR/repo, QT/reserves/RRP, MBS convexity.** TreasuryDirect/Fiscal Data provides free auction fields including bid-to-cover, high yield, total tendered/accepted, and primary/direct/indirect bidder accepted amounts, and the **2026-06-10 10y reopening** had **$39.0bn accepted, $100.0bn tendered, 2.57 bid-to-cover, 4.538% high yield, $3.68bn primary-dealer accepted, $4.80bn direct accepted, and $30.44bn indirect accepted** ([Fiscal Data auctions API](https://api.fiscaldata.treasury.gov/services/api/fiscal_service/v1/accounting/od/auctions_query); data saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_recent_auctions.json`). [HARD; confidence: high]
The **2026-06-11 30y reopening** had **$22.0bn accepted, $51.2bn tendered, 2.33 bid-to-cover, 5.020% high yield, $3.24bn primary-dealer accepted, $5.56bn direct accepted, and $13.16bn indirect accepted**, which is an adequate but not euphoric demand read for long duration ([Fiscal Data auctions API](https://api.fiscaldata.treasury.gov/services/api/fiscal_service/v1/accounting/od/auctions_query); data saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_recent_auctions.json`). [HARD; confidence: high]
Auction **tails** require a when-issued yield at the bidding deadline, and I found no official free real-time WI feed; therefore tails should be marked **source_identified/no-free-real-time**, while bid-to-cover and bidder takedown are **latest_published** free via TreasuryDirect/Fiscal Data. [HARD/DIR; confidence: high]
Swap spreads are structurally capable of being negative because a Treasury yield embeds balance-sheet, funding, special-collateral, and supply premia while an OIS/SOFR swap fixed rate embeds collateralized expected overnight rates plus dealer balance-sheet costs; however, free real-time SOFR swap-spread levels are not available from FRED because its classic 10y swap series is discontinued ([FRED interest-rate swaps category](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/categories/32299), [NY Fed SOFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/sofr)). [DIR/HARD; confidence: high]
SOFR plumbing is clean but watchable: SOFR is a broad measure of overnight cash borrowing collateralized by Treasury securities, published around 8:00 a.m. ET and based on tri-party, GCF, and FICC-cleared bilateral repo transactions, so quarter-end balance-sheet constraints or collateral scarcity can transmit into SOFR even if the policy target range is unchanged ([NY Fed SOFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/sofr)). [HARD/DIR; confidence: high]
QT accounting is now a reserve-scarcity monitoring exercise because the Fed says balance-sheet reduction is conducted mainly by adjusting reinvestment of SOMA principal payments and that the long-run goal is an ample-reserves regime, while current RRP is just **$0.454bn** against reserves of **$3.081tn** and WALCL of **$6.725tn** ([Fed balance-sheet principles](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20220126c.htm), [FRED RRPONTSYD](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/RRPONTSYD), [FRED WRESBAL](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WRESBAL), [FRED WALCL](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WALCL)). [HARD; confidence: high]
The balance-sheet identity for desk use is: **Fed assets down via QT must be mirrored by liabilities down or capital/other factors changing; if ON RRP is near zero, the marginal drain hits reserves or is offset by TGA/currency changes**, and BIS explains that central-bank bond sales to banks swap reserves for bonds while sales to non-banks can shrink deposits and bank balance sheets ([BIS Quarterly Review](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2309d.htm), [Fed balance-sheet principles](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20220126c.htm)). [DIR/HARD; confidence: high]
MBS convexity can amplify big moves because higher rates extend mortgage duration and create paying/receiving hedging needs by mortgage investors, while lower rates shorten duration and can intensify rallies; the live mortgage anchor is **6.52% for the 30y fixed mortgage average on 2026-06-11** ([FRED MORTGAGE30US](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/MORTGAGE30US)). [DIR/HARD; confidence: medium-high]

**§8 Cross-asset — rates ↔ dollar, equities, credit, gold.** Higher front-end real rates usually support the dollar by improving cash yield and tightening global financial conditions, but broad dollar spot is not available as an official free real-time feed in the source set, so the task's “DXY around 100” is a live-environment input rather than a verified free-source value. [DIR; confidence: medium]
Higher real rates pressure long-duration equities through discount rates, and the 2023 Treasury tantrum is the template: the Fed notes the 10y yield rose from below 4% to above 5% and then back to 3.9%, driven primarily by higher term premium linked to QT, issuance, and uncertainty ([Federal Reserve 2023 Treasury Tantrum note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-treasury-tantrum-of-2023-20240903.html)). [HARD/DIR; confidence: high]
Credit is outside desk scope but rates matter to credit via refinancing cost and risk-free discount rate; a front-end oil/Fed repricing hurts floating-rate borrowers less on mark-to-market but more through cash interest expense, while long-end term-premium repricing hurts fixed-rate issuers through new-issue concessions. [DIR; confidence: medium-high]
Gold is two-sided: geopolitical stress and inflation fear support it, while higher real yields raise its opportunity cost; the task's “gold record near $4,365” is a live-environment input with no official free real-time feed in this workstream. [DIR; confidence: medium]
The best cross-asset rates dashboard is therefore DGS2/DGS10/DFII10/T10YIE/SOFR plus no-free-feed placeholders for DXY, spot gold, MOVE, and live swap spreads. [DIR; confidence: high]

**§9 Catalyst transmission — Iran war/Hormuz → rates with explicit chains.** The physical shock is large because EIA says the Strait of Hormuz handled about **20 million b/d in 2024**, around **20% of global petroleum liquids consumption** and more than one-quarter of global seaborne oil trade ([EIA Hormuz chokepoint](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=65504)). [HARD; confidence: high]
The task's live environment says the Strait has been closed since **2026-03-04**, OPEC output is down **30%+**, and Brent fell from roughly **$101 to $87** on fragile ceasefire/peace-talk headlines; these are desk-live inputs and should be tracked against EIA's official May forecast that assumed closure through late May and gradual resumption in June ([EIA STEO global oil](https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/steo/report/global_oil.php)). [HARD for EIA assumptions, DIR for task-live pricing; confidence: medium]
Chain A is **Hormuz closure → crude/gasoline shock → headline CPI → inflation expectations → Fed path → bills/2s/SOFR higher**, and the parameter rule is that each permanent 10% oil rise can add roughly 0.4 pp to headline CPI in broad estimates or, in the Fed note's decomposition, raise energy CPI 1.5–2.3% and core CPI about 0.1% over eight quarters ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/second-round-effects-of-oil-prices-on-inflation-in-the-advanced-foreign-economies-accessible-20231215.htm)). [HARD/DIR; confidence: medium-high]
Chain B is **Hormuz closure → growth-tax/safe-haven shock → lower risk appetite → long-end rally or bull flattening**, but the long end only rallies durably if the market believes the Fed can look through the shock and that demand destruction dominates inflation persistence. [DIR; confidence: medium]
Chain C is **war + deficits/QT → higher uncertainty and duration supply premium → 10s/30s underperform → bear steepener**, and the 2023 analogue supports this channel because the Fed attributed that long-end selloff primarily to term premium linked to QT, issuance, and uncertainty ([Federal Reserve 2023 Treasury Tantrum note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-treasury-tantrum-of-2023-20240903.html)). [HARD/DIR; confidence: high]

## 5 · WINNERS / LOSERS

**Short duration wins** in the base oil/Fed shock because bills and short coupons avoid the 8–16 duration embedded in 10s/30s, and the worked examples show a +100 bp shock costs about **−1.88%** in 2y versus **−7.62%** in 10y and **−13.77%** in 30y before carry (calculation saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_math_examples.json`). [HARD/DIR; confidence: medium-high]
**Long duration wins** in the de-escalation or growth-scare rally because convexity turns favorable: the worked examples show a −100 bp shock gains about **+8.38%** in 10y and **+17.32%** in 30y before carry (calculation saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_math_examples.json`). [HARD/DIR; confidence: medium-high]
**Belly duration is the compromise trade** because it has more carry/roll than bills but less term-premium tail risk than 30s; the illustrative 5y carry-plus-roll is **4.34%** under an unchanged curve, but a +100 bp shock still costs **−4.35%** before carry (calculation saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_math_examples.json`). [HARD/DIR; confidence: medium]

**TIPS beat nominals** when realized CPI and breakeven repricing exceed the inflation compensation embedded in nominals, and that case is plausible when May gasoline CPI is **+40.5% y/y** and 10y breakevens are only **2.31%** ([BLS CPI Summary](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.nr0.htm), [FRED T10YIE](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/T10YIE)). [HARD/DIR; confidence: medium-high]
**Nominals beat TIPS** in de-escalation if oil falls, breakevens compress, and real yields rally less than nominal yields, because TIPS then lose the inflation-compensation tailwind. [DIR; confidence: medium]
**Floating-rate instruments win** if the Fed hikes or stays higher for longer because coupon income resets upward, but they lose relative upside in an aggressive bull-steepener because they do not lock long duration before yields fall. [DIR; confidence: high]

**Front-end receivers win** in de-escalation because cuts re-enter the path and 2y yields can fall faster than 10s, while **front-end payers win** in a sticky-inflation shock because the policy path reprices higher. [DIR; confidence: medium-high]
**Long-bond longs win** only in the safe-haven/growth-scare branch or if term premium compresses, while **long-bond shorts win** in the supply/QT bear-steepener branch where ACM/KW premium and auction concessions rise. [DIR; confidence: medium-high]
**MBS holders face negative convexity** because rising rates extend duration and falling rates shorten duration, so MBS can underperform duration-matched Treasuries in large rate moves unless compensated by spread. [DIR; confidence: high]

**Auction concession buyers win** when weak auctions tail and then stabilize, because they capture new-issue cheapness; **pre-auction longs lose** when dealer balance-sheet constraints force bigger concessions. [DIR; confidence: medium]
**Repo lenders and collateral-rich accounts win** during SOFR/repo spikes because cash earns more against Treasury collateral, while leveraged RV books lose if financing jumps faster than asset carry. [DIR; confidence: medium-high]
**Gold and inflation hedges can win** in the geopolitical/inflation branch, but gold's rate sensitivity makes it vulnerable if real yields rise faster than geopolitical risk premia. [DIR; confidence: medium]

A layperson should read these numbers as four levers: cash rates say what money earns today, 2y says what the Fed may do, 10y says the economy’s long discount rate, and breakevens say how much inflation compensation is embedded. [DIR; confidence: high]

## 7 · LAYMAN KIT

**1. Duration = bond price speedometer.** The everyday analogy is a seesaw: the longer the bond, the farther you sit from the fulcrum, so a small rate move makes your price move more. [DIR; confidence: high]
Worked example: a 10y par Treasury at 4.45% has modified duration about **8.00**, so a +25 bp yield move costs roughly **2.0%**, while a 30y par Treasury at 4.95% has modified duration about **15.54**, so the same +25 bp costs roughly **3.8%** before carry (calculation saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_math_examples.json`). [HARD; confidence: medium-high]
Top misconceptions: duration is not maturity; duration is not a forecast; and a high coupon lowers duration but does not eliminate rate risk. [DIR; confidence: high]

**2. Convexity = the bond’s airbag.** The everyday analogy is a car suspension: convexity softens gains/losses differently as moves get large. [DIR; confidence: high]
Worked example: the 30y par-bond example loses **13.77%** for +100 bp but gains **17.32%** for −100 bp, so large rallies help more than equal-sized selloffs hurt in a plain Treasury (calculation saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_math_examples.json`). [HARD; confidence: medium-high]
Top misconceptions: convexity is always free; MBS convexity behaves like Treasury convexity; and convexity matters only to options desks. [DIR; confidence: high]

**3. Carry and roll-down = rent plus hill slope.** The everyday analogy is owning a rental house on a hill: rent is coupon income, and roll-down is the price benefit if your bond “rolls” into a lower-yield maturity bucket. [DIR; confidence: high]
Worked example: under an unchanged interpolated curve, the 10y par example has about **4.45% coupon carry** plus **0.40% roll price return**, or **4.85% carry-plus-roll**, before any rate shock (calculation saved in `/home/user/workspace/rates_math_examples.json`). [HARD; confidence: medium]
Top misconceptions: positive carry guarantees profit; roll-down survives curve reshaping; and carry is the same for hedged and unhedged trades. [DIR; confidence: high]

**4. Breakeven inflation = insurance premium.** The everyday analogy is choosing between a fixed mortgage and an inflation-adjusted lease: the breakeven is the inflation rate that makes the two deals equal. [DIR; confidence: high]
Worked example: if a 10y nominal yield is about **4.45%** and the 10y real yield is **2.16%**, the difference is about **2.29%**, close to the published 10y breakeven of **2.31%** ([FRED DGS10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS10), [FRED DFII10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFII10), [FRED T10YIE](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/T10YIE)). [HARD; confidence: high]
Top misconceptions: breakeven equals a pure inflation forecast; TIPS always win when inflation is high; and liquidity/risk premia do not matter. [DIR; confidence: high]

**5. Term premium = duration hazard pay.** The everyday analogy is being paid extra to take a long road through uncertain weather instead of a sequence of short trips. [DIR; confidence: high]
Worked example: if the 10y yield is **4.45%** and ACM term premium is **0.67%**, the model-implied expected short-rate component is roughly **3.78%**, before model and fitting caveats ([FRED DGS10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS10), [NY Fed ACM CSV](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/data_indicators/acmPlot_data.csv)). [HARD; confidence: medium]
Top misconceptions: term premium is directly observable; all models agree; and positive term premium always means bonds are cheap. [DIR; confidence: high]

**6. The curve = market storyline.** The everyday analogy is a menu of loan rates from overnight to 30 years; the shape tells you whether markets think today's high or low rates will last. [DIR; confidence: high]
Worked example: 2s10s is positive because 10y **4.45%** minus 2y **4.05%** equals **+40 bp**, while FRED's spread series prints **+39 bp** on 2026-06-12 ([FRED DGS10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS10), [FRED DGS2](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS2), [FRED T10Y2Y](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/T10Y2Y)). [HARD; confidence: high]
Top misconceptions: an upward curve always means growth is safe; inversion causes recession by itself; and 2s10s and 10y–3m always send the same signal. [DIR; confidence: high]

**7. Oil-to-CPI-to-Fed = shock transmission belt.** The everyday analogy is gasoline becoming more expensive, then delivery costs and wage demands responding, then the central bank deciding whether the household budget squeeze is temporary or persistent. [DIR; confidence: high]
Worked example: a permanent **10% oil price increase** can raise energy CPI roughly **1.5% immediately** and **2.3% after two quarters**, with smaller but persistent food/core effects, so a 20–30% oil shock can plausibly add multiple tenths to headline inflation if sustained ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/second-round-effects-of-oil-prices-on-inflation-in-the-advanced-foreign-economies-accessible-20231215.htm)). [HARD/DIR; confidence: medium]
Top misconceptions: oil only affects gasoline; the Fed ignores all supply shocks; and lower oil instantly removes all prior inflation pressure. [DIR; confidence: high]

**8. Repo/SOFR = overnight pawn shop for Treasuries.** The everyday analogy is borrowing cash overnight by leaving a Treasury as collateral. [DIR; confidence: high]
Worked example: SOFR is **3.60%**, IORB is **3.65%**, and ON RRP award is **3.50%**, so the secured overnight market is currently trading inside the administered-rate neighborhood ([FRED SOFR](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/SOFR), [FRED IORB](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/IORB), [FRED RRPONTSYAWARD](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/RRPONTSYAWARD)). [HARD; confidence: high]
Top misconceptions: repo is only a back-office issue; SOFR cannot spike if the Fed is on hold; and all Treasury collateral is equally easy to finance. [DIR; confidence: high]

**9. QT/RRP/reserves = the banking-system bathtub.** The everyday analogy is a bathtub where Fed assets are the water source and reserves/RRP/TGA/currency are drains and basins. [DIR; confidence: high]
Worked example: with Fed assets at **$6.725tn**, reserves at **$3.081tn**, TGA at **$828bn**, and RRP at **$0.454bn**, further QT has less RRP water to drain before reserves become the marginal adjustment item ([FRED WALCL](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WALCL), [FRED WRESBAL](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WRESBAL), [FRED WTREGEN](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WTREGEN), [FRED RRPONTSYD](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/RRPONTSYD)). [HARD; confidence: high]
Top misconceptions: QT is just the reverse of QE; reserves never become scarce; and Treasury issuance has no plumbing effect if the Fed is not changing rates. [DIR; confidence: high]

## 8 · COUNCIL & HONEST GAPS

**Camp 1: Inflation/Fed-dominant.** This camp argues Hormuz is first and foremost an inflation shock because EIA says Hormuz carries about **20 million b/d** and BLS already shows gasoline CPI **+40.5% y/y**, so the Fed cannot validate cuts while headline inflation is accelerating ([EIA Hormuz chokepoint](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=65504), [BLS CPI Summary](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.nr0.htm)). [HARD/DIR; confidence: medium-high]
Its trade expression is front-end underweight/2y payers, TIPS over nominals, and caution on duration until oil and breakevens break lower. [DIR; confidence: medium]
Its weak point is that supply shocks can destroy demand and ultimately become bullish for duration if the Fed looks through the shock or recession probability rises. [DIR; confidence: medium]

**Camp 2: Safe-haven/growth-dominant.** This camp argues the oil shock is a tax on consumers and a hit to risk appetite, so the long end can rally even while gasoline is hot. [DIR; confidence: medium]
