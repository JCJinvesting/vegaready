# P-08 GPT-5.5 research: US credit factors, market technicals, and regime correlations

**Executive summary — analytical angle.** My main conclusion is that credit “factor rotation” is best treated as a risk-budgeting problem rather than a style box: carry is usually short-volatility and high-DTS, quality is a liquidity-and-default hedge, value only diversifies if it is beta/DTS-aware, and momentum is most useful when it detects forced-risk reduction rather than when it simply chases spread tightening ([Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf); [Robeco DTS note](https://assets.ctfassets.net/tl4x668xzide/4Aapx0GUWLqc0ow8jlMAMb/0a213610fc9f13098d30308c88558723/201911-measuring-credit-risk-with-dts-hk-sg.pdf); [MSCI](https://www.msci.com/research-and-insights/blog-post/factors-in-focus-risk-sentiment-and-factor-dynamics-in-a-crisis)). The practical desk framework should therefore start with **DTS × funding-liquidity × issuer-quality** and only then map to familiar labels such as IG/HY, BB/CCC, loans, and EM sovereigns ([Robeco DTS note](https://assets.ctfassets.net/tl4x668xzide/4Aapx0GUWLqc0ow8jlMAMb/0a213610fc9f13098d30308c88558723/201911-measuring-credit-risk-with-dts-hk-sg.pdf); [Greenwood and Hanson](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w17197/w17197.pdf)). The strongest public evidence for this view is March 2020, when low-risk and quality factors protected capital while carry had the largest drawdown, and the subsequent rally began after liquidity backstops improved market functioning before large Fed purchases actually occurred ([MSCI](https://www.msci.com/research-and-insights/blog-post/factors-in-focus-risk-sentiment-and-factor-dynamics-in-a-crisis); [Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)).

## GAP 1 — Credit factors: definitions, stress behavior, and regime rotation

### 1.1 Practitioner definitions and the measurement problem

Credit carry is the expected excess return earned if market conditions remain unchanged, and public factor work commonly implements it with OAS or OAS plus roll-down rather than with a single universal desk formula ([Robeco](https://www.robeco.com/nl-be/visie/2017/06/does-carry-add-value-to-existing-credit-factors); [Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf)). A stricter practitioner version subtracts expected loss from spread, but the public studies reviewed here do not provide a single canonical live “OAS minus expected loss” data set that can be cited as a free current factor score ([Robeco](https://www.robeco.com/nl-be/visie/2017/06/does-carry-add-value-to-existing-credit-factors); [Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf)). The desk implication is that “carry” should be decomposed into spread compensation, expected default loss, roll-down, duration exposure, liquidity exposure, and downgrade risk before it is treated as an allocation signal ([Robeco](https://www.robeco.com/nl-be/visie/2017/06/does-carry-add-value-to-existing-credit-factors)).

Quality is the up-in-quality posture in credit, which can mean IG over HY, BB over CCC, higher issuer quality within a rating bucket, stronger balance sheets, lower leverage, lower default sensitivity, or shorter duration inside a rating group depending on the index construction ([Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf); [MSCI](https://www.msci.com/research-and-insights/blog-post/factors-in-focus-risk-sentiment-and-factor-dynamics-in-a-crisis)). Greenwood and Hanson provide a macro-quality version: credit booms are more dangerous when the quality of issuers deteriorates, and their issuer-quality measure forecasts subsequent high-yield excess returns ([Greenwood and Hanson](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w17197/w17197.pdf)). In desk terms, quality is both a balance-sheet factor and a market-access factor, because lower-quality issuers are most exposed when refinancing windows shut and dealer balance sheets stop absorbing risk ([BIS Annual Economic Report 2020](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2020e.pdf)).

Spread duration measures price sensitivity to an absolute change in credit spread, while DTS measures sensitivity to relative spread changes by multiplying spread duration by spread ([Robeco DTS note](https://assets.ctfassets.net/tl4x668xzide/4Aapx0GUWLqc0ow8jlMAMb/0a213610fc9f13098d30308c88558723/201911-measuring-credit-risk-with-dts-hk-sg.pdf)). The Robeco DTS note gives the simple risk-equivalence example that a one-year bond at a 500 bp spread and a five-year bond at a 100 bp spread each have DTS of 500, so they have comparable exposure to a proportional spread move even though their absolute spread durations differ ([Robeco DTS note](https://assets.ctfassets.net/tl4x668xzide/4Aapx0GUWLqc0ow8jlMAMb/0a213610fc9f13098d30308c88558723/201911-measuring-credit-risk-with-dts-hk-sg.pdf)). The same note says DTS became an industry standard after the 2007 Ben Dor, Dynkin, Hyman, Houweling, van Leeuwen, and Penninga paper, with later adoption in Barclays POINT, MSCI RiskMetrics, and Bloomberg PORT ([Robeco DTS note](https://assets.ctfassets.net/tl4x668xzide/4Aapx0GUWLqc0ow8jlMAMb/0a213610fc9f13098d30308c88558723/201911-measuring-credit-risk-with-dts-hk-sg.pdf)).

Value and momentum are more ambiguous in credit than in equities because a cheap bond may simply be a deteriorating balance sheet with poor liquidity and a large DTS footprint ([Northern Trust](https://www.northerntrust.com/content/dam/northerntrust/pws/nt/documents/investment-management/systematic-value-investing-in-corporate-bonds.pdf); [Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf)). Invesco implements credit value by ranking OAS among peers with comparable sector, rating, and country characteristics, and it implements momentum as trailing nine-month excess return excluding the most recent month ([Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf)). Northern Trust defines credit value as spread relative to fundamental risk and argues that value works best when separated from simple high-spread carry ([Northern Trust](https://www.northerntrust.com/content/dam/northerntrust/pws/nt/documents/investment-management/systematic-value-investing-in-corporate-bonds.pdf)).

Crowding does not have one canonical public gauge in credit, so it should be triangulated from issuer quality, fund flows, dealer inventories, primary issuance, and index-option hedging rather than represented by one invented number ([Greenwood and Hanson](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w17197/w17197.pdf); [NY Fed Primary Dealer Statistics](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/counterparties/primary-dealers-statistics); [ICI weekly flows](https://www.ici.org/research/stats/flows); [SIFMA corporate-bond statistics](https://www.sifma.org/research/statistics/us-corporate-bonds-statistics); [Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle](https://phd-finance.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:bc0971f7-a73f-475f-99d6-e73f9bc7f7f0/FS_spring21_paper_collin-dufresne.pdf)). Greenwood and Hanson’s issuer-quality signal is one of the cleanest public crowding proxies because deterioration in the quality of new debt issuers forecasts low future high-yield excess returns ([Greenwood and Hanson](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w17197/w17197.pdf)). Their paper reports that a one-standard-deviation rise in the issuer-quality deterioration measure lowers following-year high-yield excess returns by 4.5 percentage points, using a 1962–2008 sample and their ISSEDF construction ([Greenwood and Hanson](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w17197/w17197.pdf)).

### 1.2 Empirical factor rotation by regime

The most useful public rotation evidence comes from Invesco’s cross-market fixed-income factor study, which tests Bloomberg US IG, US HY, and EM USD indices from January 2001 through July 2023 using duration-matched excess returns and controls for duration, spread duration, DTS, rating, and sector exposures ([Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf)). Invesco finds that carry tends to have strong information ratios in low-stress environments and negative information ratios in high-stress environments, which supports the shorthand that carry is a short-volatility credit factor ([Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf)). The same study finds that quality has the opposite sign pattern, with weak performance in benign equity/volatility regimes and strong performance in stressed regimes ([Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf)).

In Invesco’s VIX-quintile table, HY carry has an information ratio of 1.41 in the lowest VIX quintile and -1.35 in the highest VIX quintile, while HY quality has an information ratio of -2.51 in the lowest VIX quintile and 3.32 in the highest VIX quintile ([Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf)). In the same table, IG carry has an information ratio of 2.17 in the lowest VIX quintile and -1.46 in the highest VIX quintile, while IG quality has an information ratio of -3.48 in the lowest VIX quintile and 2.50 in the highest VIX quintile ([Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf)). These are hard historical factor statistics from Invesco’s published study, not current live desk readings ([Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf)).

Northern Trust’s IG corporate value work offers a complementary business-cycle view for January 2004 through December 2023, using duration-matched active excess returns for the top factor quintile minus the benchmark ([Northern Trust](https://www.northerntrust.com/content/dam/northerntrust/pws/nt/documents/investment-management/systematic-value-investing-in-corporate-bonds.pdf)). In its economic-regime table, high quality outperforms by 0.11% per month in contraction and underperforms by -0.14% per month in expansion, while high carry underperforms by -0.06% per month in contraction and outperforms by 0.69% per month in expansion ([Northern Trust](https://www.northerntrust.com/content/dam/northerntrust/pws/nt/documents/investment-management/systematic-value-investing-in-corporate-bonds.pdf)). Northern Trust’s credit-regime table shows high carry at -0.18% per month in downturn and +0.70% per month in credit repair, while high quality is +0.19% per month in downturn ([Northern Trust](https://www.northerntrust.com/content/dam/northerntrust/pws/nt/documents/investment-management/systematic-value-investing-in-corporate-bonds.pdf)).

MSCI’s COVID-period factor analysis is the clearest public stress-test because it reports Q1 2020 active excess returns for USD IG corporate factor indexes relative to the parent index ([MSCI](https://www.msci.com/research-and-insights/blog-post/factors-in-focus-risk-sentiment-and-factor-dynamics-in-a-crisis)). MSCI reports that low-risk returned +3.28% and quality returned +1.77% in Q1 2020, while carry, defined as high OAS, suffered the largest drawdown among the factors considered ([MSCI](https://www.msci.com/research-and-insights/blog-post/factors-in-focus-risk-sentiment-and-factor-dynamics-in-a-crisis)). MSCI also reports that the USD IG OAS started 2020 at 107 bp, widened to 338 bp on March 23, 2020, and eased to 246 bp by March 30, 2020, which gives a hard stress-path reference for the factor drawdown ([MSCI](https://www.msci.com/research-and-insights/blog-post/factors-in-focus-risk-sentiment-and-factor-dynamics-in-a-crisis)).

Robeco’s 1994–2016 study is an important caveat against treating carry as an independent alpha factor, because it finds high-carry bonds have more risk and that regressions of carry on market, value, and other factors do not produce significant alpha ([Robeco](https://www.robeco.com/nl-be/visie/2017/06/does-carry-add-value-to-existing-credit-factors)). In Robeco’s reported quintile table, the highest carry quintile has Sharpe ratios of 0.19 in IG and 0.18 in HY, but high carry also loads materially on value and risk characteristics such as lower rating, higher beta, and lower distance to default ([Robeco](https://www.robeco.com/nl-be/visie/2017/06/does-carry-add-value-to-existing-credit-factors)). The practical interpretation is that carry often pays because it is exposed to credit beta and liquidity beta, not necessarily because it is a clean standalone mispricing premium ([Robeco](https://www.robeco.com/nl-be/visie/2017/06/does-carry-add-value-to-existing-credit-factors)).

### 1.3 Stress-episode decoder for credit factors

The 2008 GFC was a simultaneous credit-default, liquidity, and dealer-balance-sheet shock, so the best “factor” was quality plus liquidity rather than generic value or carry ([Bao, Pan, and Wang](https://www.mit.edu/~junpan/bond_liquidity_jf.pdf); [Cambridge Associates](https://publishedresearch.cambridgeassociates.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/High-Yield-Bonds-Toxic-or-Tasty-Oct-2008.pdf)). Bao, Pan, and Wang report that their aggregate corporate-bond illiquidity measure doubled from August 2007 to 0.60, tripled by March 2008 to 0.90, reached 1.59 in September 2008, and peaked at 3.37 in October 2008, using a 2003–2009 sample ([Bao, Pan, and Wang](https://www.mit.edu/~junpan/bond_liquidity_jf.pdf)). Cambridge Associates reports that high yield returned -8.0% in September 2008, fell another -16% through October 23, had a trailing one-year total return of -25.6%, and traded at roughly 65% of par with the Lehman HY Composite OAS above 1,450 bp and Merrill Lynch HY Master II OAS above 1,600 bp going into the last week of October 2008 ([Cambridge Associates](https://publishedresearch.cambridgeassociates.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/High-Yield-Bonds-Toxic-or-Tasty-Oct-2008.pdf)).

The 2011 euro-sovereign episode was a sovereign-bank-funding and dollar-funding stress in which up-in-quality and flight-to-quality signals mattered more than ordinary credit carry ([BIS Quarterly Review, December 2011](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1112a.pdf)). The BIS reports that euro-area sovereign news drove global markets from early September to early December 2011, that financial institutions with sovereign exposure faced deteriorating funding access, and that banks deleveraged, sold assets, and tightened credit ([BIS Quarterly Review, December 2011](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1112a.pdf)). The same BIS review reports that U.S. money-market-fund exposure to European banks fell 42% since end-May, that emerging-market fund withdrawals exceeded $25 billion in August and September, and that the cost of euro-dollar swap funding reached its highest level since December 2008 before the November 30 swap-line action ([BIS Quarterly Review, December 2011](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1112a.pdf)).

The 2015–16 commodity and energy shock was a sector-default and liquidity stress that punished high-carry energy exposure and lower-quality HY more than generic IG credit ([BIS Quarterly Review, March 2016](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1603t.htm)). The BIS reports that the BofA Merrill Lynch U.S. High Yield Index fell 4.6% in 2015, U.S. high-yield spreads exceeded 7%, and high-yield issuance fell from more than $400 billion annually in 2013–14 to $334 billion in 2015 ([BIS Quarterly Review, March 2016](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1603t.htm)). The BIS also reports that severe redemptions forced specialized high-yield funds to unload illiquid assets and that Third Avenue Focused Credit Fund stopped redemptions and planned an orderly liquidation ([BIS Quarterly Review, March 2016](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1603t.htm)).

The 2020 COVID dash-for-cash was the cleanest example of quality and low-risk leading while carry broke, because the same public dataset shows both spread widening and factor returns ([MSCI](https://www.msci.com/research-and-insights/blog-post/factors-in-focus-risk-sentiment-and-factor-dynamics-in-a-crisis)). MSCI reports that USD IG OAS widened from 107 bp at year-end 2019 to 338 bp on March 23, 2020, while low-risk and quality factor indexes outperformed the parent index by +3.28% and +1.77% in Q1 2020 ([MSCI](https://www.msci.com/research-and-insights/blog-post/factors-in-focus-risk-sentiment-and-factor-dynamics-in-a-crisis)). The Federal Reserve reports that bond mutual funds had more than $250 billion of net outflows, or about 5% of AUM, during March 2020, and that short-term IG funds had outflows of 8% of AUM ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)).

The 2022 rate-shock and UK-LDI episode shows that “quality” is not enough when duration is the main loss channel and stock-bond correlation turns positive ([Federal Reserve FEDS 2025-002](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/files/2025002pap.pdf); [J.P. Morgan Asset Management](https://am.jpmorgan.com/content/dam/jpm-am-aem/global/en/insights/portfolio-insights/fixed-income/weekly-bond-bulletin-/the-uk-is-far-from-ok.pdf)). Guggenheim reports that high-yield corporate bonds had a -14% total return and -8.2% excess return relative to matched Treasuries in the first half of 2022, while leveraged loans returned -4.4% through June 30, 2022 ([Guggenheim](https://www.guggenheimpartners.jp/getattachment/c287261c-dd07-4166-a2b5-853f14417ace/q3-2022-high-yield-bank-loan-report.pdf)). J.P. Morgan Asset Management reports that sterling IG OAS was 252 bp as of October 11, 2022, more than double a year earlier and wider than U.S. IG at 154 bp and eurozone IG at 222 bp, while UK IG credit funds saw £6.4 billion of outflows in the two weeks to October 5, 2022 ([J.P. Morgan Asset Management](https://am.jpmorgan.com/content/dam/jpm-am-aem/global/en/insights/portfolio-insights/fixed-income/weekly-bond-bulletin-/the-uk-is-far-from-ok.pdf)).

## GAP 2 — Positioning and technicals: supply, demand, and forced-selling diagnosis

### 2.1 Dealer inventory and balance-sheet constraints

The NY Fed Primary Dealer Statistics are the main free public source for primary-dealer corporate-bond inventory, and the data tool is updated on Thursdays around 4:15 p.m. with the previous week’s statistics ([NY Fed Primary Dealer Statistics](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/counterparties/primary-dealers-statistics)). The broader FR 2004 reporting framework includes the Weekly Report of Dealer Positions, cumulative dealer transactions, financing, and fails, and the NY Fed publishes corporate-security categories within the dealer statistics ([Federal Reserve FR 2004](https://www.federalreserve.gov/apps/reportingforms/Report/Index/FR_2004); [NY Fed Primary Dealer Statistics](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/counterparties/primary-dealers-statistics)). The OFR describes the same NYPD dataset as a weekly snapshot of primary-dealer positions, transactions, financing, and fails, while emphasizing that it covers primary dealers rather than the whole market ([OFR NYPD dataset](https://www.financialresearch.gov/short-term-funding-monitor/datasets/nypd/)).

Dealer inventory should be read as a **capacity-and-risk-transfer gauge**, not as an outright directional forecast ([NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr796.pdf)). Inventory building during fund outflows can mean dealers are absorbing customer selling, which may temporarily cushion spreads but can later create overhang if risk limits are hit ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)). Inventory drawdown during widening can mean dealers are unwilling or unable to warehouse bonds, which is a technical stress tell when it coincides with high TRACE volume, wider transaction costs, and fund outflows ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html); [Kargar et al.](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/frbp/assets/working-papers/2020/wp20-43.pdf)).

Post-2008 balance-sheet constraints are central to interpreting inventory because dealer intermediation capacity structurally changed after the GFC ([NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr796.pdf)). Adrian, Fleming, Shachar, and Vogt report that dealer assets peaked near $5 trillion in early 2008, contracted to $3.5 trillion in late 2008, and remained around $3.5 trillion through mid-2016 ([NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr796.pdf)). The same NY Fed staff report reports that dealer leverage peaked at 48 in Q1 2008 and fell to 25 by June 2009, and that dealer ownership of corporate bonds averaged 2.7% in 1990–2008 versus 1.2% in 2009–2016 ([NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr796.pdf)).

The liquidity evidence is not one-sided, so the desk should avoid a simplistic “regulation killed liquidity” story ([NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr796.pdf)). The NY Fed staff report finds limited evidence of broad deterioration in average liquidity measures, but it also says institutional-size trades worsened relative to pre-crisis and that executed-trade data do not capture unexecuted demand ([NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr796.pdf)). The right reading is that average conditions can look fine while tail-risk market-making capacity becomes scarce exactly when credit beta and mutual-fund redemptions rise together ([NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr796.pdf); [Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)).

March 2020 illustrates the dealer-inventory diagnostic in real time ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)). The Federal Reserve reports that liquidity provision by dealers deteriorated, some dealers reached balance-sheet capacity, IG transaction costs rose from about 30 bp in February to nearly 90 bp in mid-March, and block-trade costs rose from 24 bp in February to more than 150 bp by March 23 ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)). Kargar, Lester, Lindsay, Liu, Weill, and Zúñiga report that dealers did not absorb additional inventory during the turmoil, reduced inventory during the acute phase, and then more than doubled corporate-debt holdings after Fed credit facilities were announced ([Kargar et al.](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/frbp/assets/working-papers/2020/wp20-43.pdf)).

### 2.2 TRACE volume, breadth, and what the public tape does not show

FINRA’s TRACE Fact Book is a free public source for aggregate historical activity in OTC U.S. corporate bonds, agency debentures, ABS, and MBS, and FINRA says the Fact Book is based on aggregated data reported into TRACE ([FINRA TRACE Fact Book](https://www.finra.org/filing-reporting/trace/trace-fact-book)). The public Fact Book is useful for trading-volume, issue, participant, and transaction aggregates, but it is not a live order book and does not reveal latent demand, failed RFQs, or executable depth away from completed trades ([FINRA TRACE Fact Book](https://www.finra.org/filing-reporting/trace/trace-fact-book); [NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr796.pdf)). FINRA’s market aggregate statistics product also reports activity such as number of securities traded, dollar volume, advances and declines, and 52-week high/low information by broad corporate-bond groupings, which makes it useful for breadth but not a full dealer-depth measure ([FINRA TRACE market aggregate statistics](https://www.finra.org/sites/default/files/AppSupportDoc/p015182.pdf)).

TRACE is very powerful for stress diagnosis when paired with transaction-cost models, but many of those models require academic or commercial access to trade-level details rather than the simplest public tables ([Kargar et al.](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/frbp/assets/working-papers/2020/wp20-43.pdf)). Kargar et al. use TRACE, FISD, and FINRA aggregate data from January 2 through June 5, 2020, covering 7.4 million trades across 40,279 bonds, with 61% customer-dealer trades and 39% interdealer trades ([Kargar et al.](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/frbp/assets/working-papers/2020/wp20-43.pdf)). Their study reports that customer volume increased by about 50% from March 5 to March 23, 2020, while risky-principal transaction costs peaked above 200 bp and agency costs rose from a baseline near 8 bp to a peak near 28 bp ([Kargar et al.](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/frbp/assets/working-papers/2020/wp20-43.pdf)).

TRACE breadth tells the analyst whether widening is broad and indiscriminate or concentrated in a sector, rating cohort, or liquidity bucket ([FINRA TRACE Fact Book](https://www.finra.org/filing-reporting/trace/trace-fact-book); [Kargar et al.](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/frbp/assets/working-papers/2020/wp20-43.pdf)). Broad widening with high customer selling, high interdealer activity, falling dealer inventory, and deteriorating execution costs is more consistent with technical forced selling ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html); [Kargar et al.](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/frbp/assets/working-papers/2020/wp20-43.pdf)). Sector-specific widening with rating downgrades, default news, and persistent dispersion after liquidity normalizes is more consistent with fundamental repricing ([BIS Quarterly Review, March 2016](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1603t.htm)).

The public TRACE limitation is important because executed trades are the visible part of the liquidity iceberg ([NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr796.pdf)). The NY Fed’s market-liquidity study notes that executed-trade data do not capture unexecuted demand, which means stress can be understated if investors simply cannot find bids ([NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr796.pdf)). A desk should therefore treat TRACE as a breadth-and-cost diagnostic rather than a sufficient measure of market depth ([FINRA TRACE Fact Book](https://www.finra.org/filing-reporting/trace/trace-fact-book); [NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr796.pdf)).

The 2020 experience shows why TRACE needs to be combined with policy and inventory data ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)). The Federal Reserve reports that liquidity improved after the March 23 announcement of corporate-credit facilities even though the SMCCF did not begin ETF purchases until May 12 and did not begin bond purchases until June 16 ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)). This timing supports the interpretation that part of the March widening was a market-functioning and risk-transfer problem rather than a pure revision to expected default losses ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)).

### 2.3 Fund flows, CDX options, and primary supply

ICI is the main free public U.S. fund-flow source, and its weekly estimates are derived from data covering 98% of industry assets ([ICI weekly flows](https://www.ici.org/research/stats/flows)). ICI’s weekly page provides estimates for total bond, taxable bond, and municipal bond funds, while the page notes that additional taxable categories are available online, including investment grade, high yield, government, multisector, and global bond funds ([ICI weekly flows](https://www.ici.org/research/stats/flows)). The correct use is directional: persistent outflows from credit funds add price-insensitive supply, and inflows add price-insensitive demand, but flows are not a standalone valuation signal ([ICI weekly flows](https://www.ici.org/research/stats/flows); [Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)).

EPFR and Lipper are widely referenced in weekly market commentary, but a complete free public historical IG/HY fund-flow database was not found in the reviewed public sources ([EPFR](https://epfr.com); [Lipper Alpha Insight](https://lipperalpha.refinitiv.com/2024/05/u-s-weekly-fundflows-insight-report-fund-market-reports-first-outflow-in-six-weeks/)). Lipper Alpha Insight publishes episodic weekly U.S. fund-flow articles that use a Thursday-to-Wednesday reporting window, but that is not the same as a complete free API or historical file for desk backtesting ([Lipper Alpha Insight](https://lipperalpha.refinitiv.com/2024/05/u-s-weekly-fundflows-insight-report-fund-market-reports-first-outflow-in-six-weeks/)). The honest conclusion is that ICI is the best public free baseline, while EPFR and Lipper are better treated as commercial or article-based sources unless the user has licensed access ([ICI weekly flows](https://www.ici.org/research/stats/flows); [EPFR](https://epfr.com); [Lipper Alpha Insight](https://lipperalpha.refinitiv.com/2024/05/u-s-weekly-fundflows-insight-report-fund-market-reports-first-outflow-in-six-weeks/)).

Credit-index options and CDX swaptions can reveal hedging pressure through payer demand and skew, but complete public live positioning and skew series are not available in the reviewed sources ([Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle](https://phd-finance.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:bc0971f7-a73f-475f-99d6-e73f9bc7f7f0/FS_spring21_paper_collin-dufresne.pdf)). Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle study CDX North American IG options and report that CDX implied-volatility smiles are consistently positively skewed, while SPX smiles are negatively skewed, because both price bad states in which spreads are high and equities are low ([Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle](https://phd-finance.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:bc0971f7-a73f-475f-99d6-e73f9bc7f7f0/FS_spring21_paper_collin-dufresne.pdf)). Their trade sample from December 31, 2012 through April 30, 2020 shows an estimated average daily CDX-option volume of $4.35 billion, a median trade size of $100 million, a payer share of 63.1%, a five-year-tenor share of 98.1%, and an on-the-run share of 94.0% ([Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle](https://phd-finance.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:bc0971f7-a73f-475f-99d6-e73f9bc7f7f0/FS_spring21_paper_collin-dufresne.pdf)).

Primary issuance is the cleanest public supply technical, and SIFMA publishes U.S. corporate-bond statistics covering issuance, trading, and outstanding bonds with IG/HY and other breakouts ([SIFMA corporate-bond statistics](https://www.sifma.org/research/statistics/us-corporate-bonds-statistics)). SIFMA reports that U.S. corporate-bond issuance was $1,226.8 billion year-to-date through May 2026, up 21.1% year over year, that trading averaged $69.6 billion per day, up 13.1%, and that outstanding bonds were $11.5 trillion as of 4Q25, up 3.5% ([SIFMA corporate-bond statistics](https://www.sifma.org/research/statistics/us-corporate-bonds-statistics)). Heavy primary supply can cheapen secondary spreads through concessions and dealer balance-sheet usage, while a closed primary market can signal stress even if new supply temporarily falls ([SIFMA corporate-bond statistics](https://www.sifma.org/research/statistics/us-corporate-bonds-statistics); [BIS Annual Economic Report 2020](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2020e.pdf)).

BIS evidence from 2020 shows how supply, funding, and liquidity can shut at the same time ([BIS Annual Economic Report 2020](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2020e.pdf)). The BIS reports that corporate funding froze in the first half of March 2020, the high-yield bond market was effectively shut from late February to March, leveraged-loan issuance fell below the 2019 average, and CLO issuance halted ([BIS Annual Economic Report 2020](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2020e.pdf)). That combination is a stronger forced-selling and market-functioning signal than any single spread level because it shows that cash credit, loans, CLOs, and funding markets were all impaired ([BIS Annual Economic Report 2020](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2020e.pdf)).

### 2.4 Technical-versus-fundamental diagnostic framework

A technical widening should be suspected when spreads widen rapidly while fund outflows, TRACE volume, execution costs, and dealer-balance-sheet stress all deteriorate at the same time ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html); [Kargar et al.](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/frbp/assets/working-papers/2020/wp20-43.pdf)). In March 2020, the Federal Reserve reports more than $250 billion of bond mutual-fund outflows, transaction-cost deterioration, dealer balance-sheet constraints, and rapid improvement after the corporate-credit-facility announcements, which is a textbook technical-liquidity signature ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)). Kargar et al. report that risky-principal costs rose above 200 bp and that the agency-trade share increased by as much as 15 percentage points during the March 5–23 stress window, which signals deterioration in dealer willingness to take principal risk ([Kargar et al.](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/frbp/assets/working-papers/2020/wp20-43.pdf)).

A fundamental widening should be suspected when sector dispersion, downgrades, default expectations, earnings weakness, leverage, refinancing risk, and rating migration remain the dominant explanatory variables after liquidity normalizes ([BIS Quarterly Review, March 2016](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1603t.htm); [Greenwood and Hanson](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w17197/w17197.pdf)). The 2015–16 energy episode fits this better than the March 2020 dash-for-cash because the BIS identifies energy-sector turmoil, defaults, and energy-index underperformance as central features ([BIS Quarterly Review, March 2016](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1603t.htm)). Greenwood and Hanson’s evidence also supports a fundamental-cycle lens because deteriorating issuer quality forecasts future high-yield returns, BBB spread changes, and default rates over multi-year horizons ([Greenwood and Hanson](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w17197/w17197.pdf)).

The two channels often co-exist, so the desk should classify the **marginal driver** rather than force a binary answer ([Bao, Pan, and Wang](https://www.mit.edu/~junpan/bond_liquidity_jf.pdf); [BIS Quarterly Review, March 2016](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1603t.htm)). Bao, Pan, and Wang report that illiquidity dominated high-rated corporate-spread changes during 2008, while credit risk was more important for junk bonds, which means the same calendar episode can be technical in IG and fundamental in HY ([Bao, Pan, and Wang](https://www.mit.edu/~junpan/bond_liquidity_jf.pdf)). This cohort-specific diagnosis is critical because IG desks often need to decide whether to buy liquidity premia, while HY desks need to decide whether defaults are being underpriced ([Bao, Pan, and Wang](https://www.mit.edu/~junpan/bond_liquidity_jf.pdf); [Cambridge Associates](https://publishedresearch.cambridgeassociates.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/12/High-Yield-Bonds-Toxic-or-Tasty-Oct-2008.pdf)).

Policy-announcement boundary effects are the strongest public tell that technicals were material ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)). The Federal Reserve reports that bonds just under the SMCCF five-year maturity eligibility boundary had an extra transaction-cost decline of 21 bp in the week after the SMCCF announcement, 15 bp in the second week, and 9 bp in the third week, even before the facility was fully active ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)). Kargar et al. similarly find that SMCCF eligibility reduced risky-principal transaction costs by roughly 50 bp, with a Table 4 interaction estimate of -57.70 bp for SMCCF eligibility ([Kargar et al.](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/frbp/assets/working-papers/2020/wp20-43.pdf)).

The 2022 UK-LDI episode is a non-U.S. but highly relevant technical example because collateral calls forced asset sales after a gilt-yield shock ([J.P. Morgan Asset Management](https://am.jpmorgan.com/content/dam/jpm-am-aem/global/en/insights/portfolio-insights/fixed-income/weekly-bond-bulletin-/the-uk-is-far-from-ok.pdf)). J.P. Morgan Asset Management reports that the UK mini-budget contributed to falling long-gilt prices and collateral calls on leveraged LDI strategies, forcing pension funds to sell assets before Bank of England intervention ([J.P. Morgan Asset Management](https://am.jpmorgan.com/content/dam/jpm-am-aem/global/en/insights/portfolio-insights/fixed-income/weekly-bond-bulletin-/the-uk-is-far-from-ok.pdf)). The same note reports that listed index spreads understated actual sterling credit spreads by 15–25 bp during poor liquidity, which is a warning that index-based credit marks can lag real execution levels in forced-selling regimes ([J.P. Morgan Asset Management](https://am.jpmorgan.com/content/dam/jpm-am-aem/global/en/insights/portfolio-insights/fixed-income/weekly-bond-bulletin-/the-uk-is-far-from-ok.pdf)).

## GAP 3 — Cross-asset and regime correlations

### 3.1 Credit versus equities, volatility, rates, dollar, and stock-bond correlation

The most stable cross-asset relationship is that credit spreads behave like a leveraged equity-volatility exposure in risk-off regimes, and public evidence shows the relation strengthens when VIX is already high ([McAlley and Soper](https://jbes.scholasticahq.com/article/146576-investigating-the-vix-index-relationship-with-high-yield-investment-grade-bond-spreads-exploring-structural-breaks-threshold-effects.pdf); [Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle](https://phd-finance.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:bc0971f7-a73f-475f-99d6-e73f9bc7f7f0/FS_spring21_paper_collin-dufresne.pdf)). McAlley and Soper use Bloomberg and FRED data from November 1, 2006 through April 27, 2023 and find that log changes in VIX are positively related to changes in both IG and HY spreads, with a stronger relation in HY ([McAlley and Soper](https://jbes.scholasticahq.com/article/146576-investigating-the-vix-index-relationship-with-high-yield-investment-grade-bond-spreads-exploring-structural-breaks-threshold-effects.pdf)). Their base OLS coefficients are 0.073 for IG spread changes and 0.587 for HY spread changes, and their elevated-VIX threshold coefficients rise to 0.140 for IG and 0.901 for HY ([McAlley and Soper](https://jbes.scholasticahq.com/article/146576-investigating-the-vix-index-relationship-with-high-yield-investment-grade-bond-spreads-exploring-structural-breaks-threshold-effects.pdf)).

Credit-index option markets reinforce the same relationship from a derivatives angle ([Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle](https://phd-finance.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:bc0971f7-a73f-475f-99d6-e73f9bc7f7f0/FS_spring21_paper_collin-dufresne.pdf)). Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle report a weekly correlation of -0.802 between CDX spread changes and SPX returns, a correlation of 0.749 between CDX implied volatility and SPX implied volatility, and a correlation of -0.368 between CDX skewness and SPX skewness in their sample ([Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle](https://phd-finance.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:bc0971f7-a73f-475f-99d6-e73f9bc7f7f0/FS_spring21_paper_collin-dufresne.pdf)). Their interpretation is that both credit and equity option markets price bad states in which spreads are high and equities are low, even though CDX skew is positive and SPX skew is negative because the payoff directions differ ([Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle](https://phd-finance.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:bc0971f7-a73f-475f-99d6-e73f9bc7f7f0/FS_spring21_paper_collin-dufresne.pdf)).

The spread-rate relationship is regime-dependent and can invert depending on whether higher yields reflect growth or inflation/term-premium stress ([Duffee](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/treasury-yields-and-corporate-bond-yield-spreads-an-empirical-analysis.htm); [Federal Reserve FEDS 2025-002](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/files/2025002pap.pdf)). Duffee’s classic noncallable IG study finds that corporate spreads tend to fall when the Treasury term-structure level rises, with a small decline for Aaa bonds and a larger decline for Baa bonds ([Duffee](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/treasury-yields-and-corporate-bond-yield-spreads-an-empirical-analysis.htm)). The 2022 regime shows the limit of that historical relation because the inflation shock created a positive stock-bond correlation and caused rates and risky spreads to hurt portfolios at the same time ([Federal Reserve FEDS 2025-002](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/files/2025002pap.pdf); [Guggenheim](https://www.guggenheimpartners.jp/getattachment/c287261c-dd07-4166-a2b5-853f14417ace/q3-2022-high-yield-bank-loan-report.pdf)).

The stock-bond correlation sign is a first-order regime variable for credit because it determines whether Treasuries hedge spread widening or amplify total-return drawdowns ([Federal Reserve FEDS 2025-002](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/files/2025002pap.pdf)). Bonelli, Palazzo, and Yamarthy report that the daily three-month bond-stock return correlation averaged -0.30 in their August 2004–October 2023 sample, but rose to roughly 0.50 at end-May 2021 and December 2022 ([Federal Reserve FEDS 2025-002](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/files/2025002pap.pdf)). They describe the post-2000 period as generally a “good inflation” regime with negative stock-bond correlation, while the post-2022 positive correlation reached levels comparable to 1970s stagflation episodes ([Federal Reserve FEDS 2025-002](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/files/2025002pap.pdf)).

The dollar-spread relationship is strongest for EM and dollar-funding stress, but BIS evidence shows it is not globally invariant ([BIS Bulletin 79](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull79.htm)). BIS Bulletin 79 reports that the historically positive correlation between U.S.-dollar strength against EME currencies and EME sovereign spreads over U.S. Treasuries continued in Latin America but reversed in emerging Asia in 2022–23 ([BIS Bulletin 79](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull79.htm)). The BIS attributes that divergence to different EME central-bank policy responses, with emerging Asia using more FX intervention and less monetary tightening than Latin America ([BIS Bulletin 79](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull79.htm)).

Dollar funding stress can transmit to U.S. credit even when the original shock is outside U.S. corporates ([BIS Annual Economic Report 2020](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2020e.pdf); [BIS Quarterly Review, December 2011](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1112a.pdf)). The BIS reports that the March 2020 flight to safety became a scramble for cash, that U.S. prime money funds had withdrawals of $160 billion by end-March, or about 15% of AUM, and that non-U.S. banks had gross dollar liabilities of $10 trillion at end-2019 ([BIS Annual Economic Report 2020](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2020e.pdf)). The BIS also reports that banks and nonbanks outside the United States had more than $22 trillion in dollar liabilities and up to $40 trillion in FX-swap obligations, which explains why dollar liquidity shocks can spill across EM sovereigns, leveraged loans, and U.S. corporate credit ([BIS Annual Economic Report 2020](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2020e.pdf)).

### 3.3 When correlations should not be trusted

The VIX-spread relationship should not be trusted as a linear rule when VIX is low, when policy puts are active, or when the credit move is sector-specific rather than systemic ([McAlley and Soper](https://jbes.scholasticahq.com/article/146576-investigating-the-vix-index-relationship-with-high-yield-investment-grade-bond-spreads-exploring-structural-breaks-threshold-effects.pdf); [Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)). McAlley and Soper explicitly find threshold effects and structural breaks, including stronger spread sensitivity at elevated VIX and different coefficient regimes across 2006–2023 ([McAlley and Soper](https://jbes.scholasticahq.com/article/146576-investigating-the-vix-index-relationship-with-high-yield-investment-grade-bond-spreads-exploring-structural-breaks-threshold-effects.pdf)). The desk implication is that a low-VIX carry trade can look stable until the relationship becomes convex, while a post-backstop regime can tighten credit even before macro volatility fully normalizes ([McAlley and Soper](https://jbes.scholasticahq.com/article/146576-investigating-the-vix-index-relationship-with-high-yield-investment-grade-bond-spreads-exploring-structural-breaks-threshold-effects.pdf); [Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)).

The spread-rate relationship should not be trusted when inflation, fiscal credibility, or term-premium shocks dominate growth news ([Duffee](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/treasury-yields-and-corporate-bond-yield-spreads-an-empirical-analysis.htm); [Federal Reserve FEDS 2025-002](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/files/2025002pap.pdf)). Duffee’s historical finding that spreads often fall when Treasury yields rise is most plausible in growth-positive regimes, while the FEDS 2025 evidence shows that bad-inflation regimes can make stocks and bonds sell off together ([Duffee](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/treasury-yields-and-corporate-bond-yield-spreads-an-empirical-analysis.htm); [Federal Reserve FEDS 2025-002](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/files/2025002pap.pdf)). The practical rule is to treat Treasury duration as a hedge only after confirming whether the shock is growth, disinflation, inflation, or fiscal credibility ([Federal Reserve FEDS 2025-002](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/files/2025002pap.pdf)).

The dollar-EM spread relationship should not be trusted without regional policy context ([BIS Bulletin 79](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull79.htm)). BIS Bulletin 79 reports that the historically positive U.S.-dollar/EME-sovereign-spread relationship continued in Latin America but reversed in emerging Asia during 2022–23 because policy responses differed ([BIS Bulletin 79](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull79.htm)). The practical rule is to separate dollar funding stress, terms-of-trade shocks, FX intervention, and domestic-rate reaction functions before applying a generic “strong dollar bad for EM” template ([BIS Bulletin 79](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull79.htm); [BIS Annual Economic Report 2020](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2020e.pdf)).

The loan-versus-bond duration relationship should not be trusted as a credit-quality statement ([Guggenheim](https://www.guggenheimpartners.jp/getattachment/c287261c-dd07-4166-a2b5-853f14417ace/q3-2022-high-yield-bank-loan-report.pdf); [BIS Annual Economic Report 2020](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2020e.pdf)). Guggenheim’s 1H 2022 figures show loans outperforming HY bonds on total return because of lower rate duration, but the BIS 2020 evidence shows that loan issuance and CLO issuance can halt in a funding shock ([Guggenheim](https://www.guggenheimpartners.jp/getattachment/c287261c-dd07-4166-a2b5-853f14417ace/q3-2022-high-yield-bank-loan-report.pdf); [BIS Annual Economic Report 2020](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2020e.pdf)). The practical rule is that loans are lower-duration credit, not low-risk credit ([Guggenheim](https://www.guggenheimpartners.jp/getattachment/c287261c-dd07-4166-a2b5-853f14417ace/q3-2022-high-yield-bank-loan-report.pdf)).

The index-option signal should not be trusted as a complete positioning measure because public CDX-option data are incomplete, capped, and do not reveal all trader identities or proprietary quote surfaces ([Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle](https://phd-finance.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:bc0971f7-a73f-475f-99d6-e73f9bc7f7f0/FS_spring21_paper_collin-dufresne.pdf)). Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle note that transaction reporting covers trades when at least one U.S. counterparty is involved and that true market activity is larger than the public reporting sample ([Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle](https://phd-finance.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:bc0971f7-a73f-475f-99d6-e73f9bc7f7f0/FS_spring21_paper_collin-dufresne.pdf)). The practical rule is to use CDX skew and payer demand as hedging-pressure evidence when available, but not as a standalone current-positioning figure unless a licensed source provides the live series ([Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle](https://phd-finance.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:bc0971f7-a73f-475f-99d6-e73f9bc7f7f0/FS_spring21_paper_collin-dufresne.pdf)).

## Analyst-ready decoder cards

### Decoder card 1 — DTS math

DTS equals spread duration multiplied by spread, and it measures exposure to relative spread moves rather than absolute spread moves ([Robeco DTS note](https://assets.ctfassets.net/tl4x668xzide/4Aapx0GUWLqc0ow8jlMAMb/0a213610fc9f13098d30308c88558723/201911-measuring-credit-risk-with-dts-hk-sg.pdf)). A one-year bond with a 500 bp spread and a five-year bond with a 100 bp spread both have DTS of 500, which makes them comparable risk units under a relative-spread framework ([Robeco DTS note](https://assets.ctfassets.net/tl4x668xzide/4Aapx0GUWLqc0ow8jlMAMb/0a213610fc9f13098d30308c88558723/201911-measuring-credit-risk-with-dts-hk-sg.pdf)). The desk use is to cap portfolio concentration by DTS contribution rather than by market value alone, especially when spread levels are already high ([Robeco DTS note](https://assets.ctfassets.net/tl4x668xzide/4Aapx0GUWLqc0ow8jlMAMb/0a213610fc9f13098d30308c88558723/201911-measuring-credit-risk-with-dts-hk-sg.pdf)).

### Decoder card 2 — Carry is not automatically alpha

Carry is attractive in calm markets because it pays spread and roll-down if conditions are unchanged, but Robeco finds that carry’s apparent return is largely explained by market, value, and risk exposures in its January 1994–June 2016 sample ([Robeco](https://www.robeco.com/nl-be/visie/2017/06/does-carry-add-value-to-existing-credit-factors)). In stress, MSCI’s Q1 2020 result shows that high-OAS carry had the largest factor drawdown while low-risk and quality outperformed ([MSCI](https://www.msci.com/research-and-insights/blog-post/factors-in-focus-risk-sentiment-and-factor-dynamics-in-a-crisis)). The desk use is to approve carry only after separating expected loss, DTS, liquidity, downgrade, and sector concentration ([Robeco](https://www.robeco.com/nl-be/visie/2017/06/does-carry-add-value-to-existing-credit-factors); [Robeco DTS note](https://assets.ctfassets.net/tl4x668xzide/4Aapx0GUWLqc0ow8jlMAMb/0a213610fc9f13098d30308c88558723/201911-measuring-credit-risk-with-dts-hk-sg.pdf)).

### Decoder card 3 — Technical versus fundamental spread widening

A technical widening is most likely when outflows, TRACE activity, transaction costs, dealer inventory stress, and policy-eligibility effects line up ([Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html); [Kargar et al.](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/frbp/assets/working-papers/2020/wp20-43.pdf)). A fundamental widening is most likely when downgrades, sector earnings, refinancing risk, defaults, and issuer-quality deterioration dominate and persist after liquidity improves ([BIS Quarterly Review, March 2016](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt1603t.htm); [Greenwood and Hanson](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w17197/w17197.pdf)). The desk use is to classify the marginal driver by cohort, because Bao, Pan, and Wang find that 2008 illiquidity explained more high-rated spread changes while credit risk mattered more for junk ([Bao, Pan, and Wang](https://www.mit.edu/~junpan/bond_liquidity_jf.pdf)).

### Decoder card 4 — Correlation trust checklist

A credit-equity correlation signal is more reliable when VIX is elevated and moves are broad, but McAlley and Soper’s structural-break evidence says the coefficient is state-dependent ([McAlley and Soper](https://jbes.scholasticahq.com/article/146576-investigating-the-vix-index-relationship-with-high-yield-investment-grade-bond-spreads-exploring-structural-breaks-threshold-effects.pdf)). A rate-credit correlation signal is more reliable in growth shocks than in bad-inflation shocks, because stock-bond correlation can turn positive and remove the Treasury hedge ([Federal Reserve FEDS 2025-002](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/files/2025002pap.pdf)). A dollar-EM signal is more reliable when it reflects funding stress than when regional policy responses offset the dollar move, as BIS Bulletin 79 shows for emerging Asia in 2022–23 ([BIS Bulletin 79](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull79.htm)).

## Validation notes and public-data gaps

No complete free public live factor dashboard was found for U.S. credit carry, value, quality, momentum, DTS, and crowding, so current factor readings should be sourced from licensed providers or recreated from holdings-level data rather than inferred from index OAS alone ([Invesco](https://www.invesco.com/content/dam/invesco/apac/en/pdf/insights/2024/september/inveco-fixed-income-factors-theory-and-practice-sep24.pdf); [MSCI](https://www.msci.com/research-and-insights/blog-post/factors-in-focus-risk-sentiment-and-factor-dynamics-in-a-crisis); [Robeco DTS note](https://assets.ctfassets.net/tl4x668xzide/4Aapx0GUWLqc0ow8jlMAMb/0a213610fc9f13098d30308c88558723/201911-measuring-credit-risk-with-dts-hk-sg.pdf)). No complete free public CDX-option positioning or skew time series was found, and the best public evidence is historical academic work based on Markit quotes and SDR trade reporting rather than a live free dashboard ([Collin-Dufresne, Junge, and Trolle](https://phd-finance.uzh.ch/dam/jcr:bc0971f7-a73f-475f-99d6-e73f9bc7f7f0/FS_spring21_paper_collin-dufresne.pdf)). No complete free EPFR or Lipper historical IG/HY flow database was found, while ICI provides the strongest free public weekly U.S. fund-flow baseline and notes that its estimates cover 98% of industry assets ([ICI weekly flows](https://www.ici.org/research/stats/flows); [EPFR](https://epfr.com); [Lipper Alpha Insight](https://lipperalpha.refinitiv.com/2024/05/u-s-weekly-fundflows-insight-report-fund-market-reports-first-outflow-in-six-weeks/)). TRACE public aggregates are useful for volume and breadth, but they do not provide a full order book or unexecuted demand, so stress diagnostics should combine TRACE with dealer positions, fund flows, transaction-cost studies, and policy-event evidence ([FINRA TRACE Fact Book](https://www.finra.org/filing-reporting/trace/trace-fact-book); [NY Fed Staff Report 796](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr796.pdf); [Federal Reserve FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-corporate-bond-market-crises-and-the-government-response-20201007.html)).
