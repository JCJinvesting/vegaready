## 7 · LAYMAN KIT

[DIR | Confidence: High] Dollar smile analogy: imagine the dollar as an umbrella that sells well in both storms and heatwaves, but not in mild weather. [HARD | Confidence: High] In storms, BIS-style dollar funding demand rises because the dollar is on one side of 89.2% of FX turnover and is the core funding currency; in heatwaves, high U.S. growth and positive real yields can also support it ([BIS Triennial FX](https://www.bis.org/statistics/rpfx25_fx.pdf); [FRED DFII10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFII10)). Worked example: if war risk lifts margin demand and U.S. real yields stay at 2.16%, the dollar can rally even as U.S. deficits look unattractive. Top misconceptions: the dollar only rises when the U.S. economy is strong; a strong dollar always means gold must fall; DXY is the only valid dollar measure.

[DIR | Confidence: High] Real versus nominal rates analogy: nominal rates are the sticker price of money, while real rates are the sticker price after subtracting inflation. [HARD | Confidence: High] `DGS10` was 4.45% on 2026-06-11 and `DFII10` was 2.16% on 2026-06-11, so the market-implied inflation component is roughly the difference between the nominal and real Treasury yield series ([FRED DGS10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DGS10); [FRED DFII10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFII10)). Worked example: a currency can rise if nominal rates rise because real returns rise, but it can fall if nominal rates rise only because inflation risk worsens. Top misconceptions: high nominal rates always support FX; real yields only matter for bonds; inflation shocks are always gold-positive regardless of central-bank reaction.

[DIR | Confidence: High] Carry trade analogy: borrowing low-yielding currency to buy high-yielding currency is like picking up rent every day while hoping the house does not burn down. [HARD | Confidence: High] USD/JPY at 160.26 shows the yen still reflects carry pressure despite haven status, while `DFII10` at 2.16% shows why U.S. real yield remains attractive ([FRED DEXJPUS](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DEXJPUS); [FRED DFII10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFII10)). Worked example: if risk-off forces levered investors to close yen-funded trades, yen can jump even when U.S. yields are higher. Top misconceptions: carry always wins slowly; yen is always a haven regardless of entry level; EM high yield is safe if spot has been stable.

[DIR | Confidence: High] Cross-currency basis analogy: the basis is the surcharge for renting dollars with another currency as collateral. [HARD | Confidence: High] BIS explains that a negative basis means borrowing dollars via FX swaps is more expensive than direct dollar cash funding, and March-2020 euro/yen dollar premia reached 85bp and 150bp over USD LIBOR at the squeeze peak ([BIS Bulletin 15](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull15.pdf)). Worked example: a European bank with euro collateral may have to pay extra to swap into dollars if every bank wants dollars simultaneously. Top misconceptions: spot FX shows all funding stress; central-bank swap lines eliminate all basis; a negative basis is always arbitrageable.

[DIR | Confidence: High] Why gold rises analogy: gold is a fireproof safe that pays no rent but survives legal, banking, and currency fires. [HARD | Confidence: High] WGC’s Q1-2026 244t central-bank buying and the 2.16% real 10-year yield together prove that this gold rally is not explained by the old “real yields down, gold up” rule alone ([WGC Q1 2026 central banks](https://www.gold.org/goldhub/research/gold-demand-trends/gold-demand-trends-q1-2026/central-banks); [FRED DFII10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFII10)). Worked example: a reserve manager may buy gold even when TIPS yields are positive because gold has no issuer and no sanctionable cash-flow rail. Top misconceptions: gold is only an inflation hedge; gold must fall when real yields rise; ETF flows are the whole market.

[DIR | Confidence: High] Central-bank gold buying and de-dollarization analogy: reserve managers are not burning down the dollar house; they are adding another fire exit. [HARD | Confidence: High] IMF COFER shows USD reserves at 56.77% in 2025Q4 and WGC shows official gold buying above prior quarter and five-year average in Q1-2026 ([IMF COFER March 2026](https://data.imf.org/en/news/imf%20data%20brief%20march%2027); [WGC Q1 2026 central banks](https://www.gold.org/goldhub/research/gold-demand-trends/gold-demand-trends-q1-2026/central-banks)). Worked example: a central bank can keep most reserves in dollars for liquidity while buying gold at the margin for geopolitical robustness. Top misconceptions: de-dollarization means immediate USD crash; gold buying means no one wants Treasuries; all central banks have the same reserve motives.

[DIR | Confidence: High] Reserve-currency status analogy: the dollar is the operating system of global finance; replacing it requires apps, users, legal rails, liquidity, collateral, and crisis backstops. [HARD | Confidence: High] BIS reports USD was on one side of 89.2% of FX trades in April 2025, while IMF still puts the USD reserve share above half of global FX reserves at 56.77% in 2025Q4 ([BIS Triennial FX](https://www.bis.org/statistics/rpfx25_fx.pdf); [IMF COFER March 2026](https://data.imf.org/en/news/imf%20data%20brief%20march%2027)). Worked example: even a central bank that buys gold may still need dollars for intervention, trade settlement, and collateral liquidity. Top misconceptions: reserve currency status is binary; the euro or RMB can instantly replace the dollar; sanctions automatically destroy dollar demand.
