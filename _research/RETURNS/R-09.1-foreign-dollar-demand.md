## §1 — Sovereign Dollar-Response Taxonomy

The organizing question for this entire dossier: **when a foreign sovereign faces a strong dollar, high US real rates, or a geopolitical dollar-funding shock, which responses force more US Treasury (UST) selling, and which responses substitute for it?** Eight mechanisms exist, and the policymaker's choice among them is the behavioral layer below the TIC stock data.

### Mechanism-by-mechanism map with quantified math

**(a) FX intervention via UST sales (spot).** The central bank sells dollars from reserves — usually liquidating USTs or dollar deposits — to slow currency depreciation. This is the canonical *forced-seller* channel and the cleanest UST-demand reducer at the margin.

[HARD-H] Japan's verified 2022 MOF operations: ¥2.8382tn on 22 Sep 2022, ¥5.6202tn on 21 Oct 2022, ¥0.7296tn on 24 Oct 2022 — ~$19–20bn (Sep) and ~$42–43bn (Oct) at contemporaneous USD/JPY ([MOF intervention CSV](https://www.mof.go.jp/english/policy/international_policy/reference/feio/foreign_exchange_intervention_operations.csv)). [HARD-H] Japan's Apr–May 2024 operations: ¥5.9185tn (29 Apr) + ¥3.8700tn (1 May) = ¥9.7885tn ≈ **$62bn** at USD/JPY ~157 ([MOF April–June 2024 release](https://www.mof.go.jp/english/policy/international_policy/reference/feio/quarter/2024_2Qe.html)). [HARD-M] MOF's monthly page for 28 Apr–27 May 2026 shows **¥11.7349tn** of operations, direction not disclosed in primary CSV; at ¥155/$ that's ≈ $75.7bn IF dollar-selling, flagged as provisional ([MOF monthly release](https://www.mof.go.jp/english/policy/international_policy/reference/feio/monthly/20260529e.html)).

[HARD-H] India is the live 2026 exemplar of mechanism (a) at scale: RBI net spot dollar sales of **$53.13bn in FY26** — the largest in 15+ years, against a ~9.5% rupee fall ([Business Line, 27 May 2026](https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/data-stories/data-focus/rbis-forex-intervention-in-spot-and-forward-market-in-fy26-highest-in-a-decade/article71029062.ece)). [HARD-H] Reserves fell from all-time-high $728.49bn (27 Feb 2026) to $682.3bn (29 May 2026), a ~$46bn drawdown ([RBI Governor Malhotra, 5 Jun 2026, via Upstox](https://upstox.com/news/market-news/latest-updates/forex-reserve-at-healthy-682-bn-adequate-to-provide-import-cover-for-11-months-rbi-guv/article-194879/)).

**DV01 framework (GPT-5.5):** [HARD-H] At a 10y Treasury yield of 4.45% (H.15, 11 Jun 2026) and par-ish 10y duration of 8.1, DV01 is ≈ $0.81mn per $1bn per bp ([Fed H.15](https://www.federalreserve.gov/releases/h15/)). A $50bn 10y-equivalent sale ≈ **$40.5mn/bp** of risk dealers/end-investors must absorb. If street clearing elasticity is ~1bp per $7.5bn 10y-equivalent, $50bn ≈ **6–7bp mechanical term-premium pressure** before signaling.

[HARD-H] Mar-2026 TIC: Japan's holdings fell **$47.7bn m/m** to $1.1916tn; China's fell **$41.0bn m/m** to $652.3bn ([TIC MFH](https://www.treasury.gov/resource-center/data-chart-center/tic/Documents/slt_table5.html)). The combined March drop of $88.7bn from Japan+China alone equals **~$72mn/bp DV01** dropped on the market in a single month. **UST sign: reducer / bear-steepener.**

**(b) Reserve accumulation/drawdown.** The cycle-aggregate of (a). Drawdowns hit liquid instruments (bills) first, then notes; accumulation arrives as bills, agencies, deposits, and notes. [HARD-H] Mar-2026 TIC: foreign residents decreased UST bill holdings by $16.8bn while all dollar-denominated short-term securities + custody liabilities rose $1.3bn ([Treasury March 2026 TIC release](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0499)). [HARD-H] COFER methodology now covers 100% of world FX reserves (147 reporters) after the 2025Q3 elimination of the unallocated portion ([IMF COFER](https://data.imf.org/en?sk=e6a5f467-c14b-4aa8-9f6d-5a09ec4e62a4)). [HARD-M] USD reserve share ≈ 56.8–56.9% in Q3/Q4 2025 (per Brookings/Macrobond secondary summaries; exact IMF numeric table not free-feed-exposed — **flagged**). **UST sign: drawdown = reducer; accumulation = amplifier.**

**(c) Policy-rate defense (hiking to defend the currency).** Raise domestic rates to widen the carry buffer and stem outflows. Avoids UST selling but at the cost of domestic growth. [HARD-H] Turkey took the policy rate from **8.5% to 45%** by Feb 2024 then to **50%** on 21 Mar 2024 — +4,150bp in under a year ([TCMB Mar-2024 decision](https://www.tcmb.gov.tr/wps/wcm/connect/en/tcmb+en/main+menu/announcements/press+releases/2024/ano2024-14)). [HARD-H] Brazil Selic to **13.75%** Aug-2022 ([BCB minutes 03082022](https://www.bcb.gov.br/en/publications/copomminutes/03082022)). [HARD-H] India MPC repo to **6.50%** Feb-2023 (+250bp from May-2022) ([RBI Bulletin](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/BS_ViewBulletin.aspx?Id=21725)).

**India 2026 is the negative-space case** — the MPC HELD repo at 5.25% on 5 Jun 2026 for a third consecutive meeting (neutral stance, FY27 GDP cut to 6.6% even as CPI revised up to 5.1%) ([Way2Wealth MPC update, 5 Jun 2026](https://www.way2wealth.com/Reports/RR05062026db7dd.pdf)). RBI explicitly *declined* mechanism (c) and is pivoting to (d) instead. **UST sign: neutral-to-bullish (no UST sale required); narrows own spread vs US.**

**(d) Diaspora-deposit/diaspora-bond schemes.** The heart of this dossier (§3). [DIR-H] The state pulls *private non-resident dollars* into the domestic banking system; the central bank absorbs them via the swap (taking the dollars on its balance sheet, often re-invested in USTs), and the bank deploys rupees domestically. Mechanism (d) **adds** to the dollar pool instead of **subtracting** from official reserves. **UST sign: reducer of forced selling at 1:1 first order; partly amplifier if RBI parks the absorbed dollars in USTs.**

**(e) FIMA repo + central-bank swap lines.** The Fed's institutional substitute for (a). [HARD-H] FIMA lets approved foreign monetary authorities raise dollars temporarily by selling USTs to the Fed's SOMA with a buyback commitment — explicitly described as "an alternative to open-market Treasury sales" ([Fed FIMA FAQ](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fima-repo-facility-faqs.htm)). Overnight pricing tied to SRF minimum bid rate; 7-day at weekly OIS+25bp.

[HARD-H] Current use on 10 Jun 2026: central-bank liquidity swaps **$28mn**, foreign-official repo **$1mn** ([Fed H.4.1](https://www.federalreserve.gov/releases/h41/current/)). Historical: swaps peaked at **~$450bn** late May 2020 (BoJ $223bn, ECB $145bn) ([Fed BSD developments](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/bsd-recent-developments-202008.htm)). FIMA repo outstanding through COVID never exceeded $1.0bn in any quarterly snapshot ([Fed FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/accounting-for-covid-related-funding-credit-liquidity-and-loan-facilities-20210730.html)).

[DIR-H] FIMA's option value is much larger than its observed balance. A $50bn FIMA draw would replace a $50bn market sale with a Fed collateralized repo asset — removing ~$40.5mn/bp of immediate DV01 from private absorption. **The lead indicator is not current FIMA outstanding (~$0) but the spread between offshore dollar funding stress and the FIMA/SRF administered rate. UST sign: strong reducer (when used).**

**(f) Capital controls / macroprudential FX measures.** Restrictions on outflows, export-realization rules, gold-import curbs. [HARD-M] Nigeria 2023: naira moved from **461/$ in May 2023** to **900/$ at end-2023** after unification of FX windows in June 2023, then a further **40% depreciation** in Jan–Feb 2024 ([IMF Nigeria Article IV 2024](https://www.elibrary.imf.org/view/journals/002/2024/102/article-A001-en.xml)). India in 2026 restored the export-proceeds realisation window to 9 months ([Moneycontrol, 9 Jun 2026](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/personal-finance/rbi-absorbs-hedging-costs-to-woo-nri-deposits-what-fcnr-b-investors-can-expect-next-13944432.html)). **UST sign: short-run reducer, long-run ambiguous (controls damage confidence, can force eventual reserve losses at worse FX).**

**(g) Gold accumulation / reserve diversification.** [HARD-H] WGC April 2026: central banks bought **19t net** (Poland 14t, China 8t, Czech 3t); 36-mo average **29t/month** ([World Gold Council](https://www.gold.org/goldhub/gold-focus/2026/06/central-bank-gold-statistics-central-banks-resume-net-buying-april)). At $3,300/oz, 29t/mo ≈ $3.1bn/mo ≈ **$37bn/yr of reserve flow diverted from USTs** — equivalent to ~$30mn/bp of 10y DV01 in annual demand removal. **UST sign: slow structural reducer.** The COFER dollar share at 56.9% Q3 2025 (25-year low, down from 72% peak 2001) is the cumulative footprint of this and (h).

**(h) Local-currency bond-market development.** [HARD-M] India joined JPMorgan GBI-EM on **28 Jun 2024**, expected to generate at least $20bn of index inflows over 10 months; JPM strategists estimated 32–40% realized around inclusion ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/markets/rates-bonds/five-things-know-india-enters-jpmorgan-em-debt-index-2024-06-26/)). India's 2026 package widened the FAR (Fully Accessible Route) universe and started counting VRR investments under General Route limits to attract long-term FPI debt ([SCC Times, 16 Feb 2026](https://www.scconline.com/blog/post/2026/02/16/rbi-released-development-and-regulatory-policies/amp/)). **UST sign: relative-demand reducer (global capital that could buy USTs buys India duration instead); but UST-stabilizing if it helps RBI avoid reserve liquidation.**

### The forced-selling ledger — taxonomic summary

| Mechanism | Amplifies UST selling | Reduces UST selling |
|---|---|---|
| (a) FX intervention via UST sales | ✅ Yes (directly) | — |
| (b) Reserve drawdown | ✅ Yes | (Accumulation = amplifier of demand) |
| (c) Policy-rate defense | — | ✅ Yes (avoids reserve sale) |
| (d) Diaspora schemes | — | ✅ Yes (substitutes private dollars) |
| (e) FIMA / swap lines | — | ✅ Strongest reducer (borrows against UST, doesn't sell) |
| (f) Capital controls | — | ✅ Short-run; ambiguous long-run |
| (g) Gold accumulation | ✅ Marginally (over years) | — |
| (h) Local-currency bond dev | ✅ Relative (global UST demand → local) | (Stabilizing for the sovereign) |

**The synthesis insight (all 3 models converge):** India in mid-2026 has *already* spent heavily on (a)/(b) — $53bn spot, $46bn drawdown, $110bn forward book — and is now deliberately pivoting to (d), having *declined* (c). That pivot is the live behavioral-layer answer to the council's central question. It is also the model the council will see replicated, with country-specific variants, across other EM importers if the Hormuz shock persists.

## §2 — The Carry Complex: In Depth and With History

The carry trade borrows in low-yielding "funding" currencies (historically JPY, CHF, at times EUR and CNH) to buy higher-yielding assets — EM local-currency bonds, USD credit, equities. The mid-2026 environment is unusually hostile to traditional EM carry because the dollar itself, at 10y real yield +2.16%, has become a partial funding *destination* rather than a funding source.

### Current funding/recipient configuration

[HARD] Funding currency policy rates (mid-2026): JPY ~0.75%, CHF 0.0%, CNH ~3.0%, EUR ~2.4%. High-yield EM recipients: TRY ~37%, BRL ~14.5%, MXN ~6.5%, ZAR ~7.0%, INR 5.25%. US (Fed funds floor ~5.5% effective, 10y 4.49%, real 10y +2.16%) acts as the ultimate high-yield low-risk vacuum, stripping marginal carry capital away from traditional EM targets ([central bank data, Jun 2026]; [US Treasury H.15](https://www.federalreserve.gov/releases/h15/)).

[HARD-H] Indonesia and Brazil were hiking in mid-June 2026 to defend their currencies as the dollar firmed ([Gramercy EM Weekly, 6 Jun 2026](https://www.gramercy.com/2026/06/em-weekly-june-6-2026/)). ECB and BoJ also expected to hike ~60bp / ~40bp by year-end (Gramercy). **This is a carry-unfriendly regime:** the funding-cost floor is rising globally, squeezing the spread that makes carry profitable.

### Carry mechanics and unwind triggers

Investors borrow in yen, convert to dollars/pesos/rupees, buy local sovereign debt. Profit = rate spread minus hedging cost (if hedged) or FX depreciation (if unhedged). Unwind triggers:
- **Hawkish pivot by funding central bank** (BoJ 2024 example).
- **Sudden VIX spike** (>35) and global volatility shock.
- **Rapid recipient-currency depreciation** that wipes out years of yield in days.

[DIR-H] When triggers hit, leverage forces rapid indiscriminate liquidation: high-yield assets dumped, funding currency frantically bid back. **The funding currency need not be touched by any policy change for the carry to unwind — leverage forces the hand** (1998 LTCM lesson).

### History — dated unwinds with magnitudes

**1994 — the "bond massacre."** [HARD] Fed hiked from 3.0% to 3.25% in Feb 1994, first of six hikes taking the rate to 5.5% by year-end ([Federal Reserve archives, 1994]). Global bond rout; capital sucked out of EMs. **The Mexican peso crisis broke in Dec 1994** — peso devalued from 3.5 to 5.75/$ within days, ~$50bn IMF/US bailout ([Brookings/Richmond Fed, 1995]). **Speed:** initial bond shock weeks; EM currency break delayed 10 months. **Council lesson:** Fed tightening transmits to EM via carry, but with long and variable lag.

**1998 — LTCM / Russian ruble default — the archetype.** [HARD-H] Russia devalued the ruble and defaulted on GKO debt **17 Aug 1998**; NY Fed orchestrated a **~$3.6bn LTCM rescue on 23 Sep 1998** ([Eco3min crisis hub](https://eco3min.fr/en/macro-financial-regimes/crisis-hub/1998-russia-ltcm-crisis/)). [HARD-H] **Yen appreciated ~15–20% vs dollar in a matter of days** (council members differ slightly on the exact %, both report from primary sources) ([Federal Reserve IFDP 899, Oct 1998]; [Eco3min](https://eco3min.fr/en/macro-financial-regimes/crisis-hub/1998-russia-ltcm-crisis/)). [HARD-H] 10y UST yield troughed at **4.16% on 5 Oct 1998** in the flight to quality; VIX intraday peak **49.53 on 8 Oct 1998** ([Eco3min VIX history](https://eco3min.fr/en/vix-history-major-spikes-1990-2026-volatility-events/)). **Speed: blistering, <2 weeks. Magnitude: systemic, required Fed intervention.**

**2003–2007 — yen-carry buildup.** [HARD] ZIRP-funded global risk-taking through the mid-2000s. **First crack on 16 Aug 2007**: AUD/JPY collapsed **−7.7% in a single day** (daily average prior 0.7%) ([CESifo WP 2707, 2007]). Further unwinds in Jan 2008 and Oct 2008 as the GFC accelerated. [HARD] BIS: yen +15%, Swiss franc +9% over the 10 months to Apr 2008 ([BIS Annual Report 2008](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2008e5.pdf)).

**2013 — taper tantrum.** [HARD-H] Bernanke's tapering signal (May 2013) → US 10y +100bp by Sep 2013. The "Fragile Five" (INR, IDR, BRL, ZAR, TRY) — all running CAD and fiscal deficits with loose policy — were hit hardest. [HARD-H] INR depreciated **~20% May→Aug 2013** to ~68.85/$ ([RBI data, 2013]; [SPJIMR/Narayan](https://www.spjimr.org/newsroom/blog/the-2013-rbi-fcnrb-swap-window-review-takeaways/)). **Direct ancestor of the FCNR(B) swap-window response.** Speed: sustained 4-month bleed; not a single-event unwind.

**11 Aug 2015 — CNH devaluation.** [HARD] PBOC adjusted yuan fix lower by ~2% ([PBOC, Aug 2015]). Multi-week global equity selloff; EM-carry tied to Chinese growth got crushed. [DIR-H] China subsequently spent ~$1tn of reserves over 2015–16 defending the yuan — the largest single example of mechanism (a) in history and the principal cause of the secular decline in China's UST holdings cited in the parent dossier.

**2018 — Fed QT + Turkey/Argentina.** [DIR-M] Fed hiking into QT + EM idiosyncratic crises (TRY −44% over the year, ARS) → sharp EM-carry drawdown mid-2018.

**August 2024 — the most instructive recent unwind (BIS-quantified).** [HARD-H] Going in: yen carry ~¥40tn / ~$250bn; net short-yen futures ~¥2tn / ~$14bn; cross-border yen claims on offshore non-banks >¥80tn / ~$500bn; an upper-bound estimate of speculative forward activity ~$160bn ([BIS Bulletin 90, 27 Aug 2024](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull90.pdf)). [HARD-H] **Trigger sequence:** BoJ "hawkish" hike 31 Jul 2024 → weak US payrolls 2 Aug → peak stress **5 Aug 2024** when TOPIX fell −12%, S&P −3.0%, VIX intraday **65.73** (third-highest ever) ([BIS Bulletin 90](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull90.pdf); [Eco3min VIX](https://eco3min.fr/en/vix-history-major-spikes-1990-2026-volatility-events/)).

[HARD-M] **Unwind size — only 10–15% of total yen carry positions actually unwound**, leaving the ~$4tn-estimated global yen-funded book largely intact ([LinkedIn/Machado, 10 Jun 2026](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/quiet-meeting-tokyo-could-rattle-markets-kenan-machado-cin5c)). **Unwind speed:** essentially a 3-day event (peak 5 Aug; S&P recovered all losses by Fri 9 Aug). [HARD-H] BIS specifically named yen + Swiss franc appreciation, notable CNH appreciation, and losses in MXN/BRL/ZAR ([BIS Bulletin 90](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull90.pdf)).

### Typical unwind quantification (Gemini's "carry math")

A *major* carry unwind = funding currency appreciates **5–12% vs high-beta recipients** within **3–10 days**, VIX spikes **>35**, **10%+ global equity market cap erased** before central-bank liquidity stabilization. This holds across 1998, Aug 2024, and the Jan/Oct 2008 unwinds.

### The 2026 tail risk

[DIR-H] **The tail risk for 2026 is that BoJ + ECB hike simultaneously into a hawkish Fed**, compressing remaining carry at all three funding legs at once. The Aug 2024 episode unwound 10–15% of the yen book in 72 hours and triggered crisis-level vol — if 25–40% of the remaining ~$4tn book unwound under a synchronized funding-rate squeeze, the transmission to global vol, EM currencies, and (per §4) US Treasury liquidity could exceed any post-2008 event.

### Carry transmission to USTs — the unresolved question

[DIR-H] Two channels operate in tension during a carry unwind:
1. **Safe-haven bid for USTs** (flight-to-quality lowers yields).
2. **Forced UST selling** by Japanese institutions (life insurers, banks repatriating; $1.2tn holdings) and by leveraged basis-trade hedge funds whose repo financing tightens.

The council does not resolve this artificially: GPT-5.5 and Opus call it "ambiguous"; Gemini emphasizes the bearish channel. **Empirical answer from history:** 1998 saw flight-to-quality dominate (10y troughed at 4.16%). Aug 2024 saw 10y yield briefly drop ~10bp then mean-revert — neither channel dominated cleanly. **The 2026 setup that would force the bearish channel: a carry unwind co-occurring with US fiscal stress, where flight-to-quality goes to gold, not USTs.**

### §3.2 The "17% per year, 3-year lock" claim — DEFINITIVE RECONCILIATION

**Council verdict (consensus of Opus + GPT-5.5, with Gemini partially dissenting): PARTIALLY CONFIRMED, SYSTEMATICALLY MIS-STATED.**

The **17% is NOT the FCNR(B) deposit coupon** (which is ~5.5–7% in USD). It is a **leveraged dollar internal-rate-of-return (IRR) on equity**, sourced from a **Jefferies research note**, and the "3-year" is the *deposit tenor / 1-year lock-in*, not a yield horizon.

**What Jefferies actually said.** *"We estimate that with 7–10x leverage and spread of 1.5–2 percent, customers can generate 17–27 percent US$-IRR annually over 3–5 years"* ([Moneycontrol/Jefferies, 9 Jun 2026](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/personal-finance/how-nris-can-earn-17-27-returns-on-fcnr-b-deposits-through-leverage-jefferies-report-13944415.html)) `[HARD-H]`.

**The worked example (Jefferies math):**
- NRI puts $100,000 of own equity.
- Borrows $700,000 at ~5% (7x leverage via SBLC-backed overseas loan; RBI has signaled it is "open to banks providing SBLC" so NRIs can borrow overseas and re-deposit).
- Deposits $800,000 at ~6.5% FCNR(B) yield.
- Net annual income = $800k × 6.5% − $700k × 5% = **$52,000 − $35,000 = $17,000 on $100,000 equity = 17% return**.
- At 10x leverage and/or cheaper borrow → toward 27%.

**Three things "17%" depends on that the headline omits:**

1. **Leverage (7–10x).** The unleveraged deposit yield is ~6–6.5%. The 17% only exists with borrowed money.
2. **NO FX risk to the depositor.** The deposit is in dollars and fully repatriable. The IRR is a *dollar* IRR, not rupee. Currency risk sits with RBI (it took the swap). `[DIR-H]`
3. **The spread must persist.** If overseas borrowing costs rise toward the deposit yield, the carry collapses. The 2026 spread (~1.5–2%) is far thinner than 2013's.

**Historical echo confirming "17%" is leverage, not new.** In the 2013 scheme, commentators documented NRIs earning **"17–18% annual return"** via the *same* leverage structure — own dollars + overseas overdraft at LIBOR+400bp re-deposited into FCNR(B) ([LinkedIn/Kaulgud, 23 Sep 2016](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/rajans-time-bomb-apparently-rakshita-kaulgud)) `[HARD-M]`. A 2013 Economic Times worked example showed a leveraged depositor making **"$16 or 16% of the original investment"** in year one ([ET, 30 Sep 2013](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/nri/forex-and-remittance/fcnr-swap-deal-what-it-means-for-nris/articleshow/23312691.cms)) `[HARD-H]`. So "~17% leveraged" is a *recurring, historically-grounded* figure — real for sophisticated leveraged investors, false as a description of the plain deposit.

**On Gemini's "synthetic INR-equivalent" framing.** Gemini argued the 17% might be a synthetic INR-hedged return: USD coupon + RBI subsidy that absorbs INR depreciation → effective INR-equivalent annualized yield ~15–17%. **Synthesis rejects this framing** because the FCNR(B) depositor takes NO INR exposure (the deposit is in USD, principal and interest repatriable). A "synthetic INR-equivalent return" is not a real metric for the depositor — they earn dollars. The RBI subsidy goes to the *bank's* hedging cost (so the bank can offer competitive USD rates), NOT to the depositor's INR-equivalent yield. Gemini's framing conflates a sovereign cost (RBI's subsidy) with a depositor return.

**What "17%" is NOT:** NOT an INR-equivalent yield (the depositor takes no rupee exposure); NOT a conflation with NRE rupee-deposit rates; NOT a different instrument. **Cleanest one-sentence verdict:** *"17% per year"* is a 7–10x-leveraged dollar IRR modelled by Jefferies, viable only for investors who borrow overseas against an SBLC; the underlying FCNR(B) deposit pays ~6–7% in USD, and the "3 years" is the lock-in/tenor, not a yield horizon. Any "guaranteed 17% in dollars" framing is **rumor-grade marketing.** Confidence: **High**.

### §3.4 The substitution mechanism — diaspora deposits AS UST-selling substitute

The mechanism, stated plainly: **a country defending its currency has two ways to supply dollars to the market.**

- **Route (a):** Central bank *sells its own reserves* (liquidating USTs) — the forced-seller channel.
- **Route (d):** Central bank *induces private non-residents to bring fresh dollars into the domestic banking system*, which the central bank then absorbs via the swap (taking the dollars on its balance sheet, often reinvested in USTs), while the bank deploys rupees domestically.

Route (d) **adds** to the dollar pool instead of **subtracting** from official reserves. As one Gulf-based advisor framed the 2026 program: *"in substance, a sovereign subsidy on currency risk"* ([Khaleej Times, 10 Jun 2026](https://www.khaleejtimes.com/world/asia/india-offers-dollar-rupee-swap-to-boost-foreign-currency-deposits-as-reserves-fall)) `[DIR-H]`.

**The 2013 episode is the cleanest proof of concept.** SPJIMR/Narayan calls the FCNR(B) swap window *"essentially a covered intervention mechanism"*: reserves were mobilised during peak stress, deployed via intervention at elevated USD/INR, and *later rebuilt* after the rupee stabilised — generating realised transaction gains. The dollar inflows were **invested in US Treasuries yielding risk-free USD returns**, while RBI's rupee leg "involved monetary creation without financial cost," so the swap income accrued without a funding cost ([SPJIMR/Narayan](https://www.spjimr.org/newsroom/blog/the-2013-rbi-fcnrb-swap-window-review-takeaways/); [LinkedIn/Sankara Narayanan, 8 May 2026](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/sankara-narayanan-v-92a24a14_fcnrb-inr-fxreserves-activity-7458530637382193152-EGUo)) `[HARD/DIR, H]`.

**Net effect on the UST market in 2013:** the diaspora dollars partly ended up *as* UST purchases by RBI, and crucially they removed the need to *sell* USTs to defend the rupee. The rupee recovered from 68.85 to ~62 by early 2014, and the current-account deficit compressed from **4.8% of GDP (FY13) to 0.9% (Q3 FY14)** ([LinkedIn/Sankara Narayanan](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/sankara-narayanan-v-92a24a14_fcnrb-inr-fxreserves-activity-7458530637382193152-EGUo)) `[HARD-M]`.

### §3.5 Substitution math — $X FCNR ≈ $X less reserve drawdown / UST selling

**First-order arithmetic is 1:1:** $1 of fresh FCNR(B) inflow that the central bank absorbs is ~$1 it does not have to draw from reserves to supply the same dollars to the market.

**Live 2026 figures:**
- RBI FY26 spot dollar sales: **$53.13bn** ([Business Line, 27 May 2026](https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/data-stories/data-focus/rbis-forex-intervention-in-spot-and-forward-market-in-fy26-highest-in-a-decade/article71029062.ece)) `[HARD-H]`.
- Reserve drawdown Feb→May 2026: **~$46bn** ($728.49bn → $682.3bn) `[HARD-H]`.
- Jefferies inflow estimate: **$50–70bn** `[HARD-H]`.

**Conclusion:** A successful $50–70bn FCNR(B) raise would **more than fully offset the entire FY26 spot intervention ($53bn) AND the Feb–May reserve drawdown ($46bn).** In UST terms, that's **~$50–70bn of UST selling avoided**.

**DV01 translation (GPT-5.5):** $40bn FCNR ≈ $32mn/bp of 10y-equivalent UST DV01 avoided; $50bn ≈ $40.5mn/bp; $70bn ≈ $57mn/bp. **For context, that's roughly 2/3 to ~1x of Japan's verified Apr–May 2024 $62bn MOF intervention in DV01 terms** — meaningful but not market-shaking on its own.

## §6 — Layman Anchor Bank + Kit

### §6.1 Five anchor concepts

1. **Carry trade** — borrowing where money is cheap to invest where it pays more, until the funding currency suddenly appreciates and forces a violent exit.
2. **FX intervention & reserves** — a country's "savings account" of foreign money it spends to prop up its currency.
3. **Diaspora bonds/deposits** — asking citizens abroad to lend the homeland their dollars, often with the home government covering currency risk as the sweetener.
4. **Swap lines / FIMA** — emergency dollar overdraft between central banks; borrow against your Treasuries instead of fire-selling them.
5. **"Why a country selling USTs raises US mortgage rates"** — fewer buyers for US government IOUs → US must offer higher interest → all US borrowing costs (mortgages, auto loans, business credit) rise.

### §6.2 Five hardest concepts — analogy + worked example + top-3 misconceptions

**1. Carry trade.**
*Everyday analogy:* Borrow on a 2% credit card to put money in a 6% savings account — pocket the 4%, until the card rate jumps or your savings bank wobbles. The global version: borrow in Tokyo's "boring casino" (0.75%), walk to the "high-stakes VIP room" (Mexico 9%, Turkey 37%), pocket the spread. When the VIP room crashes OR Tokyo raises rates, everyone races to repay simultaneously — that's a "carry unwind."
*Worked example:* Borrow ¥100m at 0.5%, convert to rupees at 1USD=¥150 = ~$667k, deposit in India at 6.5%. Gross annual carry ≈ 6%. If the yen rises 10% (back to ¥135/$), you now owe more dollars to repay your yen loan, losing ~10% on currency — wiping years of carry in days (the Aug 2024 lesson).
*Top-3 misconceptions:* (i) *"It's safe because rates are fixed"* — no, the **currency** move is the risk. (ii) *"Only banks do it"* — retail FX traders, crypto stablecoin arbitrage, and even pension funds do versions of it. (iii) *"Small unwinds are harmless"* — only 10–15% of the yen carry book unwound on 5 Aug 2024, and that 10–15% crashed global vol to crisis levels for 72 hours.

**2. FX intervention & reserves.**
*Everyday analogy:* Your currency is a stock; the central bank is a buyer-of-last-resort spending its dollar "war chest" to stop the price falling.
*Worked example:* RBI sold $53bn in FY26 and reserves fell ~$46bn from the Feb peak — like a household burning $46k of savings to defend its house price during a downturn. Japan's MOF spent ~$62bn in two days (Apr 29 / May 1 2024) on the same objective.
*Top-3 misconceptions:* (i) *"Reserves are free money"* — spending them is selling US Treasuries; every $1 sold reduces foreign UST demand. (ii) *"Intervention always works"* — it slows, rarely reverses, a trend; in 2013 it took rate hikes + FCNR + global stabilization to turn the rupee. (iii) *"Big reserves = no problem"* — China spent ~$1tn defending the yuan in 2015–16; even huge reserves bleed fast under sustained attack.

**3. Diaspora bonds/deposits.**
*Everyday analogy:* Instead of dipping into your savings to pay a bill, you ask your kids working abroad to lend you their dollars — and you cover their currency risk as a sweetener (you absorb the loss if your local currency falls vs the dollar).
*Worked example:* India hopes to pull in $50–70bn of NRI dollars; that's $50–70bn it need NOT drain from reserves (i.e., need NOT sell in USTs). The catch: it must repay in 3–5 years — so it's a time-shift, not a permanent fix. A successful $50bn raise ≈ $40.5mn/bp of UST DV01 of avoided selling — meaningful but not market-shaking globally.
*Top-3 misconceptions:* (i) *"It's all patriotic NRI money"* — in 2013, much was overseas-bank money lent to leveraged NRIs and round-tripped, per Ananth Narayan's critique. (ii) *"17% guaranteed in dollars"* — false; the plain deposit pays ~6–7% in USD. The 17% is the LEVERAGED IRR for someone who borrows 7–10x against an SBLC. (iii) *"It solves the problem"* — it time-shifts pressure 3–5 years; the dollars must be returned at maturity, with risk of outflow then.

**4. Swap lines / FIMA repo.**
*Everyday analogy:* A pre-approved overdraft between central banks. Borrow dollars against your Treasury collateral instead of fire-selling it. Like using your home equity line of credit instead of selling your house during a cash crunch.
*Worked example:* A central bank needing $20bn can repo its USTs to the Fed overnight at the SRF rate (~SRF min bid), get $20bn cash, and unwind the trade later — avoiding dumping $20bn of bonds into a falling market. Current FIMA outstanding (10 Jun 2026): $1mn. Peak swap-line usage was $450bn in May 2020 — the option value is huge even when the observed balance is small.
*Top-3 misconceptions:* (i) *"It's a bailout"* — it's a collateralized loan, repaid with interest, priced above private repo in normal markets. (ii) *"Everyone has one"* — standing lines are only the 5 core central banks (ECB, BoJ, BoE, SNB, BoC); FIMA is the broader repo backstop for ~250+ approved counterparties. (iii) *"It prints unlimited dollars"* — it's capped and collateralized; not QE for foreigners.

**5. "Why a country selling USTs raises US mortgage rates."**
*Everyday analogy:* If the biggest regular bidder at an auction stops showing up, the seller must drop the price (raise the yield) to attract other buyers.
*Worked example:* US 30-yr mortgage rates are priced off the 10y Treasury. If China/Japan together sell $90bn USTs in a month (as occurred Mar-2026), that's ~$72mn/bp of DV01 dropped on dealers, and 10y can rise 8–15bp on that alone. A 1% rise in 10y adds roughly $200/month to a $300k 30-yr mortgage.
*Top-3 misconceptions:* (i) *"Foreigners can crash the US overnight"* — the market is deep ($9.3tn foreign holdings out of ~$36tn outstanding); selling is gradual and partly offset by Fed/domestic buyers. (ii) *"It only hurts the government"* — it raises **all** US borrowing costs (mortgages, auto loans, business credit). (iii) *"Mortgage rates follow the Fed funds rate"* — they actually track the **long** Treasury yield, which foreigners influence at the margin.

### §6.3 Three foundational misconceptions to debunk

**Misconception #1: "Foreigners hold all US debt."**
False. Foreign investors hold ~$9.5tn of US debt — only ~25% of total outstanding. Vast majority is domestic: Federal Reserve, US mutual funds, commercial banks, pensions, individuals. Foreign flows dictate the margin and can spike rates short-term, but the core of US funding is domestic.

**Misconception #2: "If China sells its Treasuries, the US will go bankrupt."**
False. Sudden Chinese liquidation (~$0.7tn holdings) would spike rates and cause market chaos, but the US borrows in its own currency — in extreme crisis, the Fed can be buyer of last resort (QE) to absorb supply. Threat is to **market stability**, not sovereign solvency.

**Misconception #3: "High oil prices are always terrible for US financial markets."**
Historically false — under the "petrodollar" system, oil price spikes meant Gulf nations took windfall profits and reinvested directly in USTs, cushioning the blow. The 2026 Hormuz crisis is uniquely dangerous because it chokes off **physical volume** of oil, destroying revenue capacity and breaking the protective recycling loop — though the council disagrees on how complete that fracture is (Gemini: severe; Opus/GPT: partial since revenue per barrel is up).
