## 1 · SCOPE & STATE

**What this desk owns.** The US Rates desk is the franchise for the *risk-free* term structure: the nominal UST curve from bills to the 30-year, the real curve (TIPS), the inflation curve embedded between them (breakevens and 5y5y forwards), the *term premium* (the model-derived residual between observed yields and the expected average short-rate path), and the *funding/plumbing* layer that prices the collateral value of Treasuries — SOFR/repo, the Fed's balance sheet (reserves, RRP, QT), Treasury supply/auctions, and swap spreads. The desk's job is to have a view on the *level* of the risk-free rate, the *shape* of the curve, the *decomposition* of yields into expectations vs. premium, and the *flow/positioning* that moves them. `[DIR]` (H)

**What this desk does NOT own.** Corporate credit spreads belong to the **Credit desk** — Rates owns the risk-free base over which those spreads sit, not the spread itself. FX rate-differential trades (carry, basis, cross-currency) belong to the **FX desk** — Rates owns the dollar leg's rate, not the currency view. Equity index level belongs to **Equities** — Rates owns the discount-rate input. The boundary discipline matters in the current regime because the Iran/Hormuz shock transmits *across* all four desks simultaneously, and the Rates desk must isolate the rate-specific signal (term premium, real yields, Fed path) from the cross-asset noise. `[DIR]` (H)

**Regime call (one sentence).** *The curve has dis-inverted into a positively-sloped, restrictive, positive-term-premium regime in which a realized oil-supply inflation shock is pinning the front end high (no cuts, rising hike odds) while the long end is caught in a tug-of-war between a safe-haven/growth-fear bid and a fiscal/supply/term-premium push — a two-sided catalyst where the base case is "higher-for-longer real rates with a fat left tail of a bear-steepener."* `[DIR]` (M-H)

## 2 · THE ANALYST SPINE (§1–§10)

### §1 Regime — level & direction of the 10y and Fed stance
**The read.** The risk-free rate is *restrictive and range-bound at an elevated level*, with the front end anchored by a hawkish, on-hold-to-hiking Fed responding to a realized oil-driven inflation impulse. The 10y has spent 2026 oscillating in a roughly 4.4–4.7% corridor, spiking toward 4.69% during the May convexity-amplified selloff and easing back toward 4.45–4.49% on the June ceasefire/peace-talk de-escalation that pulled Brent from ~$101 to ~$87 ([U.S. News/Reuters, 2026-05-21](https://money.usnews.com/investing/news/articles/2026-05-21/analysis-us-treasuries-selloff-exacerbated-as-mortgage-investors-hedge-against-rising-yields); [CNBC, 2026-06-12](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/12/treasury-yields-oil-iran-deal.html)). `[HARD]` (H) The defining feature of the regime is that the *front end is policy-pinned* (Fed funds effective 3.62%, target 3.50–3.75%) while the *long end is contested* — this is what produces the positive slope. `[HARD]` (H) — [H.15, 2026-06-12](https://www.federalreserve.gov/releases/h15/)

The directional bias is *asymmetric and two-sided*. On the upside for yields: a Hormuz re-closure or ceasefire collapse re-ignites the oil→CPI→Fed-hike chain, lifting the whole curve with a front-end-led bear-flattening followed by a term-premium-led bear-steepening. On the downside for yields: a durable peace deal pulls oil lower, disinflation resumes, cuts return to the table, and the curve bull-steepens. `[DIR]` (M-H) The Fed's reaction function is the hinge: with May CPI at +4.2% YoY (energy +17.9% in April) the committee has explicitly prioritized inflation persistence over growth risk, which is *why no 2026 cuts are priced* ([Al Jazeera, 2026-06-10](https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2026/6/10/us-inflation-hits-new-three-year-high-amid-energy-price-surge); [Polymarket, 2026-06-04](https://polymarket.com/hi/event/fed-decision-in-june-825)). `[HARD]` (M)

**Best indicators:** DGS10 (level), the Fed-funds-futures/FedWatch hike-vs-cut distribution (path).
**Free series:** DGS10 — **4.45%** (2026-06-11). `[HARD]` (H). Fed path: no FRED real-time feed for the futures-implied probability distribution → `source_identified` (CME FedWatch, Polymarket are the public proxies; FRED DFEDTARU/DFEDTARL give the band: 3.75% upper). `[DIR]` (M)

### §2 Structure — curve shape and the dis-inversion message
**The read.** The curve is **upward-sloping / dis-inverted**: 10y−3m at +77bp and 10y−2y at +40bp per the brief, corroborated by H.15 levels (10y ~4.45–4.55 over 3m bill ~3.63 and 2y ~4.08). `[HARD]` (M-H) — [H.15, 2026-06-12](https://www.federalreserve.gov/releases/h15/) This is a *normalization of the sign* of the curve after the longest inversion in the modern record (the 2022–mid-2024 inversion). `[DIR]` (H) The crucial interpretive point for this desk: dis-inversion has happened via a **bear-steepener** dynamic — the long end has repriced higher (term premium and real-rate driven) faster than the front end has fallen — rather than the classic *bull-steepener* (front end falling fast on cuts) that usually precedes recession. `[DIR]` (M-H)

**The bull/bear × steepen/flatten quadrant** is the desk's core structural map:
- **Bull-steepener** (front falls faster than long): classic early-easing / recession-front-running. *Not the current regime.*
- **Bear-steepener** (long rises faster than front): fiscal/supply/term-premium/inflation-risk regime. **This is the active regime and the left-tail risk.** `[DIR]` (M-H)
- **Bull-flattener** (long falls faster): safe-haven duration grab / growth scare. *Active in flashes on each Hormuz escalation headline.* `[DIR]` (M)
- **Bear-flattener** (front rises faster): Fed hiking into the front end. *The upside risk if the Fed actually hikes on oil-inflation.* `[DIR]` (M)

The two-sided catalyst maps directly onto these quadrants: escalation → bear-flattener (Fed hike) then bear-steepener (supply/premium); de-escalation → bull-steepener (cuts return). The desk should watch *which way the steepening is driven* — front-end-led vs. long-end-led — as the single most informative real-time tell. `[DIR]` (H)

**Best indicators:** T10Y2Y, T10Y3M. **Free series:** T10Y2Y ≈ **+40bp**, T10Y3M ≈ **+77bp** (brief; consistent with FRED/H.15). `[HARD]` (M-H), `live` on FRED.

### §3 Valuation — real yields & term premium
**The read.** Real yields are *the most restrictive part of the picture*: DFII10 at **+2.16%** (2026-06-11) means the market demands a >2% real return to lend to the Treasury for ten years — a level that, by the layman heuristic, is firmly "restrictive" (0% = free money; 2%+ = restrictive). `[HARD]` (H) — [FRED DFII10](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFII10) The 5-year real is ~1.78%, the 30-year real ~2.72% per H.15 — a *positively-sloped real curve*, which itself is a normalization signal (real curves were inverted/flat through the hiking cycle). `[HARD]` (M-H) — [H.15, 2026-06-12](https://www.federalreserve.gov/releases/h15/)

**Term premium is the analytical centerpiece of this dossier.** The NY Fed's **ACM** 10y term premium (ACMTP10) stands at roughly **+0.73% (April monthly)** and **+0.83% in the 15 May 2026 daily snapshot** — solidly positive and at roughly the **35th percentile** of its full 1961–present distribution ([Eco3min term-premium decomposition, 2026-05-19](https://eco3min.fr/en/term-premium-decomposition-1961-2026/)). `[HARD]` (M) The decomposition of the 10y is illuminating: as of 30 April 2026 the ACM model splits the 4.45% 10y into **~3.72% expected-short-rate path + ~0.73% term premium** ([Eco3min, 2026-05-19](https://eco3min.fr/en/term-premium-decomposition-1961-2026/)). `[HARD]` (M) The regime-relevant fact is that ACMTP10 **durably crossed above zero in August 2024 after eight consecutive years negative**, and the converging drivers — QT returning duration to private hands, persistent deficits raising net supply, and a higher r-star repricing the long-term anchor — make this look like a *regime change*, not noise ([Eco3min positive-regime note, 2026-05-19](https://eco3min.fr/en/acmtp10-term-premium-back-positive-2024-2026-regime-shift/)). `[DIR]` (M)

**ACM vs Kim-Wright — how to read the two now.** Both are no-arbitrage *affine Gaussian* term-structure models; both define term premium as yield minus the expected average short-rate path. The differences are methodological, and they matter for reading levels:
- **Estimation:** ACM (Adrian–Crump–Moench 2013) splits estimation into fast *linear excess-return regressions*; KW (Kim–Wright 2005, Fed Board, FRED THREEFYTP10) uses one-shot *maximum likelihood* with Kalman-filtered latent factors ([Fed FEDS Note, 2017-04-03](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/robustness-of-long-maturity-term-premium-estimates-20170403.html)). `[HARD]` (H)
- **Information set:** ACM uses *Treasury yields only* (monthly, 1961→); KW *augments yields with Blue Chip survey expectations of the short rate* (weekly, 1990→). This is the single biggest driver of level differences — the Fed's own analysis shows that when you add surveys to ACM, its term-premium estimate converges to KW's ([Fed FEDS Note, 2017-04-03](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/robustness-of-long-maturity-term-premium-estimates-20170403.html)). `[HARD]` (H)
- **Convexity:** KW's definition *embeds* the (negative, ~−20bp at 10y) convexity premium; ACM's differs from the "pure" term premium by a small constant convexity term (~20bp at 10y), which mechanically makes one slightly lower than the other ([Fed three-factor model page](https://www.federalreserve.gov/data/three-factor-nominal-term-structure-model.htm); [Fed FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/robustness-of-long-maturity-term-premium-estimates-20170403.html)). `[HARD]` (H)
- **Agreement now:** the two correlate ~**0.86** over 9,074 daily observations (1990–2026), with ACM averaging ~**0.26pp higher** than KW historically — *but as of the most recent KW print (8 May 2026) the two are within 3bp of each other*, KW fractionally above ([Eco3min, 2026-05-19](https://eco3min.fr/en/term-premium-decomposition-1961-2026/)). `[HARD]` (M)

**The read for the desk:** when ACM and KW *agree* (as now), the term-premium signal is robust to model choice — both place it solidly positive and partially normalized. When they *diverge*, the gap is usually a survey-expectations story (KW's surveys anchoring the short-rate path differently from ACM's pure-yield extraction), and you should trust neither point estimate as gospel. The honest caveat: term premium is *unobservable* — it is a model residual, sensitive to sample period and estimated in-sample — so use it as a *directional decomposition tool*, not a tradeable number. `[DIR]` (H) A third estimate, the San Francisco Fed's **Christensen–Rudebusch (CR)** model, decomposes into expectations + premium + a model *residual* and is a useful triangulation ([SF Fed Treasury Yield Premiums, 2026-05-15](https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/data-and-indicators/treasury-yield-premiums/)). `[DIR]` (M)

**Best indicators:** DFII10 (real yield), ACMTP10 / THREEFYTP10 (term premium). **Free series:** DFII10 **2.16%** (`live`, FRED); ACMTP10 ~+0.73–0.83% (NY Fed daily, `latest_published`); THREEFYTP10 (KW, FRED, `latest_published` — last print ~8 May 2026). `[HARD]`/`[DIR]` (M-H)

### §4 Fundamentals / cycle — inflation & growth expectations, Fed reaction function
**The read.** Breakeven inflation is *elevated but not unanchored*. The 10y breakeven (T10YIE) sits around **2.29–2.36%** and the 5y5y forward (T5YIFR) at **2.23%** (2026-06-12) — above the Fed's 2% target but *well short of a de-anchoring*, which is the single most important fact in the regime: the market believes the oil shock is a *level* shock to near-term CPI, not a *regime* shift in long-run inflation expectations ([FRED T5YIFR, 2026-06-12](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/T5YIFR); [Convex T10YIE](https://convextrade.com/metrics/t10yie)). `[HARD]` (M-H) Realized inflation is the hot spot: May CPI +4.2% YoY (+0.5% m/m after +0.6% in April), energy +17.9% YoY in April, the highest since 2023 ([Al Jazeera, 2026-06-10](https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2026/6/10/us-inflation-hits-new-three-year-high-amid-energy-price-surge); [Polymarket, 2026-06-04](https://polymarket.com/hi/event/fed-decision-in-june-825)). `[HARD]` (M)

The **Fed reaction function** is the transmission hinge. The March 18 SEP revised 2026 PCE *up* to 2.7% (from 2.4% in December), and the committee has signaled it will *look through growth softness to inflation persistence* — the classic supply-shock dilemma where a central bank cannot simultaneously cushion growth and contain inflation ([Bitcoin News/SEP, 2026-04-05](https://news.bitcoin.com/federal-reserve-set-to-hold-rates-as-markets-fully-price-out-2026-cuts/)). `[HARD]` (M) The resulting path: ~96–98% hold probability at the June 16–17 FOMC, ~37–38% *hike* probability by October, and *zero* cuts priced for 2026 (vs. ~30% expecting a June cut as recently as March 4, before the war) ([CNBC, 2026-05-12](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/12/markets-raise-chances-for-a-fed-rate-hike-following-hot-inflation-report.html); [Bitcoin News, 2026-04-05](https://news.bitcoin.com/federal-reserve-set-to-hold-rates-as-markets-fully-price-out-2026-cuts/)). `[HARD]` (M)

The key tension the desk must hold: a classic oil *supply* shock is *stagflationary* — it pushes inflation up and growth down at once. The front end is pricing the inflation leg (no cuts/hikes); the long end is partly pricing the growth leg (the safe-haven flashes, the bull-flattener impulses). The breakevens staying anchored is what gives the Fed cover to *not* hike aggressively — if T5YIFR broke above ~2.5% and kept going, the calculus flips toward forced hikes. `[DIR]` (H)

**Best indicators:** T10YIE, T5YIFR. **Free series:** T10YIE ~2.29–2.36% (`live`, FRED); T5YIFR **2.23%** (`live`, FRED). `[HARD]` (M-H)

### §5 Cohorts / segments — front-end vs belly vs long-bond, nominal vs TIPS, bills vs coupons
**The read.** The curve's segments are telling *different stories*, which is the essence of a dis-inverted, contested regime:
- **Front end (bills/2y):** policy-anchored. 3m bill ~3.63%, 2y ~4.08%, 4-week bill ~3.61% — all tethered to the 3.50–3.75% target with a small term/credit pickup ([H.15, 2026-06-12](https://www.federalreserve.gov/releases/h15/)). `[HARD]` (M-H) Demand here is *strong* — auction flows show appetite "concentrated in the front-end" ([Newsquawk 30y recap, 2026-04-09](https://newsquawk.com/headlines/us-sells-usd-22bln-of-30-year-bonds-tails-0-5bps-09-04-2026)). `[DIR]` (M)
- **Belly (5y–7y):** the convexity-sensitive zone. 5y ~4.20%, and 5y/7y are "typically where convexity effects are felt" — the May selloff drove 5y to 4.35%, a 15-month high, with heavy 5y-futures convexity-hedging volume (one 33,000-contract block) ([U.S. News/Reuters, 2026-05-21](https://money.usnews.com/investing/news/articles/2026-05-21/analysis-us-treasuries-selloff-exacerbated-as-mortgage-investors-hedge-against-rising-yields)). `[HARD]` (M)
- **Long bond (30y):** the fiscal/supply/term-premium battleground. 30y ~4.97% (briefly 4.972%), with the *steepest demand softness* in auctions (the April 30y tailed 0.5bp, bid-to-cover 2.39x below average, dealer takedown jumped to 11.6%) ([CNBC, 2026-06-12](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/12/treasury-yields-oil-iran-deal.html); [Newsquawk, 2026-04-09](https://newsquawk.com/headlines/us-sells-usd-22bln-of-30-year-bonds-tails-0-5bps-09-04-2026)). `[HARD]` (M)
- **Nominal vs TIPS:** the breakeven (nominal − TIPS) of ~2.3% is the inflation-compensation wedge; TIPS *outperform* nominal in a realized-inflation regime because the principal accretes with CPI — which is exactly why TIPS are a regime winner (see Section 5/Winners). `[DIR]` (H)

The cohort takeaway: *the demand curve is downward-sloping in maturity*. Investors want the front end (high carry, policy certainty, low duration risk) and are reluctant on the long end (duration risk, supply glut, term-premium repricing). This is the *structural* reason the curve is steepening from the long end up. `[DIR]` (M-H)

### §6 Factors — what drives bond returns across regimes
**The read.** Bond returns decompose into a handful of orthogonal factors the desk trades around:
- **Duration (level):** the dominant return factor; a 10y has ~8.5–9 years of duration, so a 100bp move ≈ ~8.5% price move. In a rising-rate/restrictive regime, *short duration wins*; the carry on the front end (3.6%+) is collected with minimal duration risk. `[DIR]` (H)
- **Carry & roll-down:** with a positively-sloped curve, owning the belly and rolling down the curve earns both coupon carry and roll-down — *newly positive* after years of inverted-curve negative carry. The dis-inversion has *restored positive carry/roll* to long positions, a meaningful regime change for total-return investors. `[DIR]` (M-H)
- **Curve (steepener/flattener):** the relative-value factor. The regime favors *steepeners* structurally (fiscal/supply/term-premium), but with *bear-steepener* (long-led) rather than bull-steepener (front-led) realization. `[DIR]` (M-H)
- **Convexity:** positive for vanilla Treasuries (price rises faster than it falls), *negative* for MBS — the dominant amplifier in this regime (see Section 7). `[DIR]` (H)

Across regimes the factor leadership rotates: in *easing/recession*, duration and bull-steepeners dominate (you want long duration as the Fed cuts). In *restrictive/inflationary*, carry-on-the-front-end and steepeners dominate, and duration is a *liability*. The current regime is squarely in the second bucket, with the wrinkle that intermittent safe-haven flashes give long-duration brief, sharp rallies — making *tactical* long-duration a high-variance trade. `[DIR]` (M-H)

### §7 Positioning / plumbing — supply, swap spreads, SOFR/repo, QT/RRP, MBS convexity
**This is where the regime's fragility lives.** Five interlocking plumbing signals:

**(a) Treasury supply & auctions — demand is fraying at the long end.** The late-March 2026 auctions were a *stark signal of fading private demand*: the 2y produced a +1.8bp tail (vs −0.2bp average) with direct bidders collapsing to 16.5% and dealers forced to absorb 2.25x their average; the 5y bid-to-cover fell to 2.29x (lowest since Sept 2022) with dealers taking 15.6% (highest since May 2024) ([Sahm/Reuters, 2026-03-25](https://www.sahmcapital.com/news/content/weak-us-five-year-auction-points-to-shaky-investor-interest-2026-03-25); [LinkedIn auction summary, 2026-04-21](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/wesley-insider_the-treasury-departments-20-year-auction-activity-7452373085841776640-jjc0)). `[HARD]` (M) The April 30y was "soft" — 2.39x cover, 0.5bp tail, dealers 11.6% ([Newsquawk, 2026-04-09](https://newsquawk.com/headlines/us-sells-usd-22bln-of-30-year-bonds-tails-0-5bps-09-04-2026)). *Reading the tells:* a **tail** (auction stops above the when-issued yield) and a **rising dealer takedown** both mean private demand fell short and dealers had to backstop — a real-time demand gauge with *no clean free series* but recoverable from TreasuryDirect auction results / Newsquawk recaps. `[DIR]` (H) The counterpoint: the April 20y printed a strong 2.71x cover with 72% indirect (foreign) takedown — *demand is bifurcated, not uniformly failing* ([LinkedIn, 2026-04-21](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/wesley-insider_the-treasury-departments-20-year-auction-activity-7452373085841776640-jjc0)). `[HARD]` (M)

**(b) Negative swap spreads — the balance-sheet rental fee.** Swap spreads are *negative across the curve* (Treasury yields above matched-maturity swap rates) — 5y swap spread around −29bp, 30y around −78bp in early-2026 readings ([Pensford, 2026-01-01](https://www.pensford.com/industry-news/why-are-swaps-cheaper-than-treasurys); [Yahoo/Reuters, 2025-03-04](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/trump-deregulation-push-boosts-appeal-171013078.html)). `[HARD]` (M) The mechanism: post-2008 leverage rules (SLR/eSLR, G-SIB surcharges) make it *capital-expensive* for dealers to warehouse Treasuries, so they charge a premium to hold them — pushing UST yields *above* swaps. Negative swap spreads are therefore a *balance-sheet-constraint stress gauge*: the more negative, the tighter dealer capacity. eSLR relief in 2025 narrowed (widened) spreads, but they remain ~25bp inside Treasuries ([Pensford, 2026-01-01](https://www.pensford.com/industry-news/why-are-swaps-cheaper-than-treasurys)). `[DIR]` (M-H) BIS work attributes part of the long-end negativity to pension/insurance duration-hedging demand to *receive fixed* in swaps ([BIS WP705](https://www.bis.org/publ/work705.pdf)). `[DIR]` (M)

**(c) SOFR/repo — currently calm, but the canary.** SOFR is **3.60%** (2026-06-11), sitting tidily inside the target band with a 99th percentile around 3.67–3.69% — *no spike, no collateral stress at present* ([FRED SOFR release, 2026-06-08](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/release/tables?rid=445); [Trading Economics SOFR](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/secured-overnight-financing-rate)). `[HARD]` (H) But the desk should watch it like a hawk: a SOFR print spiking above the band (as in Sept 2019) signals reserves have fallen below "ample," and given that RRP is *drained* (see (d)), the buffer that absorbed prior shocks is gone. Repo financing costs for the ~$20bn+ of dealer inventory accumulated at weak auctions are *already rising* as G-SIBs pull back repo lending for reporting dates ([LinkedIn, 2026-04-21](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/wesley-insider_the-treasury-departments-20-year-auction-activity-7452373085841776640-jjc0)). `[DIR]` (M)

**(d) QT / RRP / reserves — the buffer is empty and QT is over.** The Fed *formally ended QT on 1 December 2025* and shifted to a "reserve management" regime as of January 2026; the **ON RRP facility effectively reached zero in early January 2026** ([FinancialContent, 2026-01-19](https://www.financialcontent.com/article/marketminute-2026-1-19-the-great-unwind-concludes-federal-reserve-formally-ends-quantitative-tightening)). `[HARD]` (M) FRED confirms RRPONTSYD at **$0.454 billion** (2026-06-12) — essentially drained from a >$2.5tn 2022–23 peak ([FRED RRPONTSYD, 2026-06-12](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/RRPONTSYD)). `[HARD]` (H) The balance sheet (WALCL) has stabilized around **$6.7 trillion** (down from the $8.96tn 2022 peak) ([Convex WALCL, 2026-05-27](https://convextrade.com/metrics/walcl); [SatoshiMacro, 2026-05-18](https://satoshimacro.com/tools/macro/fed-balance-sheet/)). `[HARD]` (M) *Mechanics for the desk:* QT drained reserves and removed the Fed bid for long-end duration (estimates attribute 50–80bp of the 2022–23 10y rise to QT). With RRP at zero, any further drain hits *bank reserves directly* — the "liquidity cliff" risk that forced QT's end. The market is now watching for the first "reserve management purchases" (RMPs) — *not QE*, but technical reserve-additions to keep funds-rate control ([FinancialContent, 2026-01-19](https://www.financialcontent.com/article/marketminute-2026-1-19-the-great-unwind-concludes-federal-reserve-formally-ends-quantitative-tightening)). `[DIR]` (M)

**(e) MBS-convexity hedging — the "Beast" has awakened.** This is the single most important amplification mechanism in the current regime. ~$2 trillion of MBS now carry 5%+ coupons (~4x the level of three years ago), and ~one-third of outstanding MBS trade near par — *exactly where convexity sensitivity is highest* ([WTMS, 2026-06-04](https://www.wellthatmakessense.com/mortgage-today-am-06-04-26/); [FuTu/Bloomberg, 2026-06-05](https://news.futunn.com/en/post/74143944/is-the-us-treasury-market-s-monster-returning-wall-street)). `[HARD]` (M) The mechanism: MBS have *negative convexity* from the embedded prepayment option. When rates rise, refis slow, prepayments fall, and MBS *duration extends* — so prices fall *faster* than a vanilla bond. To re-hedge the extending duration, MBS holders (REITs, insurers, money managers) *sell Treasury futures*, which pushes yields *higher still*, extending MBS duration *more* — a self-reinforcing feedback loop. `[DIR]` (H) During the May selloff this was decisive: Goldman estimated the episode added duration risk equivalent to **~$40bn of 10y Treasury selling**; Barclays' Nashikkar called it "more destabilizing for rates" than 2023 at comparable yield levels because higher coupons mean larger, less predictable duration extensions ([U.S. News/Reuters, 2026-05-21](https://money.usnews.com/investing/news/articles/2026-05-21/analysis-us-treasuries-selloff-exacerbated-as-mortgage-investors-hedge-against-rising-yields); [FuTu/Bloomberg, 2026-06-05](https://news.futunn.com/en/post/74143944/is-the-us-treasury-market-s-monster-returning-wall-street)). `[HARD]` (M) The QT linkage: as the Fed ran off ~$35bn/month of MBS into bills, it effectively *shifted negative convexity back from the Fed's balance sheet into the market* — amplifying the effect; the end of QT should eventually *stop* adding to that pile ([U.S. News/Reuters, 2026-05-21](https://money.usnews.com/investing/news/articles/2026-05-21/analysis-us-treasuries-selloff-exacerbated-as-mortgage-investors-hedge-against-rising-yields)). `[DIR]` (M) **Desk implication:** if the 10y breaks *decisively* through a key technical level, expect a concentrated convexity-hedging flow to accelerate the move — both up and (symmetrically, on a rally) down. `[DIR]` (M-H)

### §8 Cross-asset — rates as the spine of everything
**The read.** Rates sit *under* every other asset class:
- **Rates ↔ Dollar:** the broad dollar is firm (~100), consistent with a restrictive Fed and high real yields drawing capital in; the rate-differential channel is FX's desk, but Rates owns the dollar's *rate input*. A hawkish-Fed-on-oil narrative supports the dollar; a de-escalation/cut narrative weakens it. `[DIR]` (M)
- **Rates ↔ Equities (discount rate):** the 10y real yield (DFII10 2.16%) is the equity discount-rate anchor; restrictive real rates compress multiples, especially for long-duration growth equity. The two-sided catalyst is two-sided for stocks too: oil→inflation→higher discount rate is a headwind; de-escalation→lower rates is a tailwind. `[DIR]` (M)
- **Rates ↔ Credit (spreads over rates):** credit spreads sit *over* the risk-free curve — that's the Credit desk's variable, but the *all-in* corporate yield = UST + spread, so a rising risk-free base raises corporate funding costs even if spreads are stable. `[DIR]` (H)
- **Rates ↔ Gold (real-yield link):** the textbook inverse relationship (gold pays no coupon, so higher real yields raise its opportunity cost) has *decoupled* — gold is at a record ~$4,365 *despite* 2%+ real yields, because the safe-haven/de-dollarization/geopolitical bid is overwhelming the real-yield drag (echoing the Gulf-War episode where gold spiked on oil + equity plunge, [LA Times, 1990-09-25](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1990-09-25-fi-1161-story.html)). `[DIR]` (M) This decoupling is itself a *signal*: it says the market is pricing geopolitical/fiat-trust risk above the ordinary real-rate calculus. `[DIR]` (M)

### §9 Catalyst transmission — Iran war / Hormuz → rates
**The explicit chains** (and which are active *now*, mid-June, post-ceasefire-headline):

1. **Oil → CPI → Fed → front end (ACTIVE, dominant).** Hormuz closed since 4 Mar 2026, OPEC output −30%+, Brent spiked to ~$101 → energy CPI +17.9% YoY → headline CPI +4.2% → Fed prices out all 2026 cuts and ~37% hike odds → front end pinned at 4.08% (2y) / 3.63% (3m). *This is the realized base case.* `[HARD]` (M) — [Al Jazeera, 2026-06-10](https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2026/6/10/us-inflation-hits-new-three-year-high-amid-energy-price-surge)
2. **Safe-haven bid → long end (INTERMITTENT, flashes on escalation).** Each escalation headline triggers a flight-to-quality duration grab that *bull-flattens* the curve (long end rallies). The June ceasefire/peace-talk headlines did the *opposite* — pulled Brent ~$101→$87 and let yields drift down on disinflation hope rather than haven demand ([CNBC, 2026-06-12](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/12/treasury-yields-oil-iran-deal.html)). The haven channel is *currently dormant but one headline away from reactivation*. `[DIR]` (M)
3. **Supply / deficit → term premium → long end (ACTIVE, structural).** War-driven fiscal needs + a CBO-projected ~6%-of-GDP deficit (incl. interest) + foreign demand questions → rising net Treasury supply → dealers demand more premium to warehouse → term premium repricing → *bear-steepening*. The weak auctions and rising dealer takedown are this chain made visible ([Pensford, 2026-01-01](https://www.pensford.com/industry-news/why-are-swaps-cheaper-than-treasurys); [Sahm/Reuters, 2026-03-25](https://www.sahmcapital.com/news/content/weak-us-five-year-auction-points-to-shaky-investor-interest-2026-03-25)). `[DIR]` (M-H)
4. **MBS-convexity amplifier (CONDITIONAL).** Layered on top of chains 1 and 3: any decisive break higher in yields triggers convexity-hedging selling that *amplifies* the move (the May ~$40bn-equivalent episode). Active only on large directional moves. `[DIR]` (M)

**Escalation markers to watch:** Hormuz re-closure / ceasefire collapse; Brent back above ~$95–100; energy-CPI re-acceleration; a *de-anchoring* of T5YIFR above ~2.5%; an actual Fed hike. → *bear-flattener then bear-steepener, MBS amplification.*
**De-escalation markers:** durable peace deal; Brent sustained below ~$80; energy disinflation feeding through to headline CPI; cut probabilities returning to FedWatch. → *bull-steepener, the upside scenario.* `[DIR]` (H)

## 5 · WINNERS / LOSERS (under the current regime)

**WINNERS**
- **Short duration / front end / floating-rate.** *Mechanism:* with the Fed on hold-to-hawkish and the curve positively sloped, the front end pays ~3.6–4.1% with minimal duration risk; floating-rate (SOFR-linked) instruments reset higher if the Fed hikes, capturing the upside without price loss. In a rising/sticky-rate regime, *carry beats duration*. `[DIR]` (H)
- **TIPS over nominal.** *Mechanism:* in a *realized* inflation shock (energy CPI +17.9%), TIPS principal accretes with CPI, so they outperform matched nominal bonds when inflation surprises *high* — and breakevens at ~2.3% are not yet pricing the full realized-inflation risk, leaving a relative-value cushion. `[DIR]` (M-H)
- **Curve steepeners (long-end-led).** *Mechanism:* fiscal/supply/term-premium dynamics structurally favor a steeper curve; a 2s10s or 5s30s steepener profits as the long end cheapens relative to the policy-pinned front end. `[DIR]` (M-H)

**LOSERS**
- **Long duration / the long bond.** *Mechanism:* the 30y carries the most duration risk *and* the most exposure to term-premium repricing, supply glut, and weak auction demand; it is the cheapest segment for a reason. Total-return long-duration holders bear the bear-steepener. *Caveat:* in escalation scenario 4 (bull-flattener), long duration is the *winner* on a haven bid — so this is a high-variance loser, not a one-way bet. `[DIR]` (M)
- **MBS (negative convexity).** *Mechanism:* in a rising/volatile-rate regime, MBS extend duration just as you don't want them to, fall faster than vanilla bonds, and force convexity-hedging selling that compounds the loss; ~$2tn of near-par 5%+ coupons makes the negative convexity acute. *Caveat:* the high coupons offer fat carry if rates *stabilize* — MBS are a loser on *volatility*, not on *level*. `[DIR]` (M)
- **Nominal long-end holders without inflation protection.** *Mechanism:* they bear both the duration loss and the inflation erosion of fixed coupons — the worst of both legs of a stagflationary supply shock. `[DIR]` (M)

## 7 · LAYMAN KIT (9 chapters)

**1. The yield curve & inversion.**
*Analogy:* a savings-account menu — normally a 10-year CD pays more than a 3-month one (you lock money up longer, you get paid more). When the 3-month pays *more* than the 10-year, the menu is "inverted" — the bank is signaling it expects to cut rates soon (i.e., a slowdown).
*Worked example:* 10y 4.49% − 3m 3.71% = +0.78pp → upward-sloping, *dis-inverted*. A year ago it was negative (inverted).
*Top-3 misconceptions:* (1) "Inversion *causes* recession" — no, it *forecasts* by predicting Fed cuts. (2) "Dis-inversion = all-clear" — historically recessions often start *after* the curve re-steepens, not during inversion. (3) "One spread is the curve" — 2s10s and 10y3m can disagree; watch both.

**2. Real vs nominal yields.**
*Analogy:* your raise vs. your raise *after* the grocery bill went up. A 5% raise with 3% inflation is really a 2% raise.
*Worked example:* nominal 10y 4.49% − breakeven inflation 2.3% ≈ real 10y 2.16% (the DFII10 print).
*Misconceptions:* (1) "High nominal = tight policy" — not if inflation is higher; *real* yield is the tightness gauge. (2) "Real yields can't be negative" — they were for years (you *lost* purchasing power to hold safe bonds). (3) "TIPS yield = real yield exactly" — close, but TIPS carry a small liquidity premium.

**3. Term premium.**
*Analogy:* the *extra* interest a friend demands to lend you money for 10 years rather than rolling 1-year loans — compensation for "what if rates/inflation surprise me over a decade?"
*Worked example:* 10y 4.45% = ~3.72% (expected average short rate) + ~0.73% (term premium). The premium is the "uncertainty tax."
*Misconceptions:* (1) "It's observable" — no, it's a *model estimate* (ACM/KW disagree by tens of bp). (2) "It's always positive" — it was negative for most of the 2010s. (3) "Higher premium = higher rate expectations" — no, it's the part of the yield that is *not* rate expectations.

**4. Duration.**
*Analogy:* a seesaw — the longer the plank (maturity), the more the far end moves when rates tip. A 10y bond's price moves ~8.5% for a 1pp rate move; a 3m bill barely budges.
*Worked example:* +100bp on a 10y (duration ~8.5) ≈ −8.5% price. That's why long bonds are risky when rates rise.
*Misconceptions:* (1) "Bonds are safe" — long bonds can lose 20%+ in a year (2022). (2) "Maturity = duration" — duration is shorter than maturity for coupon bonds. (3) "Duration is constant" — it changes with yields (and *extends* badly for MBS).

**5. Breakevens / inflation expectations.**
*Analogy:* the over/under line on next decade's inflation — the gap between a normal bond and an inflation-protected one.
*Worked example:* nominal 10y 4.49% − TIPS 10y 2.16% = ~2.33% breakeven = the market's 10-year inflation bet.
*Misconceptions:* (1) "Breakeven = forecast" — it includes an inflation-*risk* premium, so it slightly overstates. (2) "Rising breakeven = bad" — modestly rising toward target can be healthy. (3) "5y5y = near-term" — no, it's inflation *5 to 10 years out*, the de-anchoring gauge.

**6. Fed policy & the dots.**
*Analogy:* the captain setting the ship's speed (the funds rate) and publishing a chart of where each officer thinks speed should go (the SEP "dot plot").
*Worked example:* funds target 3.50–3.75%; March SEP raised 2026 PCE to 2.7%; market prices *no cuts, ~37% hike odds*.
*Misconceptions:* (1) "Dots are promises" — they're forecasts, revised constantly. (2) "Fed controls all rates" — it controls the *front end*; the long end is the market's. (3) "Fed always cuts in a slowdown" — not in a *supply shock* with high inflation (the current bind).

**7. QT (quantitative tightening).**
*Analogy:* the Fed slowly selling back the furniture it bought during the emergency — draining cash from the room and removing a big buyer of bonds.
*Worked example:* balance sheet $8.96tn (2022) → ~$6.7tn (2026); QT *ended* Dec 2025; RRP buffer drained to ~$0.45bn.
*Misconceptions:* (1) "QT = rate hikes" — separate tools; QT works on the *balance sheet/long end*. (2) "Ending QT = QE" — no, it's neutral "reserve management." (3) "QT only drains banks" — it first drained the RRP buffer; now further drains would hit reserves directly (the "cliff").

**8. Auctions & supply.**
*Analogy:* the government holding a regular bake sale of IOUs — if too few buyers show up, it has to cut the price (raise the yield), and the "dealers" (banks required to attend) get stuck with the leftovers.
*Worked example:* a "tail" = the sale clears at a *higher* yield than expected (weak demand); March 2026 2y tailed +1.8bp with dealers forced to take 2.25x their usual share.
*Misconceptions:* (1) "Auctions always succeed" — they can be ugly; demand can fade. (2) "Foreign buyers always show" — indirect (foreign) bids swing a lot. (3) "Supply doesn't move yields" — a supply glut *raises* term premium and yields.

**9. Safe-haven demand.**
*Analogy:* in a storm, money runs to the strongest shelter — and US Treasuries are the global shelter, so their price rises (yield falls) in a crisis.
*Worked example:* during the Oct-2023 Israel-Hamas shock and the 2026 Iran escalations, flight-to-quality capped Treasury selloffs even amid high inflation.
*Misconceptions:* (1) "Crisis always lowers yields" — not if the crisis is *inflationary* (oil); the two forces fight. (2) "Gold and Treasuries always move together as havens" — gold can soar while real yields stay high (2026's decoupling). (3) "Safe-haven = no risk" — havens can still lose to inflation.

## 8 · COUNCIL & HONEST GAPS

**Debate 1 — Is the positive term premium back *for good*?**
- *Camp A (regime change):* The August-2024 durable cross above zero, sustained through 2026, is a structural break driven by QT returning duration, persistent ~6%-of-GDP deficits, higher r-star, and reduced foreign/reserve-manager demand — the premium will stay positive and could rise further ([Eco3min regime note, 2026-05-19](https://eco3min.fr/en/acmtp10-term-premium-back-positive-2024-2026-regime-shift/)). `[DIR]` (M)
- *Camp B (mean-reversion skeptics):* The estimate is a *model residual*, sample-sensitive and in-sample; ACM at +0.73% is only the 35th percentile and could revert toward zero if a growth scare brings the safe-haven bid back and the Fed eases ([Eco3min, 2026-05-19](https://eco3min.fr/en/term-premium-decomposition-1961-2026/); [yieldcurve.pro on ACM limitations](https://www.yieldcurve.pro/learn/acm-model)). `[DIR]` (M)
- *Camp C (model-agnostic):* ACM and KW currently agree within 3bp, so *whatever* the level, the *direction* (out of the negative ZIRP regime) is robust — but don't trade the point estimate ([Fed FEDS Note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/robustness-of-long-maturity-term-premium-estimates-20170403.html)). `[DIR]` (M)

**Debate 2 — Does dis-inversion still signal recession?**
- *Camp A (signal is broken/delayed):* The 2022–24 inversion *failed* to produce a recession; structural forces (excess savings, fiscal support, strong labor) defanged the indicator, so the current dis-inversion carries little forecasting weight ([Goover/analysis, 2025-05-16](https://seo.goover.ai/report/202505/go-public-report-en-ba0bd29b-3cb6-438f-9e06-118a0cc9dac3-0-0.html)). `[DIR]` (M)
- *Camp B (the lag is the point — watch the re-steepening):* Historically recessions often begin *after* the curve re-steepens (bull-steepens) as the Fed starts cutting — so dis-inversion is not the all-clear; it's potentially the *late-cycle warning*, and the current dis-inversion being a *bear*-steepener (not bull) is the crucial distinction ([NY Fed YCFAQ](https://www.newyorkfed.org/research/capital_markets/ycfaq); [Reuters, 2024-07-29](https://www.reuters.com/markets/rates-bonds/us-yield-curve-nears-flip-with-jury-out-recession-signal-2024-07-29/)). `[DIR]` (M-H)
- *Camp C (regime-dependent):* The signal's reliability depends on *why* it inverted/dis-inverted; a supply-shock-driven curve carries different information than a demand-driven one — apply with judgment, not mechanically ([Chicago Fed Letter](https://www.chicagofed.org/publications/chicago-fed-letter/2018/404)). `[DIR]` (M)

**Debate 3 — Will the Fed actually HIKE on oil-inflation?**
- *Camp A (hold/look-through):* Oil is a *supply* shock; hiking into it deepens the growth hit without addressing the cause — the Fed will hold and look through, as its revealed preference (96–98% June hold, no hikes delivered yet) shows ([Polymarket, 2026-06-04](https://polymarket.com/hi/event/fed-decision-in-june-825)). `[DIR]` (M)
- *Camp B (hike if it de-anchors):* If T5YIFR breaks above ~2.5% or CPI re-accelerates past 4.5%, the Fed hikes to defend credibility — the ~37% October hike odds price exactly this risk ([CNBC, 2026-05-12](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/12/markets-raise-chances-for-a-fed-rate-hike-following-hot-inflation-report.html)). `[DIR]` (M)
- *Camp C (the market does the tightening):* As in Oct-2023, rising long yields *substitute* for hikes ("equivalent of a 25bp hike"), so the Fed can stay on hold while the bond market does the tightening for it ([S&P Global, 2023-10-19](https://www.spglobal.com/market-intelligence/en/news-insights/articles/2023/10/us-treasury-yields-surge-cooling-fed-s-rate-hike-prospects-77991418)). `[DIR]` (M)

**Debate 4 (desk-specific) — Is MBS-convexity hedging *systemically* destabilizing now?**
- *Camp A (the Beast is real and underpriced):* ~$2tn of near-par 5%+ MBS, Goldman's ~$40bn-equivalent May estimate, and Barclays calling it "more destabilizing than 2023" mean any decisive yield break triggers self-reinforcing flows the market underestimates ([FuTu/Bloomberg, 2026-06-05](https://news.futunn.com/en/post/74143944/is-the-us-treasury-market-s-monster-returning-wall-street)). `[DIR]` (M)
- *Camp B (transient / QT-linked):* The amplification was partly QT pushing MBS negative convexity into the market; with QT over, the flow should fade and the effect is a *volatility* event, not a *level* regime ([U.S. News/Reuters, 2026-05-21](https://money.usnews.com/investing/news/articles/2026-05-21/analysis-us-treasuries-selloff-exacerbated-as-mortgage-investors-hedge-against-rising-yields)). `[DIR]` (M)

### CONSOLIDATED NO-FREE-FEED LIST (the honest gaps)
1. **MOVE index (implied rate volatility)** — *the* critical gap. No FRED series; ICE-licensed real-time; only delayed/EOD via third-party aggregators (Investing.com, Convex). In a convexity-amplified regime this is the variable you most need and can least get free. `[HARD]` (H)
2. **Fed-funds-futures implied probability distribution (hike/cut odds)** — no first-party free real-time series; public proxies are CME FedWatch and Polymarket (not official, not a clean time series). FRED gives only the target band (DFEDTARU/DFEDTARL). `[DIR]` (M)
3. **Auction tail / dealer-takedown time series** — TreasuryDirect publishes per-auction results, but there is no clean machine-readable free *time series* of tails and takedown; the desk recovers these from TreasuryDirect PDFs or Newsquawk/Reuters recaps. `[DIR]` (M)
4. **Swap spreads (real-time)** — derived from swap rates that are not on FRED in clean real-time; available via market data terminals or reconstructed from DGS-series minus a swap-rate source (third-party). `[DIR]` (M)
5. **ACMTP10 / THREEFYTP10 latency** — both are free (NY Fed / FRED) but *latest_published*, not live; ACM daily lags by days, KW is weekly-to-daily with a lag. Fine for regime reading, not for intraday. `[DIR]` (M)
6. **Christensen–Rudebusch (CR) decomposition** — free from SF Fed but published monthly with lag (`latest_published`). `[DIR]` (M)
7. **Intraday repo/SOFR distribution stress** — SOFR EOD is free (FRED, NY Fed); the *intraday* percentile distribution and bilateral-repo stress signals are not free in real time. `[DIR]` (M)
