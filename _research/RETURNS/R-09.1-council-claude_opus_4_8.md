## Section 1 — Sovereign Dollar-Response Taxonomy (8 mechanisms)

When the dollar firms and US real rates rise, a sovereign with an external-financing gap has a menu of responses. The crucial organizing question for this council is **which responses force more US Treasury (UST) selling and which ones substitute for it.** Below, each mechanism is mapped to (i) its effect on UST demand/supply and (ii) its effect on the country's own rate spread vs the US.

**(a) FX intervention via UST sales (spot).** The central bank sells dollars in the spot market to slow currency depreciation; the dollars are sourced by liquidating reserve assets, predominantly USTs and dollar deposits. This is the canonical *forced-seller* channel. India is the live exemplar: the RBI sold a record **$53.13bn net in the spot market in FY26** — its largest currency defence in 15+ years — as the rupee fell ~9.5% ([Business Line, 27 May 2026](https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/data-stories/data-focus/rbis-forex-intervention-in-spot-and-forward-market-in-fy26-highest-in-a-decade/article71029062.ece)) `[HARD, H]`. Effect on UST market: **directly bearish (supply ↑ / a marginal holder exits)**. Effect on own spread: narrows the country's risk premium *if* successful, but burns reserves. `[DIR, H]`

**(b) Reserve accumulation / drawdown.** The mirror of (a) over the cycle. India's reserves fell from an all-time high **$728.49bn (week ended 27 Feb 2026)** to **$682.3bn (29 May 2026)** — a ~$46bn drawdown, with $40bn lost in a single month and $11bn in one week ([RBI Governor Malhotra, 5 Jun 2026, via Upstox](https://upstox.com/news/market-news/latest-updates/forex-reserve-at-healthy-682-bn-adequate-to-provide-import-cover-for-11-months-rbi-guv/article-194879/); [Tribune, 6 Jun 2026](https://www.tribuneindia.com/news/business/indias-forex-reserve-rises-by-usd-938-million-to-usd-682-32-billion-rbi-data/)) `[HARD, H]`. Drawdown = UST-bearish; accumulation = UST-bullish. The forward book is a hidden second drawdown channel: RBI's net-short forward dollar position reached **$110–115bn by early June 2026** ([KuCoin flash, 9 Jun 2026](https://www.kucoin.com/news/flash/rbi-s-forex-defense-hits-110-billion-as-rupee-hits-record-low)) `[HARD, M — single secondary source; corroborated qualitatively by Business Line at >$103bn]`. Forward intervention defers the spot UST sale but commits to it later.

**(c) Policy-rate defense (hiking to defend the currency).** Raising domestic rates to widen the carry buffer and stem outflows. This *avoids* UST selling but at the cost of domestic growth. Notably, India in 2026 has **declined** this route — the MPC held the repo at 5.25% for a third consecutive meeting on 5 Jun 2026 and kept a neutral stance, prioritising growth (FY27 GDP cut to 6.6%) even as it revised FY27 CPI up to 5.1% ([Way2Wealth MPC update, 5 Jun 2026](https://www.way2wealth.com/Reports/RR05062026db7dd.pdf); [Governor Malhotra statement, 4–5 Jun 2026](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9u3MtqbdZ44)) `[HARD, H]`. Contrast 2013, when Rajan hiked the repo *twice* (Oct 2013, Jan 2014) alongside the FCNR scheme ([Economic Times, 20 Jun 2016](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/economy/policy/raghuram-rajans-tenure-a-lot-done-a-lot-more-left/articleshow/52825546.cms)) `[HARD, H]`. Effect on UST: neutral-to-bullish (reduces the need to sell reserves). Effect on own spread: widens it favourably.

**(d) Diaspara-deposit / diaspora-bond schemes.** The heart of this dossier (Section 3). The state pulls *private* non-resident dollars into the banking system, which the central bank can then either swap into local currency (covered intervention) or hold. This is the principal **UST-selling-substitute** in the taxonomy: every $1 of fresh diaspora deposit is ~$1 the central bank does *not* have to draw from reserves. Effect on UST: **bullish/neutral** (private inflow replaces official outflow; some inflow even ends up reinvested in USTs by the central bank). Effect on own spread: relieves pressure without a policy hike. `[DIR, H]`

**(e) FIMA repo / central-bank swap lines.** The Fed's FIMA (Foreign and International Monetary Authorities) repo facility lets foreign central banks borrow dollars *against* their UST holdings rather than selling them outright; standing Fed swap lines (to ECB, BoJ, BoE, SNB, BoC) do the same for the five core counterparties. This is explicitly designed to **prevent forced UST liquidation** during dollar squeezes. Effect on UST: **strongly bullish/stabilising** (removes the fire-sale incentive). Effect on own spread: caps the offshore dollar funding premium. `[DIR, H]` (Detailed in the parent Rates dossier; flagged here as the institutional substitute for (a).)

**(f) Capital controls / administrative measures.** Restrictions on outflows, export-proceeds realisation rules, gold-import curbs. India in 2026 restored the export-proceeds realisation window to 9 months and used gold-import curbs in 2013 ([Moneycontrol, 9 Jun 2026](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/personal-finance/rbi-absorbs-hedging-costs-to-woo-nri-deposits-what-fcnr-b-investors-can-expect-next-13944432.html); [Deccan Herald, 20 Jun 2016](https://www.deccanherald.com/opinion/understanding-so-called-time-bomb-2070659)) `[DIR, H]`. Effect on UST: neutral-to-bullish (reduces the BoP gap, hence the need to sell reserves). Effect on own spread: can widen the *offshore* premium (NDF) if controls bite.

**(g) Gold accumulation / diversification.** Buying gold (and non-dollar reserves) to diversify away from USTs. This is a *slow structural* UST-bearish channel — central banks (notably China, and Gulf SWFs at the margin) have shifted reserve composition toward gold. The dollar's COFER reserve share has fallen to ~56.9% in Q3 2025, lowest since 1995, from a 72% peak in 2001 ([IMF COFER via Fortune, 24 Mar 2026](https://fortune.com/2026/03/24/iran-hormuz-petrodollar-national-debt-trump/)) `[HARD, H]`. Effect on UST: marginally bearish over years; not a crisis tool.

**(h) Local-currency bond-market development.** Deepening domestic debt markets (and opening them to foreigners via index inclusion / Fully Accessible Route) reduces "original sin" and the future need to sell reserves. India's 2026 package widened the FAR universe and counts VRR investments under General Route limits to attract long-term FPI debt inflows ([SCC Times, 16 Feb 2026](https://www.scconline.com/blog/post/2026/02/16/rbi-released-development-and-regulatory-policies/amp/); [Moneycontrol, 9 Jun 2026](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/personal-finance/rbi-absorbs-hedging-costs-to-woo-nri-deposits-what-fcnr-b-investors-can-expect-next-13944432.html)) `[DIR, H]`. Effect on UST: long-run bullish (inflows can be parked partly in USTs; reduces forced-seller risk).

**Synthesis — the forced-selling ledger.** Mechanisms (a) and (b-drawdown) **amplify** UST supply. Mechanisms (c), (d), (e), (f), (h) **reduce** the need to sell USTs; (g) is structurally bearish but slow. The 2026 Indian policy stance is revealing: faced with a record-firm dollar, the RBI has *already* spent heavily on (a)/(b) and is now deliberately pivoting toward (d) — the FCNR(B) swap window — precisely to slow the reserve bleed and avoid the growth cost of (c). That pivot is the central narrative of this supplement, and it is the behavioural-layer answer to "who keeps buying / stops selling USTs."

## Section 2 — The Carry Complex

**Funding currencies and recipients.** The carry trade borrows in low-yield "funding" currencies — historically the yen (JPY), Swiss franc (CHF), and at times the euro (EUR) and offshore renminbi (CNH) — to buy higher-yielding assets, often EM local-currency bonds, USD credit, or equities. When US *real* rates are high (≈+2.16% on the 10y in mid-2026), the dollar itself becomes a partial funding-*destination* rather than a funding source, and the relative attractiveness of EM carry compresses. The 2026 configuration is unusual: a firm dollar (DXY ~100), a hawkish Fed pricing a hike, and EM central banks (Indonesia, Brazil) being forced to hike to defend their currencies ([Gramercy, 6 Jun 2026](https://www.gramercy.com/2026/06/em-weekly-june-6-2026/)) `[HARD, H]`. This is a carry-*unfriendly* regime: the funding-cost floor is rising globally (ECB and BoJ also expected to hike ~60bps and 40bps by year-end per Gramercy) `[HARD, M]`, squeezing the spread that makes carry profitable.

**Yen carry, 2003–07 build and 2008 unwind.** Through the mid-2000s, near-zero BoJ rates funded a vast global carry book. The 2008 GFC forced a violent unwind as risk assets collapsed and the yen surged — a textbook "carry crash" where the funding currency appreciates fastest exactly when leveraged holders most need to cover. `[DIR, H]`

**1998 LTCM / ruble — the archetype.** Russia defaulted on domestic (GKO) debt and devalued the ruble on **17 Aug 1998**; LTCM, whose counterparties had funded positions in yen, imploded; the NY Fed orchestrated a **~$3.6bn private rescue on 23 Sep 1998** ([Eco3min](https://eco3min.fr/en/macro-financial-regimes/crisis-hub/1998-russia-ltcm-crisis/)) `[HARD, H]`. The yen surged **~20% against the dollar within weeks** purely on forced deleveraging, with the 10y UST yield troughing at **4.16% on 5 Oct 1998** in the flight to quality, and the VIX peaking intraday at **49.53 on 8 Oct 1998** ([Eco3min crisis hub](https://eco3min.fr/en/macro-financial-regimes/crisis-hub/1998-russia-ltcm-crisis/); [Eco3min VIX history](https://eco3min.fr/en/vix-history-major-spikes-1990-2026-volatility-events/); [LinkedIn/Machado, 10 Jun 2026](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/quiet-meeting-tokyo-could-rattle-markets-kenan-machado-cin5c)) `[HARD, H]`. The lesson the council should carry: **the funding currency need not be touched by any policy change for the carry to unwind — leverage forces the hand.** `[DIR, H]`

**1994 "bond massacre."** The Fed's surprise tightening cycle from Feb 1994 doubled short rates within a year and triggered a global bond rout and the Orange County / Mexico (Dec 1994 "Tequila") cascade. `[DIR, M]` — flagged for completeness; no free single primary feed captured here, *no free feed for precise unwind magnitudes*.

**2013 taper tantrum.** Bernanke's May 2013 tapering signal triggered mass EM outflows; the "Fragile Five" (India, Indonesia, Brazil, Turkey, South Africa) — all running current-account and fiscal deficits with loose policy — were hit hardest; the rupee fell to ~68.85/$ ([SPJIMR/Narayan](https://www.spjimr.org/newsroom/blog/the-2013-rbi-fcnrb-swap-window-review-takeaways/); [TOP STORY EM video, 1 Jun 2026](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=S1TiiVkQxnI)) `[HARD/DIR, H]`. This episode is the direct ancestor of the FCNR(B) swap-window response (Section 3).

**2015 CNH devaluation.** China's August 2015 mini-devaluation and subsequent reserve drawdown (China spent ~$1tn of reserves over 2015–16 defending the yuan) is the largest single example of mechanism (a) — and the principal cause of the secular decline in China's UST holdings cited in the parent Rates dossier. `[DIR, H]`

**2018.** Fed hiking into quantitative tightening plus the Turkey/Argentina mini-crises produced a sharp EM-carry drawdown in mid-2018. `[DIR, M]`

**August 2024 — the most instructive recent unwind.** Going into the event, the yen carry book was about **¥40tn (~$250bn)**; net short-yen futures positioning was ~**¥2tn (~$14bn)**; an upper-bound estimate of hedge-fund speculative forward activity was ~**$160bn**; cross-border yen bank claims on offshore non-banks exceeded **¥80tn (~$500bn)** ([BIS Bulletin No. 90, 27 Aug 2024](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull90.pdf)) `[HARD, H]`. The trigger was the BoJ's "hawkish" 31 Jul 2024 hike plus weak US payrolls (2 Aug); the peak stress hit **5 Aug 2024**, when the TOPIX fell **−12%**, the S&P 500 fell **−3.0%**, and the VIX spiked **above 60** (intraday 65.73, third-highest ever) ([BIS](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull90.pdf); [Eco3min VIX](https://eco3min.fr/en/vix-history-major-spikes-1990-2026-volatility-events/)) `[HARD, H]`. **Unwind speed:** essentially a 3-day event (peak 5 Aug; S&P had recovered all losses by Fri 9 Aug) `[HARD, H]`. **Unwind size:** strategists estimated only **10–15% of total yen carry positions actually unwound**, leaving the ~$4tn-estimated global yen-funded book largely intact ([LinkedIn/Machado, 10 Jun 2026](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/quiet-meeting-tokyo-could-rattle-markets-kenan-machado-cin5c)) `[HARD, M]`. **Council-relevant takeaway:** a partial unwind of a fraction of one funding-currency book moved global equity vol to crisis levels in 72 hours — the tail risk for 2026 is that BoJ/ECB hikes plus a hawkish Fed compress the remaining carry simultaneously.

### 3.2 The "17% per year, 3-year lock" claim — DEFINITIVE RECONCILIATION

**Verdict: PARTIALLY TRUE BUT SYSTEMATICALLY MIS-STATED.** The "17%" is **not** the FCNR(B) deposit coupon (which is ~5.5–7% in USD). It is a **leveraged internal-rate-of-return (IRR) on equity**, sourced from a **Jefferies research note**, and the "3-year" is the *deposit tenor / lock-in*, not a yield horizon. The conflation of the two into "17% per year for 3 years" is a marketing distortion.

**What Jefferies actually said.** *"We estimate that with 7–10x leverage and spread of 1.5–2 percent, customers can generate 17–27 percent US\$-IRR annually over 3–5 years"* ([Moneycontrol/Jefferies, 9 Jun 2026](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/personal-finance/how-nris-can-earn-17-27-returns-on-fcnr-b-deposits-through-leverage-jefferies-report-13944415.html)) `[HARD, H]`. The worked example: an NRI puts **$100,000 of own equity**, borrows another **$700,000 at ~5%** (7x leverage via an SBLC-backed overseas loan), deposits **$800,000** at a ~6.5% FCNR(B) yield; net annual income = $800k × 6.5% − $700k × 5% = **$52,000 − $35,000 = $17,000 on $100,000 equity = 17% return**. Push to 10x leverage and/or a cheaper borrow → toward 27% ([Moneycontrol](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/personal-finance/how-nris-can-earn-17-27-returns-on-fcnr-b-deposits-through-leverage-jefferies-report-13944415.html)) `[HARD, H]`.

**Three things the "17%" depends on that the headline omits:**
1. **Leverage (7–10x).** The unleveraged deposit yield is ~6–6.5%. The 17% only exists with borrowed money — RBI is "open to banks providing SBLC (Standby Letter of Credit)" so NRIs can borrow overseas and re-deposit ([Moneycontrol](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/personal-finance/how-nris-can-earn-17-27-returns-on-fcnr-b-deposits-through-leverage-jefferies-report-13944415.html)) `[HARD, H]`.
2. **No FX risk to the depositor** — the deposit is in dollars and fully repatriable, so the IRR is a *dollar* IRR, not rupee. The currency risk sits with RBI (it took the swap). `[DIR, H]`
3. **The spread persists** — if overseas borrowing costs rise toward the deposit yield, the carry collapses. The 2026 spread (~1.5–2%) is far thinner than 2013's.

**Historical echo confirming the "17%" is a leveraged figure, not new.** In the 2013 scheme, commentators documented NRIs earning **"17–18% annual return"** via the *same* leverage structure — own dollars + overseas overdraft at LIBOR+400bps re-deposited into FCNR(B) ([LinkedIn/Kaulgud, 23 Sep 2016](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/rajans-time-bomb-apparently-rakshita-kaulgud)) `[HARD, M]`. And a 2013 Economic Times worked example showed a leveraged depositor making **"$16 or 16% of the original investment"** in year one ([ET, 30 Sep 2013](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/nri/forex-and-remittance/fcnr-swap-deal-what-it-means-for-nris/articleshow/23312691.cms)) `[HARD, H]`. So "~17% leveraged" is a *recurring, historically grounded* number — it is real for sophisticated leveraged investors, false as a description of the plain deposit.

**What it is NOT:** It is **not** an INR-equivalent yield (the depositor takes no rupee exposure); it is **not** a conflation with NRE rupee-deposit rates (NRE rates in 2003 were 4–5%, today's are rupee-denominated and irrelevant to a dollar IRR); it is **not** a different instrument. The cleanest one-sentence council verdict: ***"17% per year"* is a 7–10x-leveraged dollar IRR modelled by Jefferies, viable only for investors who borrow overseas against an SBLC; the underlying FCNR(B) deposit pays ~6–7% in USD, and the "3 years" is the lock-in/tenor, not a yield horizon.** `[HARD, H]`

A separate, even more inflated "17–27%" claim circulating on YouTube/Instagram simply repackages the Jefferies leverage math for retail audiences ([YouTube, 10 Jun 2026](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_pdXEeo_D3Q)) — same source, more hype. Treat any "guaranteed 17%" framing as **rumor-grade marketing**. `[DIR, H]`

So "diaspora money" is partly a narrative; the substance is a **subsidised dollar borrowing dressed as patriotic deposits**. ### 3.4 The heart of the prompt — diaspora deposits AS a UST-selling substitute

The mechanism, stated plainly: **a country defending its currency has two ways to supply dollars to the market.** Either (a) the central bank *sells its own reserves* (liquidating USTs) — the forced-seller channel; or (d) it *induces private non-residents to bring fresh dollars into the domestic banking system*, which the central bank then absorbs via the swap (taking the dollars onto its balance sheet, often reinvested in USTs) while the bank deploys rupees domestically. Route (d) **adds** to the dollar pool instead of **subtracting** from official reserves. As one Gulf-based advisor put it, the swap window is *"in substance, a sovereign subsidy on currency risk"* ([Khaleej Times, 10 Jun 2026](https://www.khaleejtimes.com/world/asia/india-offers-dollar-rupee-swap-to-boost-foreign-currency-deposits-as-reserves-fall)) `[DIR, H]`.

The 2013 episode is the cleanest proof of concept. SPJIMR's Narayan calls the FCNR(B) swap window **"essentially a covered intervention mechanism"**: reserves were mobilised during peak stress, deployed via intervention at elevated USD/INR, and *later rebuilt* after the rupee stabilised — generating realised transaction gains. The dollar inflows were **invested in US Treasuries yielding risk-free USD returns**, while RBI's rupee leg "involved monetary creation without financial cost," so the swap income accrued without a funding cost ([SPJIMR/Narayan](https://www.spjimr.org/newsroom/blog/the-2013-rbi-fcnrb-swap-window-review-takeaways/); [LinkedIn/Sankara Narayanan, 8 May 2026](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/sankara-narayanan-v-92a24a14_fcnrb-inr-fxreserves-activity-7458530637382193152-EGUo)) `[HARD/DIR, H]`. **Net effect on the UST market: the diaspora dollars partly ended up *as* UST purchases by RBI, and crucially they removed the need to *sell* USTs to defend the rupee.** The rupee recovered from 68.85 to ~62 by early 2014, and the current-account deficit compressed from 4.8% of GDP (FY13) to 0.9% (Q3 FY14) ([LinkedIn/Sankara Narayanan](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/sankara-narayanan-v-92a24a14_fcnrb-inr-fxreserves-activity-7458530637382193152-EGUo)) `[HARD, M]`.

### 3.5 Substitution math — $X FCNR ≈ $X less reserve drawdown / UST selling

The first-order arithmetic is one-for-one: **$1 of fresh FCNR(B) inflow that the central bank absorbs is ~$1 it does not have to draw from reserves to supply the same dollars to the market.** Quantifying against the live 2026 figures:

- RBI's FY26 spot dollar sales: **$53.13bn** ([Business Line, 27 May 2026](https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/data-stories/data-focus/rbis-forex-intervention-in-spot-and-forward-market-in-fy26-highest-in-a-decade/article71029062.ece)) `[HARD, H]`.
- Reserve drawdown Feb→May 2026: **~$46bn** ($728.49bn → $682.3bn) ([RBI/Upstox](https://upstox.com/news/market-news/latest-updates/forex-reserve-at-healthy-682-bn-adequate-to-provide-import-cover-for-11-months-rbi-guv/article-194879/)) `[HARD, H]`.
- Jefferies inflow estimate: **$50–70bn** ([Moneycontrol/Jefferies](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/personal-finance/how-nris-can-earn-17-27-returns-on-fcnr-b-deposits-through-leverage-jefferies-report-13944415.html)) `[HARD, H]`.

**Therefore: a successful $50–70bn FCNR(B) raise would more than fully offset the entire FY26 spot intervention ($53bn) and the Feb–May reserve drawdown ($46bn).** In UST terms, that is on the order of **$50–70bn of UST selling avoided** — meaningful even against India's modest UST footprint, and large relative to the drawdown it is meant to plug. A private banker framed the 2013 scale-equivalence usefully: $26bn then was ~2.5% of the deposit base and ~10% of reserves ([Business Standard, 9 Jun 2026](https://www.business-standard.com/amp/finance/news/rbi-hedge-subsidy-lifts-fcnr-b-appeal-but-narrower-spread-may-cap-inflows-126060900988_1.html)) `[HARD, H]`.

**Two important hedges on this estimate.** (1) *Round-tripping leakage:* if, as in 2013, much of the "inflow" is overseas-bank money lent to leveraged NRIs (the 7–10x SBLC trade), the *net* new dollars to the system are smaller than the gross deposit figure — the substitution ratio falls below 1:1. (2) *Maturity cliff:* the dollars must be returned in 3–5 years; in 2013 RBI faced a ~$20bn redemption outflow at maturity ([Indian Express, 3 Sep 2016](https://indianexpress.com/article/business/banking-and-finance/fcnr-bonds-were-least-bad-option-to-raise-dollars-raghuram-rajan-3011772/)) `[HARD, H]`. So diaspora deposits **time-shift** rather than permanently eliminate the reserve/UST pressure — they buy 3–5 years, betting (as Rajan did, successfully) that the currency stabilises in the interim.

## Section 6 — Layman Anchor Bank + Kit

**Anchor concepts:**
1. **Carry trade** — borrowing where money is cheap to invest where it pays more.
2. **FX intervention & reserves** — a country's "savings account" of foreign money it spends to prop up its currency.
3. **Diaspora bonds/deposits** — asking citizens living abroad to lend the homeland their dollars.
4. **Swap lines / FIMA** — an emergency dollar overdraft between central banks.
5. **"Why a country selling USTs raises US mortgage rates"** — fewer buyers for US government IOUs → US must offer higher interest → all US borrowing (including mortgages) gets pricier.

**Top-5 hardest — analogy + worked example + misconceptions:**

**1. Carry trade.** *Analogy:* Borrow on a 2% credit card to put money in a 6% savings account — pocket the 4%, until the card rate jumps or your savings bank wobbles. *Worked example:* Borrow ¥ at 0.5%, convert to rupees earning 6.5% → 6% gross carry; if the yen suddenly rises 10%, you lose 10% on repayment — wiping years of carry in days (Aug 2024). *Misconceptions:* (i) "It's safe because rates are fixed" — no, the *currency* move is the risk; (ii) "Only banks do it" — retail and crypto traders do too; (iii) "Small unwinds are harmless" — a 10–15% unwind crashed global vol in 72 hours in 2024.

**2. FX intervention & reserves.** *Analogy:* Your currency is a stock; the central bank is a buyer of last resort spending its dollar "war chest" to stop the price falling. *Worked example:* RBI sold $53bn in FY26 and reserves fell ~$46bn from the Feb peak — like a household burning $46k of savings to defend its house price. *Misconceptions:* (i) "Reserves are free money" — spending them is selling US Treasuries; (ii) "Intervention always works" — it slows, rarely reverses, a trend; (iii) "Big reserves = no problem" — $8bn can vanish in two days under attack.

**3. Diaspora bonds/deposits.** *Analogy:* Instead of dipping into your savings to pay a bill, you ask your kids working abroad to lend you their dollars — and you cover their currency risk as a sweetener. *Worked example:* India hopes to pull in $50–70bn of NRI dollars; that's $50–70bn it need *not* drain from reserves (i.e., need not sell in USTs) — but it must repay in 3–5 years. *Misconceptions:* (i) "It's all patriotic NRI money" — in 2013 much was leveraged bank money round-tripped; (ii) "17% guaranteed" — that's a *leveraged* IRR, the plain deposit pays ~6–7%; (iii) "It solves the problem" — it time-shifts it 3–5 years.

**4. Swap lines / FIMA.** *Analogy:* A pre-approved overdraft between central banks — borrow dollars against your Treasury collateral instead of fire-selling it. *Worked example:* A central bank needing $20bn can repo its USTs to the Fed overnight and get them back later, avoiding dumping $20bn of bonds into a falling market. *Misconceptions:* (i) "It's a bailout" — it's a collateralised loan, repaid with interest; (ii) "Everyone has one" — standing lines are only the 5 core central banks; FIMA is the broader repo backstop; (iii) "It prints unlimited dollars" — it's capped and collateralised.

**5. "Why a country selling USTs raises US mortgage rates."** *Analogy:* If the biggest regular bidder at an auction stops showing up, the seller must drop the price (raise the yield) to attract other buyers. *Worked example:* US mortgage rates are priced off the 10y Treasury; if China/Japan sell and the 10y rises from 4.5% to 5.5%, a 30-yr mortgage roughly tracks that +1% — adding ~$200/month on a $300k loan. *Misconceptions:* (i) "Foreigners can crash the US overnight" — the market is deep; selling is gradual and partly offset by the Fed/domestic buyers; (ii) "It only hurts the government" — it raises *all* US borrowing costs; (iii) "Mortgage rates follow the Fed funds rate" — they actually track the *long* Treasury yield, which foreigners influence.
