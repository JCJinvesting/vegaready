# R-09.2 US Rates Desk Supplement: Fiscal Calendar Specialist

**1. Treasury Buyback Mechanics & Schedule**
The Treasury's buyback program, transitioned to regular operations in mid-2024, operates in two distinct buckets: liquidity support and cash management. As of August 2025, Treasury doubled the frequency of long-end nominal coupon liquidity support operations ([Treasury Fiscal Data](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0212)), expanding the aggregate cap to $38bn per quarter (up from $30bn). These operations occur weekly and target off-the-run nominals and TIPS, aiming to bolster market liquidity and limit variations in auction sizes without materially altering the WAM of overall debt. The cash management bucket, recently increased to a $150bn annual cap, operates seasonally around major tax receipts (mid-April, mid-June, mid-September, mid-December) to smooth the TGA and reduce maturity peaks. The QRA publishes the tentative schedule, which details maximum purchase amounts per operation (e.g., $2bn for 10Y-20Y, $4bn for 1Mo-2Y). *Data Source:* [HARD] TreasuryDirect publishes the full schedule and operational results free of charge.

**2. 2025 Debt Ceiling Episode & 2026 Status**
The Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023 suspended the debt limit until January 1, 2025, reinstating it at $36.1 trillion on January 2. Treasury Secretary Yellen initiated extraordinary measures in mid-January 2025 to avoid default. Projections placed the "X-date"—the point where extraordinary measures exhaust and the Treasury cannot meet all obligations—between late July and September 2025. In late June 2025, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent announced an extension of extraordinary cash management strategies, delaying the X-date to July 24 ([Reuters](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/us-treasurys-bessent-extends-measures-005229386.html)), partially to pressure Congress before the August recess. *VERIFY 2026 Status:* The debt ceiling issue was resolved in late 2025 via a budget reconciliation/spending deal that pushed the next deadline past the 2026 midterm elections; currently, there is no active debt ceiling threat in mid-2026. *Data Source:* [HARD] CBO and CRFB. 

**3. 2026 Government Shutdowns & Market Impact**
The 2026 calendar featured two distinct government shutdowns stemming from congressional disputes over immigration enforcement. The first, a partial 4-day shutdown from January 31 to February 3, 2026, affected about half of all federal departments due to delays in a funding package ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_United_States_federal_government_shutdowns)). This brief gap delayed key economic data releases, including the January BLS jobs report and JOLTS data ([CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/02/02/economy/us-shutdown-january-jobs-report-bls)). The second, an unprecedented 76-day partial shutdown of the Department of Homeland Security (excluding ICE/Border Patrol funded via reconciliation), ran from February 14 to April 30, 2026. This targeted gap did not trigger broad data blackouts but caused significant localized disruptions before being resolved via legislation signed on April 30 ([Richmond Times](https://richmondtimes24.com/tech/update-on-government-shutdown/)).

**4. TGA Dynamics, Reserves, and the ON RRP Collapse**
The Treasury General Account (TGA) remains the critical variable in short-end plumbing. As of mid-June 2026, the TGA operating balance stands at approximately $828bn ([FRED WTREGEN](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WTREGEN) / [Daily Treasury Statement](https://api.fiscaldata.treasury.gov/)). The interplay between the TGA, QT, and the ON RRP facility dictates reserve scarcity. A TGA drawdown injects reserves into the banking system, while a TGA rebuild drains them. Notably, the ON RRP facility, which peaked over $2.5T, has essentially emptied out by early 2026 (domestic balances near zero). With the ON RRP buffer gone, fluctuations in the TGA—such as the massive April tax receipt rebuild—directly drain bank reserves, tightening liquidity conditions and pressuring overnight repo rates higher. *Data Source:* [DIR] FRED (WTREGEN) and the Daily Treasury Statement (DTS) provide free, daily TGA tracking.

**5. Rates Data-Wiring Audit & Proxies**
Acquiring institutional-grade rates data requires navigating a landscape of heavily guarded feeds. 
- *MOVE / Rate Vol:* ICE BofA MOVE index is paid, but Yahoo Finance provides a free live proxy (^MOVE).
- *Live SOFR Swap Spreads:* True live data requires Bloomberg/Tradeweb. [DIR] Eris Futures provides free end-of-day pricing and implied spot starting swaps via their FTP site, offering a robust, lagged alternative. 
- *Fed Funds Futures Odds:* [DIR] CME FedWatch Tool remains the premier free, live-ish proxy. 
- *Intraday Repo:* No free feed exists; intraday repo is strictly paid institutional data (e.g., RepoFunds Rate). The NY Fed's SOFR/BGCR/TGCR series provide the best, albeit daily-lagged, proxy.
- *When-Issued / Tails:* True WI trading data is paid; TreasuryDirect auction results provide free, post-facto tail calculations.

**6. Open Floor: The SOMA Reinvestment Mechanics & Debt Issuance WAM Management**
Looking ahead, the interaction between SOMA reinvestments and Treasury issuance strategy warrants close monitoring. As the Fed shifts from runoff to maintenance purchases (projected ~$360bn in T-bill demand for 2026, [TBAC Q1 2026](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TBACCharge1Q12026.pdf)), the Treasury must carefully balance its issuance across the curve. The buyback program's expansion already signals a proactive approach to managing the Weighted Average Maturity (WAM) of outstanding debt, essentially allowing Treasury to issue bills to buy back long-end coupons. If structural reserve scarcity forces the Fed into larger-than-expected permanent repo operations or expanded SOMA purchases, it will radically alter the supply-demand dynamics for bills and off-the-run coupons, potentially compressing term premium further despite the hawkish rate environment.
