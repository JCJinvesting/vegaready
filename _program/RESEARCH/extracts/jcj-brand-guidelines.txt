Definition: Pull Quote Block
A pull quote block is a visually isolated design element used to emphasize a key message, quote, or theme within a document. It is intended to break visual pacing, create resonance, and frame a transition — especially in materials targeted at institutional allocators.

In the JCJ system, pull quote blocks are not decorative. They serve a structural purpose by briefly pausing the narrative to highlight design ethos, investment philosophy, or narrative inflection points.

Best practices for allocator-facing pull quote blocks include:

• Centered layout with no surrounding clutter
• Font: Playfair Display Italic or Georgia Italic
• Font size: 18–22pt
• Background fill: Fog Purple, Gold, or Bone
• No inline styling or multi-color words
• No quotation marks unless contextually needed
• Serves as a narrative transition or emphasis — not decoration

Method & sources (why this looks the way it does)
Emotional function → layout permission. I started from each tone’s emotional job (e.g., “reassure”, “quant proof”, “legal clarity”) and only promoted to background-ready if that role benefits from owning the entire canvas (e.g., Navy, Payne’s Gray, Off White) instead of just supporting content. This aligns with the Tone Transition Map logic used to keep flows psychologically smooth. 
ADA/contrast & platform reality. Colors flagged as medium/low contrast or “panel-only” in the annotated dictionary were kept conditional or disallowed as full backgrounds (e.g., ETF Yellow, Gold, Fog Purple, Dusty Plum). 
Regional norms & allocator psychology. Warm urgency (Burnt Orange/Dijon) was restricted to closing slides or thin accents, especially for GCC/India where understated confidence tests better.
Quick sanity checks (practical rules of thumb)
If the copy is long, prefer ◽ Off White / 🟫 Bone / Payne’s Gray / Navy.
If the content is legal/compliance, use Payne’s Gray or a neutral, never a warm tone. 
If the goal is empathy/repair, keep 🟪 Dusty Plum / 🔴 Coral to one or two slides max, then bridge out via a neutral. 
CTAs live on Teal/Teal-Blue panels (never full-slide Teal).

Purpose of Gradient Usage
Gradients in the JCJ system are used to frame transitions, set tone, and move allocators emotionally without adding textual complexity. A gradient is not a background; it’s a behavioral cue. The following table and guide describe how to construct, position, and apply gradient overlays across slide layouts.
Gradient Overlay Reference Table

Gradient Layout Framework & Behavioral Zones
Gradients guide allocator attention by simulating motion, insight emergence, or thematic handoff. 
Every JCJ gradient slide contains three zones:
**Entry Zone (0–30%)** – For breadcrumbs, soft context, or supporting labels.
**Transition Zone (30–70%)** – For quotes or emotional pivots. Use sparingly and avoid complex text.
**Resolution Zone (70–100%)** – For CTAs, section titles, and structural messages.
Avoid placing important or dense content across the color blend. Content should always align with the emotional meaning of the gradient's endpoint.

Using Gradients When Space is Limited
In high-density decks or limited slide counts, avoid full-page gradient backgrounds. Instead, apply gradients in header strips, footer CTA bands, or behind white content cards. This conserves space while preserving emotional framing.
Gradient Design Checklist
✓ 2 color stops used (no more)
✓ Direction = horizontal unless footer/banner use
✓ Content placed in appropriate emotional zones
✓ Overlay text tested in both black and white
✓ No dense text across color transitions
✓ Gradient matches slide tone (signal → structure, emotion → action)
Gradient Design Framework –Walkthrough	
A gradient is not a visual flourish. In the JCJ system, it’s a behavioral cue—a transition of emotional tone, guiding allocators from confusion to clarity, hesitation to structure, or signal to decision.
You use it to:
- Frame a quote
- Introduce a section
- Bridge two contrasting themes
- Draw allocator attention without extra text
Direction of Gradient Use								
• Left → Right (default): Best for slide flow, narrative motion.
• Top → Bottom: Used in footers, section banners.
• Diagonal/Radial: Avoid. Too decorative.
Gradient Zone Strategy									
- Entry Zone (0–30%): Breadcrumbs, soft intro
- Transition Zone (30–70%): Quotes, insights, pivots
- Resolution Zone (70–100%): CTA, section title, core message
Content must align with emotional meaning of each zone.

Overlay Text & Contrast Rules								
- White text for dark tones (Cobalt, Charcoal)
- Charcoal text for light tones (Mint, Gold)
- Test overlays in both black and white
- Never place small/dense text across blend zones
Final Checklist for Gradient Use
