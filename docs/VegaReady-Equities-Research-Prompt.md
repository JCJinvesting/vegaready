ROLE & CONTEXT
You are a senior markets-research and information-architecture consultant. I'm designing the public EQUITIES DESK page of an institutional, dual-audience cross-asset market-intelligence website (a research-grade, public version of a Koyfin/Bloomberg markets page). The site reads markets as a "regime" and overlays live event-catalysts (a geopolitical conflict, an OPEC+ decision, a Fed pivot, a tariff regime) on top of a DURABLE, catalyst-neutral market framework — so the page must make sense whether or not any catalyst is active. Audience is dual: (1) institutional analysts/PMs who want density, and (2) sophisticated non-specialist investors who need plain language. Governing principle: honesty over false precision — every claim is source-tiered and scenario-aware, correlation ≠ causation, and competing drivers plus a named residual are always shown.

I need deep, structured, SOURCED research to design and fill this page. Answer ALL parts below. Use tables for the per-topic and per-sector parts. Cite a reputable source (prefer institutional/primary) for every factual claim and every data-source recommendation. End with a numbered source list.

PART A — PAGE ARCHITECTURE
A1. The ideal top-to-bottom section hierarchy for an institutional equities markets desk that serves both audiences — and why each section earns its place.
A2. What the best products (Bloomberg, Koyfin, MacroMicro, sell-side equity-strategy weeklies, FT/Economist markets pages) put on an equities overview — what to borrow and what to avoid.
A3. How to handle progressive disclosure (30-second read → 3-minute skim → deep analyst layer) and a plain-language/analyst toggle on the SAME content.

PART B — PER-TOPIC CONTENT (the durable, catalyst-neutral toolkit)
For EACH topic below give: what to include, the specific indicators an analyst tracks, how best to present it (table/chart/gauge), must-have vs nice-to-have, its leading-indicator value, and the best FREE/PUBLIC data sources with update cadence:
- Market regime (risk-on/off, leadership, volatility state)
- Market breadth
- Valuation & the return engine (forward P/E; equity risk premium; the dividend-yield + earnings-growth ± multiple-expansion return decomposition)
- Equity factors (value, growth, quality, momentum, low-vol, size) — definitions, what drives each, when each leads, how to show dispersion/valuation spreads
- Sector rotation & the business cycle
- Earnings & revisions (growth, revision breadth, margins)
- Positioning & sentiment (Fear & Greed, NAAIM, AAII, put/call, CFTC COT, fund flows)
- Cross-asset linkages (how equities connect to rates, credit, the dollar, commodities, crypto)

PART C — PER-SECTOR CONTENT (the 11 GICS sectors)
For EACH of the 11 GICS sectors (Energy, Materials, Industrials, Consumer Discretionary, Financials, Information Technology, Communication Services, Consumer Staples, Health Care, Utilities, Real Estate) give a compact desk profile:
- What structurally drives the sector (1–2 lines)
- Sensitivity to: interest rates, the business cycle (cyclical/defensive), oil/energy, the US dollar, inflation
- The 3–5 key metrics/KPIs to track
- The leading indicators that move ahead of it
- Common ETF/index proxies
- Classic risks and the conditions under which it leads vs lags
Present as a per-sector table or profile.

PART D — CATALYST OVERLAYS
How to present a specific live event/catalyst's effect on equities WITHOUT the event dominating the durable framework: best practices for "event overlay" presentation, scenario-conditional winners/losers, supporting multiple simultaneous catalysts, and avoiding recency bias.

PART E — EPISTEMIC & DATA DISCIPLINE
E1. How to label confidence and evidence (source tiers; data confidence vs attribution confidence; observed vs interpretation; value types such as baseline/current/nowcast/forecast/scenario).
E2. Reliable free/public data sources for every indicator above (FRED, exchange/index data, CBOE, AAII, NAAIM, CFTC, SEC EDGAR, etc.), with update cadence and any redistribution/attribution caveats.

PART F — EXTENSIBILITY
Briefly: the reusable "desk template" pattern so this framework extends to rates, credit, FX, commodities, and crypto desks.

OUTPUT: clear headers; tables for Parts B and C; a numbered, reputable source list at the end.
