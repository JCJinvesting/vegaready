Hormuz at Day 247: the window the market won’t price.

Premia into the June 15 reset — what the desk is watching.

01 · The window

Energy Desk, D+247 —

1Desk estimate D+247; methodology on the dashboard.

Two hundred and forty-seven days into the closure scare, the Strait of Hormuz is doing what it always does in the eighth month of a crisis: it is getting boring. Transits are down 11 per cent from the pre-war norm, tankers run the corridor at night in loose convoys, and the desk’s reconciled AIS count shows 47 laden VLCCs westbound this week1 — a number that would have read as catastrophe in October and now reads as Tuesday.

21984–2024 incident set, 14 events. Confidence: high.

Boredom is the trade. The June 15 war-risk reset is nine days out, and the underwriting committees in London will reprice every hull that touches the Gulf. Our work says closure risk is under-priced by roughly a third: the market is paying ceasefire prices for a strait that is still, by any honest reading of the incident set, contested.2

Watch

Insurance reset on the 15th — premiums move first. In every prior repricing cycle, freight has followed the quote within four sessions.

Premia tell it plainly. War-risk cover for a Hormuz transit was quoted at ⅛ per cent of hull value before the war; it touched ½ per cent at the October peak; it sits near ¼ today. The forward market treats that retracement as a destination. The desk treats it as a pause — a window between the calm of May and a committee decision that has not yet been made.

02 · The cascade
3AIS reconciliation 06:40 GST.
— revised 06:40, sourcing upgraded to three wires.

When a chokepoint reprices, it does so in a fixed order. The desk has watched the sequence run fourteen times since the Tanker War and it has never varied: insurance first, freight second, equities last.3 The lags between the three are not noise — they are the structure of the trade. Premiums reset on committee schedules, discrete and dated. Freight resets voyage by voyage as owners re-fix. Equities reset when the second-order arithmetic finally arrives in a strategy note, usually a week late and all at once.

Meanwhile the corridor itself keeps a meter running.

Demurrage, deviation fuel, the convoy wait at the western anchorages: that is what a laden VLCC bleeds for every day Hormuz runs slow, and it is the bill the cascade exists to allocate. The three figures that follow show the sequence as the desk charts it.

Sequence · 1 of 3

Premia move first. The war-risk quote is a committee number — discrete, dated, published. When the June 15 reset lands it will not drift; it will gap. Across the fourteen-event incident set the first post-reset quote moved an average of 31 per cent, and in eleven of fourteen cases the direction was set within a single session. The quote is the cascade’s clock.

Sequence · 2 of 3

Freight follows within four sessions. Owners refuse the corridor until the new premium clears their charter math, then relist at rates that recover it. TD3C has lagged the quote by three to five sessions in every cycle since 2019, and the lag is structural: fixtures are negotiated, premiums are decreed.

Sequence · 3 of 3

Equities reprice last, and largest. Tanker owners, GCC insurers, the east-of-Suez refining complex — none of them move until the freight print confirms what the premium already said. That confirmation lag is where the position lives. By the time the strategy notes arrive, the desk wants to be done buying.

The window is real. The price isn’t yet.
Desk maxim
03 · The Position

The position follows from the sequence, not from a view on the war. Ahead of the 15th the desk is long the lag: long the instruments that reprice late, funded by the ones that reprice early. The early leg is already working — prompt Brent added +38bp this week on nothing more than reset chatter, and the war-risk forward market has begun quoting June transit cover above the spot ¼ per cent. The late legs have barely stirred. Tanker equities trade where they did in April. The GCC insurers are priced for a ceasefire the incident log does not show.

We size it modestly, because the argument is asymmetry rather than probability. If the reset comes in soft — committees anchoring to the calm of May — the early hedge bleeds a little and the late legs go nowhere; the trade costs carry, nothing more. If the reset comes in hard, the cascade runs its fourteen-for-fourteen sequence and the late legs reprice by multiples of the early ones. The desk does not need to be right about Hormuz. It needs the market to stop being wrong in a particular order, and the order is the most reliable thing in this file.

What would change our mind is legible in advance. A formal extension of the naval corridor agreement before the 12th would justify the soft quote, and we would cut the late legs the same day — no averaging, no thesis-creep. A confirmed strike on tanker traffic — not a drone overflight, a hull — would collapse the lag entirely as everything repriced at once, and the trade would make its money badly, all in the hedge. Between those poles the position simply waits, which is what it was built to do.

Nine days, then. The committees meet, the quote gaps or it doesn’t, and the cascade either runs or goes back to sleep until the next reset. Until then the desk’s view is unchanged from the top of this note: the window is real, the meter is running at $4.2 million a day, and the market is still reading May’s quote in June’s strait.ν
